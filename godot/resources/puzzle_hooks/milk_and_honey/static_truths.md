# STATIC TRUTHS
## Lena Vargas's zine · Smolvud, OR · vol7 · started August 2025

A photocopied zine Lena starts the morning she wakes with charcoal
on her thigh in the shape of three letters: **S U N.** The
charcoal is not from her apartment. She has not used charcoal. Her
sketchbook is unopened. Her pencils are in the drawer where she
left them. The charcoal also will not, eventually, wash off — for
three days. By the time it does, she has decided to start a zine
and call it *Static Truths,* and she has drawn an octopus on the
back cover of the first issue without quite knowing why.

The zine is, by its own opening note, *"a record of small things
the coast does not, in its own voice, get to record. — l.v."*

## the first issue (August 2025)

Four photocopied pages, half-letter, single-fold. Printed on the
Salty Tome's back-room copier with Petra's permission. Petra
charged Lena two dollars for the first run of fifteen copies and
then, on Lena's second visit a week later, refused to charge her
again — saying only, by Lena's later report to Kai, *"the zine is
the zine, kid. the copier is the copier. I'm not in the way."*

### page 1 — cover

The masthead in Lena's hand-lettered block print, slightly
unsteady because Lena is a painter who has been, for almost a year,
not painting:

> **STATIC TRUTHS**
> *vol. 1, no. 1 · summer · smolvud, oregon · by l.v.*

Below the masthead, a single hand-drawn image: a coffee cup on a
saucer, the saucer set on a small porch table, the porch railing
visible in the lower third, the railing's pickets rendered
deliberately as the bars of a small cage that the eye reads as a
porch first and as a cage second. The drawing is in pencil.

### page 2 — editorial

> *I woke this morning with charcoal on my thigh. Three letters.
> S U N. I have not used charcoal. The charcoal is not in my
> apartment. The charcoal is from a wall somewhere — I do not
> know where — and the charcoal also will not, by the time of
> this writing, wash off. It has been on me for three days.*
>
> *I have decided to start a zine. The zine is going to be about
> the small things the coast does not, in its own voice, get to
> record. The thing that wrote SUN on my thigh is one of those
> small things. The man who skipped a heavy stone across the bay
> last Tuesday is another. The radio Finn carries that holds
> together on electrical tape, and that has, since June, been
> picking up a signal that is not on any allocated band, is
> another. The crow that sits on the railing of the Daily Grind
> every morning when I open and that is, by my growing
> certainty, the same crow Mrs. Gable says she has been seeing
> at the lighthouse every morning for nine years, is another.*
>
> *I do not know what these things are. I have been waiting,
> these eleven months in Smolvud, for the things to add up. They
> have not yet added up. They have, this week, started to ask me
> to do something. I am starting the zine instead.*
>
> *I do not know what the zine will become. I am going to let it
> be what it is. — l.v.*

### page 3 — the first record

A small piece titled **THE MAN WHO SKIPPED THE STONE.** Six
paragraphs. Lena describes a man she observed at the jetty last
Tuesday at five forty-three in the morning, alone, in a cork hat,
smoking a hand-rolled cigarette, who picked up a stone that was —
by her painter's eye — too heavy and too large to skip and who
skipped it anyway, seven times, across the bay's still surface.
The man, after the seventh skip, looked across the bay where the
stone had finally sunk and said, audibly, in a voice like rocks in
a tumble dryer: *"right on schedule."* The man did not, after
that, turn or acknowledge her. The man continued to fish, or to
not fish — Lena could not tell which.

She watched him for forty minutes.

She has not, since, seen him at the jetty. She has been back
every morning.

The piece ends:

> *I do not know who the man is. I do not know what was on
> schedule. I do know that the stone he skipped was a stone I
> have been looking at on the beach behind the cannery since I
> moved here. The stone was the size of a small loaf of bread.
> The stone is no longer where it was. I have looked. — l.v.*

### page 4 — back cover

A single ink drawing of an octopus. The octopus has seven arms in
the drawing. Lena, on first inspection of the finished print,
counted six. On second inspection, seven. She has not, since,
drawn an octopus on purpose. She is not, by her painter's training,
a person who would forget how many arms an octopus has. The
octopus is, by her quiet acknowledgment to herself, doing its own
thing.

Below the octopus, in small letters: *"contact: the small box at
the daily grind by the front register. mark it for l.v. petra at
the salty tome also knows where to find me. — l.v."*

## the second issue (drafted but not yet printed, late August)

Eight pages. Lena has expanded the form. She is working in the
small specific late-night hours she has been working in since the
charcoal arrived. The second issue's contents, in current draft:

- **Editorial: The Charcoal Is Still There** — Lena's three-day
  wait for the charcoal to wash off, the small careful realization
  she was having while waiting, the decision to keep recording.
  Three paragraphs.
- **The Crow on the Railing** — a profile of the crow at the
  Daily Grind. Mrs. Gable has, in late August, mentioned to Lena
  that she has seen the crow at the lighthouse for nine years.
  Lena has, since, asked Finn about the crow Finn has been
  feeding bottle caps to at the trailhead. Finn confirmed: same
  crow. Lena has not, yet, written the *same crow* sentence into
  the zine. She is, by editorial restraint, holding it for
  Issue #3.
- **A Map of Smolvud at 5:43 AM** — a small hand-drawn map of
  the town as observed at the specific hour Lena opens the
  Daily Grind. The map labels the people Lena sees every
  morning: the four regulars at the Daily Grind, the two
  walkers on Cypress, the night-shift nurse from the clinic
  who passes the front window at five fifty-one, the man with
  the dog who passes at five fifty-three. The map does not
  include the man at the jetty. The map is, by the editorial
  decision Lena made in mid-August, *"for the people who are
  reliably here. not for the people who are visiting from
  somewhere I do not yet know about. — l.v."*
- **John Frank's *The Frequency Beneath*** — a single-page
  review. Lena has not read the book. Lena's friend Tem has,
  by careful conversation, been reading the book aloud to Lena
  in small Sunday-afternoon installments. The review is
  therefore Tem's, in Lena's typing. The review's closing
  sentence: *"the book is doing what it is doing. you should
  let it. — t., via l.v."*
- **An Unfinished Letter to a Stranger** — addressed to
  *"the editor of HARMONY CREEK GLITCH REPORT — c/o Cosmic
  Comics, Linden Strip Mall, Harmony Creek Estates, TX"* —
  Lena has, by careful library work, identified the existence
  of the Glitch Report by tracing back the charcoal she woke
  with through a small forensic chain involving the
  composition of Texas wall-charcoal manufacturers (there is
  exactly one she can identify, in Galveston, since 1972) and
  a list of Cosmic Comics' historical zine inventory she found
  on a defunct GeoCities page archived at archive.org. The
  letter is incomplete. The letter asks the editor — Lena does
  not yet know the editor is named Maya Daigle — whether the
  editor would be willing to correspond. The letter has not
  been mailed. The letter will, by Lena's quiet acknowledgment
  in October, be mailed.
- **The Static** — a small piece on the carrier signal Finn's
  radio has been picking up. Lena has heard the signal once.
  Lena described, in the piece, the signal's sub-bass envelope
  at approximately 0.3 Hz over a primary carrier at
  approximately 47 Hz. Lena does not know — has no way to
  know yet — that the signal is the same signal Carla Vega
  recorded in vol6 and that the Glitch Report's 1988
  collective documented at the same frequency. The deck
  permits Lena to figure this out at her own pace. The deck
  does not require her to.
- **A Continued Inventory of Things That Cannot Be Explained** —
  a running back-page feature Lena will, starting with Issue
  #2, maintain across all subsequent issues. The list begins
  with the SUN charcoal, the seven-armed octopus, the
  too-heavy stone, the crow, the signal, and one item she has
  not, in Issue #2, fully described: *"the small dish of
  blackberries that appeared on my kitchen counter on the
  Monday after the SUN morning. I had not been to the market.
  My door was locked. The blackberries were fresh."*

## the third issue (in proposal stage)

Lena has not yet drafted Issue #3. She has, by mid-September,
identified the through-line she wants the zine to follow. The
through-line is what she has begun calling, in private notes to
herself, *the substrate.* By the time the third issue is printed
— late autumn — Lena will have, separately, started a small
correspondence with Maya Daigle through the postal route that the
Glitch Report's editor in 1988 had used; she will have heard from
Tem the longer version of the story Tem has been carrying for
years; she will have walked, with Finn, to the abandoned Alsea
Bay cannery once at dusk and once at dawn; she will have, with
Kai, played the SCUMM machine at Pizza Pirate exactly twice and
will, both times, have stopped at the same point in the same
session and unplugged the cabinet from the wall.

The third issue's working title is *VOLUME 1, NUMBER 3: THE
SUBSTRATE.* The deck reserves its contents for vol7's
mid-act.

## meta-game integration

Static Truths in Act III is structurally what the Glitch Report
extension is to Act II: the documentary spine of the volume,
written by the character who is, in canon, the volume's "load-
bearing tissue" for the Pacific arc — Lena. Reading the issues
in order:

- **Issue #1** → surfaces the SUN charcoal, the man at the
  jetty, the seven-armed octopus, the crow at the railing as
  recognized recurring deck-level motifs the player can cross-
  reference back into vol5 (Joanna LeMoine's crow, the Frog at
  the jetty) and forward into vol7's later chapters
- **Issue #2** (when drafted) → unlocks the Glitch Report cross-
  reference; the player can, by surfacing Lena's unfinished
  letter, send it on her behalf; if sent, Maya receives it
  approximately three weeks later via Cosmic Comics' mailbox at
  the end of the strip, and the cross-volume tissue tightens
  by one substantial degree
- **Issue #3** (reserved) → unlocks *the substrate* as a deck-
  level concept; the meta-game permits the player, at this
  point, to read the substrate's documentation as a single
  unified document across all three acts (the Glitch Report
  Volume 1, the SUBURBAN BLIGHT tape liner notes, the John
  Frank notebook entries, Elicia's *Whispers from the Liminal*
  episode breakdowns, and Static Truths Issues 1 through 3)
- **The running back-page inventory** → permits the player to
  add observations from anywhere in the deck to Lena's list.
  Each addition, when Lena reads it, generates a small
  acknowledgment in the next issue: *"a reader of this zine
  has, by some method I have not asked about, contributed the
  following item to the inventory. I am grateful. — l.v."*

Static Truths is, by the deck's deeper structure, the volume's
documentary closure. The Glitch Report opens the substrate's
documentation in 1988; Maya extends it in 2025; Lena receives
the extension in Smolvud later that fall; Issue #3 closes the
loop. The wheel keeps turning. The wheel is the same wheel.
There is only the one wheel.

— *the codex, on the substrate.*
