# RICK · accumulation
## Cosmic Comics · Linden Strip Mall · between Sally Beauty and Great Clips

Sixty-two. Grey-shot beard. A denim jacket over a graphic tee — different tee every day, all of them tour shirts from regional bands that no longer exist. Reading glasses on a chain. Lives in a small apartment over the comic shop. Has owned Cosmic Comics since 1991. Has been answering the phone-call-from-Beaumont since 2002. Has not, in twenty-four years, told anyone about the call, including his husband **DEAN** (deceased 2018, peacefully, in their bed, of a heart issue Rick had been monitoring), including Maya Daigle, including the Cosmic Comics regulars.

He has, however, been waiting. For Maya to ask. For Sam to ask. For Diego, when Diego was ready. Diego, by his own quiet arrangement with Rick across the past three years, was on the verge of being ready.

This is what Rick has been holding.

## I. THE BACK-ROOM ZINE ARCHIVE

A six-foot row of shelving in the back room of Cosmic Comics, behind the curtain, behind the practice space the Gallatin Band uses on Tuesday and Thursday nights. The shelving holds forty years of regional zines, sorted by year and labeled in Rick's hand. The archive contains, by Rick's careful count, approximately eleven thousand individual issues.

The archive is, by reasonable scholarly assessment, the most complete documentation of southern Louisiana / east Texas / Gulf Coast indie publishing from 1984 to the present. Rick has not advertised it. Rick has let, by quiet invitation, three researchers access it across the years. He has let Maya use it freely since she was twelve.

### selected holdings

The archive is too large to inventory in full. The meta-game's L4 layer permits the player to browse specific decades and topics. Highlighted items:

- **1984–1991 — the queer punk decade.** Zines from Houston (*Static Edge,* *Reactor Pulp,* *Tinfoil Heart*), New Orleans (*Bayou Punk,* *Coffee and Cigarettes Quarterly,* *The Evergreen Voice* — the room's own newsletter, hand-bound), and Lake Charles (*Cypress Saturated*). Rick was, in this decade, a contributor and occasional co-editor of *The Evergreen Voice.* His handle in the masthead was **R.B.** (his middle initial; his surname is not in the public masthead).

- **1986 — the Beaumont 9.** Nine zines published across one summer in Beaumont, Texas, by an informal collective of nine young people in their late teens and early twenties. The collective broke up at the end of that summer for reasons Rick has, in private, documented in his hardcover ledger (Item IV in this accumulation). The nine zines, when laid in chronological order on Rick's worktable, are the chapter's primary visual record of the people who would, sixteen years later in 2002, encounter the situation in Beaumont that defines Rick's adult life.

  The nine titles:

  1. **THE LEVEE INDEX** (Jacob)
  2. **WAGNER'S WAGON** (Wagner)
  3. **FAUST'S FOLIO** (Faust)
  4. **R.B.'S BACK ROOM** (Rick)
  5. **CASSANDRA QUARTERLY** (Cassie — who would become Casserole of the Evergreen Company; see S01E16)
  6. **MOTH FOLD** (Little Moth — same; see S01E16)
  7. **PORTHOLE PAGES** (Porthole — same)
  8. **REVELATIA REVUE** (Revelatia — same)
  9. **OPHIDIA OPUS** (Ophidia — same)

  The nine zinesters were friends. They were not, in any official sense, a group. They were friends. They moved in adjacent circles. The Triumvirate from vol1 (Jacob, Wagner, Faust) and the Evergreen Company from S01E16 of The Pomegranate Hour were, in their twenties, in correspondence and occasional collaboration with Rick in his early twenties through this small Beaumont scene.

  Rick has, in his archive, every issue of every nine zines, ranging from issue #1 to issue #14 across the summer. The collective's last collaborative issue was **THE NINE-WAY POTLATCH OF AUGUST,** a single A4 booklet with one contribution from each, photocopied in a print run of nine, distributed by Rick to each member at a small farewell potluck at a beach house on Bolivar Peninsula on August 24, 1986.

  The booklet is, by Rick's quiet acknowledgment, the chapter's emotional center. The booklet has been on Rick's worktable, in a small acid-free sleeve, every day for thirty-nine years.

- **1991–2003 — the Cosmic Comics era.** Zines Rick has stocked in the store from his opening. The era includes early issues of **GENERAL STORE** (Maya's grandfather worked in his funeral parlor in Graustark and had a small minicomic he made on the side; the early *GENERAL STORE* zines are his, predating Maya's project by thirty years and possibly being its source-material), **SLUDGE PASTORAL fanzines** (the band has had a small fan-zine scene since 1996), and **WUFR Transmission Logs** (a fanzine devoted to transcribing the medium's broadcasts; Rick has every issue from 1996 to present; the zine's editor is anonymous; the meta-game permits the player to identify the editor as Elicia Duchane through cross-reference with her own broadcasts).

- **2003–present — the dispersal era.** After the Beaumont 2002 situation, the regional zine scene fragmented. Rick has continued to archive. Notable holdings: the Pomegranate Hour fan-zines (yes, several exist, by viewers who began to make their own zines about the show), **WHAT THE COMMUNITY VOICE DID NOT PRINT** (the Vega/Montoya zine — all four issues), and **NEWS FROM HARMONY CREEK** (Maya's series, all twenty-three issues, plus the in-progress #23).

The strip's final panel: R.B. saying, to the door, *"the door is now locked. the door is fine. I will not, for a while, be trying it again."*

## II. RICK'S OWN COMIX — *"THE BEAUMONT BARGAINER"*

A series Rick drew and self-published from 1983 to 2002. Forty-seven issues. The series is autobiographical/observational underground comix in the R. Crumb / Aline Kominsky-Crumb register — Rick's own life, his Beaumont scene, his early Houston years, his Cosmic Comics opening, his marriage to Dean. Each issue is twenty-four pages, photocopied at the Beaumont Kinko's (1983-1991) and the Houma Kinko's (1991-2002), saddle-stitched, $1 cover price.

The series stopped in 2002.

The series stopped on issue #47.

The series stopped because Rick, in the summer of 2002, encountered a situation in Beaumont that broke his ability to render his own life as comix. He has not, in twenty-three years, drawn another page. He has not, in twenty-three years, opened the unfinished pencil layouts for issue #48 that sit in a drawer in his apartment upstairs.

### selected issues

**Issue #1 — 1983 — "RICK BUYS A LANDLINE."**

Rick at twenty installs his first apartment phone. The phone is a yellow rotary on a small wooden table. He sits on the floor and looks at it for ninety minutes. He does not know who to call.

The issue's final panel: Rick on the floor, the phone in his lap, his finger in the dial-hole, mid-thought-bubble: *"I will figure out who to call. it is fine. there is no rush. the phone will work whether I dial or not."*

**Issue #14 — 1985 — "RICK MEETS DEAN."**

Rick at twenty-two, working the door at a small Houston punk venue. Dean — slightly older, in glasses, with a denim jacket — comes to the door and asks Rick if Rick is single. Rick, surprised, says yes. Dean asks if Rick would like to get coffee after Rick's shift ends. Rick says yes. The two of them sit in a 24-hour diner from 2 AM to 5 AM. The issue ends on Rick walking home as the sun comes up, his hands in his pockets, smiling at no one.

The issue's title — *"RICK MEETS DEAN"* — is the only one in the series that uses Dean's name. Subsequent appearances of Dean in the comix refer to him as **THE DRAFTSMAN** (Dean was an architectural draftsman by trade).

**Issue #22 — 1986 — "THE SUMMER OF THE NINE."**

A long-form issue, forty-eight pages instead of twenty-four. Rick's documentation of the Beaumont 9 summer. The issue is, by Rick's quiet account, the most carefully drawn thing he ever made. The issue depicts all nine members of the collective, their gatherings at a borrowed house on the bayou, their nightly photocopying runs, their long conversations about whether the chapter the collective was building was a chapter or a friendship. The issue does not, in any panel, depict the end of the summer. The end of the summer is implied by the issue's final two pages, which are blank but for a single small panel in the lower right of the last page: nine kitchen chairs around a long table on a porch, eight of them empty, one of them holding a folded paper crane.

The crane was made by Cassie (later Casserole) on the night the collective dispersed. The crane has been, since 1986, in Casserole's possession. Rick has not seen it since the night. He has, by long-distance correspondence with Casserole, confirmed that the crane is still in her possession. She has placed it on the green-room dressing-table of the Evergreen Room at the lower French Quarter venue. The crane has, by Rick's last verification in 2019, been there for thirty-three years.

**Issue #47 — 2002 — "RICK DOES NOT WRITE ABOUT BEAUMONT."**

The series' final issue. Twenty-four pages. The first twenty-three pages are, by careful reading, a comic about anything except Beaumont. Rick documents his summer (slow at the shop, Dean's vacation to a cousin's house in Tampa, a single morning at a coffee shop in Houma where Rick sat by the window and watched a small girl draw with crayons on the floor while her grandmother bought coffee — the small girl is, on close inspection of Rick's drawing, Maya at age four, three years before her grandfather's death; Rick has, on first publication, not registered this; he registered it in 2019 when he showed the issue to Maya). The twenty-three pages are gentle. They are quiet. They are, by the publication's standards, slightly under-charged.

The final page — page 24 — is a single panel. The panel shows Rick at his back-counter at Cosmic Comics, in 2002. The phone is on the counter beside him. The phone is ringing. Rick, in the panel, is looking at the phone. The caption box at the bottom of the panel reads, in Rick's hand: *"I am going to answer this. — R.B."*

The issue's last page has no further panels. The phone is, by every reader's correct guess, the Beaumont 2002 phone. The phone, when Rick picked it up, told him what it has told him, on a rolling basis, every two-to-seven weeks for the past twenty-three years.

After Rick answered, he stopped drawing. He has not started again.

### gallery unlocks gated by Rick's comix

- **Read issues #1 through #47 in order** → unlocks Rick's long arc as a person. The meta-game's L3 personnel-graph permits the player to surface Rick's history, his relationships, his quiet integrity. The accumulation file's deeper layers open.
- **Open the unfinished pencil layouts for issue #48** in the upstairs apartment drawer (gated on Rick's permission, which is granted after Maya asks him about her father's things in the back room — see Item III) → unlocks issue #48. The issue is mostly blank. The pages show Rick at his shop in present-day Harmony Creek (2025), the phone on the counter, the phone ringing, and a single different final panel: Maya at the counter, asking Rick a question we do not, in the meta-game, hear. Rick is answering. Rick's expression is the expression of a man who has, for twenty-three years, been waiting to be asked. The panel is captioned: *"#48 — RICK ANSWERS THE QUESTION. — to be completed when the question is asked. — R.B."* The pencil layouts have been on Rick's worktable since 2019, when Maya first walked into the shop alone.
- **Cross-reference the small girl at the coffee shop in issue #47 with Maya's grandfather's funeral parlor records** → confirms the small girl is Maya at age four. The meta-game permits Rick's quiet realization that he has been, in some sense, in correspondence with Maya since she was four, by being in the same coffee shop, by drawing her without knowing.

## III. THE BACK-ROOM BOX — *"FOR MAYA · WHEN SHE ASKS"*

A wooden box, approximately 18 x 12 x 6 inches, on the bottom shelf of the back-room storage rack at Cosmic Comics, behind a stack of unsold *GENERAL STORE* zines from Maya's grandfather. The box is labeled in Rick's hand, on a piece of masking tape on the lid: **"FOR M.D. · FROM HER FATHER · DO NOT OPEN UNTIL SHE ASKS."**

The box has been in the back room since 2018. **PHILIP DAIGLE** — Maya's father — delivered the box to Rick personally on a Sunday afternoon while Maya was at her grandmother's. Philip's instructions to Rick were brief: *"Rick. Maya may ask, at some point. When she asks, give it to her. If she doesn't ask, leave it where it is. Don't tell her about it. Tell her when she asks. — P."*

Rick agreed. Rick has not told Maya. Maya has, at the time of vol6 ch1, not yet asked.

She will. Soon.

### contents (when the box is opened)

- a single sealed envelope, addressed to Maya in Philip's hand: *"Maya — read this first."* The envelope contains a letter approximately four pages long, in Philip's hand, dated 2018. The letter explains, in careful direct language: (a) why Philip cannot be present in Maya's life; (b) what he was working on when he had to disappear; (c) who can be trusted in Harmony Creek (Rick, Sam Miller's father J. Miller — yes, Sam's father, with whom Philip went to high school in Beaumont; the meta-game permits the player to discover this connection through Rick); (d) what Philip wants Maya to do when she is sixteen (read the letter; trust her own observations; not be in a hurry to find him); and (e) the specific phrase Philip wants her to say to Rick when she opens the box, so Rick knows the letter has been read: *"the kettle is on."*
- a small leather-bound journal, half-filled with Philip's handwriting. The journal documents Philip's investigation of NexCorp Residential Solutions' Harmony Creek operations from 2014 to 2017. The journal is the chapter's primary intelligence document for vol6's mid-act. The journal includes maps, names, dates, the wrong-address scheme Diego inherited and extended, the Halsey Studios chassis, the medium's broadcast network, and the building on the east side of Phase III where Diego is being held in vol6 ch1.
- a small canvas messenger bag containing: a NexCorp Residential Solutions ID badge with Philip's photograph (Philip worked NexCorp internally for three years, undercover at the request of a person Rick will identify); a portable cassette recorder with a tape inside; a small ring of unmarked keys; a single 8x10 photograph of Philip in his late twenties, sitting at the back-counter of Cosmic Comics in 1998 with Rick beside him, both of them laughing at something off-frame.
- a single small drawing on a torn piece of brown paper, in Philip's hand: a chair, a kitchen counter, a candle, a small ceramic frog. The drawing is captioned: *"the ones who have the frog — they are the receivers — they are the network — Maya, mija, you are one of them — your mother and I gave you yours when you were nine — you have not used the network yet — when you are ready, use it — the medium will route the letter. — P."*

### gallery unlocks gated by the back-room box

- **Maya asks Rick the question** (gated by Maya having read her own issue #22 in her zine series — see Maya's accumulation file Item I) → the box is given to her. The meta-game's chapter substantially opens. Rick and Maya sit at the back counter while Maya reads the letter. Rick does not interrupt. He has, in his accumulation file, been waiting nine years for this small careful sitting.

The interview is the chapter's smoking-gun document. Rick, in his accumulation file, has been holding this cassette for seven years. It is, by Philip's specific direction, for Maya only. The hundred and forty receivers are not all in Texas and Louisiana. Approximately seventy are. The others are in places the meta-game will surface as needed.

## IV. THE PHONE LEDGER — *"PHONE NOTES · DO NOT DISCUSS"*

A hardcover ledger, black, the spine taped with electrical tape Rick replaces every two years. Rick keeps the ledger in a small fireproof box bolted to the floor under the back counter of Cosmic Comics. The ledger is, by Rick's careful private accounting, the most complete document of the Beaumont 2002 phone-call relationship in existence.

The ledger contains, by Rick's count, approximately one thousand four hundred entries. Each entry follows the same format:

- date
- time
- duration
- the caller's specific phrase (always one of nine standard phrases the caller uses)
- what Rick has done in response

The caller never identifies himself. The caller is, by long inference, the gentleman from Beaumont (see S01E05 of The Pomegranate Hour). The gentleman from Beaumont has been calling Rick since 2002. Rick has, on every call, answered. Rick has, on most calls, simply listened. Rick has, on approximately two hundred calls, done what the caller asked him to do.

The nine standard phrases:

1. *"Rick. She got another one. I thought you'd want to know."* (33% of calls — typically signals a new F.T. message reaching one of Rick's contacts)
2. *"Rick. Lock the back. There's a pickup in town with the wrong address."* (12%)
3. *"Rick. The kettle's on at the Mauriceville address. You may want to call Elicia."* (8%)
4. *"Rick. The boy is fine. He is where he needs to be."* (4%)
5. *"Rick. The girl is doing what she should be doing. Let her. Don't interfere."* (14%)
6. *"Rick. The door was locked. The door is still locked."* (11% — Father Cosgrave's phrase, used by the caller as confirmation of the substrate-network's continuity)
7. *"Rick. The chapter is held."* (9% — the closing phrase from S01E20 of The Pomegranate Hour; used by the caller to signal a chapter closure that Rick can pass forward)
8. *"Rick. Close up early today. Lock the back."* (6% — signals a specific person Rick should not have contact with, that day, in the store)
9. *"Rick. The frog has been moved."* (3% — signals that one of the hundred and forty receivers' ceramic frogs has changed hands — typically because a receiver has died, or has chosen to pass the frog onward)

The ledger's entry for the morning of vol6 ch1 — the call Rick takes after Maya leaves the store for "lunch" — is the call's #2 phrase. **"There's a pickup in town with the wrong address."** Rick locks the back and reverses the OPEN sign. He has been doing what the caller tells him for twenty-four years. The caller is not, by any reasonable interpretation, controlling Rick. The caller is, by Rick's careful internal accounting, a friend who has been signaling.

### gallery unlocks gated by the ledger

- **Open the ledger** (gated on Rick's permission, which is granted to Sam and Maya jointly after they have brought him Diego's running notebook from Item IV of Diego's accumulation file) → unlocks the chapter's primary intelligence document. The meta-game's L3 personnel-graph populates by the largest single expansion in the game.
- **Cross-reference the caller's nine standard phrases with the Pomegranate Hour's recurring lines** → confirms the caller is, by careful triangulation, F.T. — Frasier Temple. Rick has, in some sense, been talking to Frasier on the phone for twenty-four years. Rick has, in his accumulation, also a single hand-written note from Frasier himself, dated 2002, mailed to the shop with no return address: *"Rick — I will call you when I can. I cannot, currently, hold a regular conversation. The chapter requires a different format. Will you accept the phrase-based exchange. — F."* Rick wrote back, on a postcard mailed to a Houston PO box that has, by Rick's check in 2019, not been active since 2003: *"yes. — R."*
- **Locate the entry from the night Dean died** → unlocks a single one-line entry in Rick's hand: *"caller called. caller did not use a standard phrase. caller said only: 'Rick. I am sorry. I cannot make this go differently. I am with you. — F.' I did not respond. The caller hung up. I went back to Dean. I do not know how the caller knew. The caller knew."*

## V. THE STORE — *the accumulated detritus of thirty-four years of independent retail*

Cosmic Comics is the meta-game's primary physical hub in vol6. The store's accumulated detritus is, by quantity, vast. The meta-game permits the player to browse the store interactively. Highlighted items:

- **The OPEN/CLOSED sign.** A hand-painted wooden sign Dean made for Rick in 1992. The sign has, on its CLOSED side, a small detail Dean added: a tiny ceramic frog glued to the lower-right corner. The frog is intact. Dean did not, at the time, know about the medium's network. He had, however, drawn the frog from a small carved one his grandmother in Houma had on her mantel. The frog is one of the hundred and forty. Dean's grandmother was one of the receivers. Rick has, since 2018, been quietly one of them too — Dean's frog passed to him at Dean's death.
- **The Pomegranate Hour shelf.** A small dedicated shelf behind the back counter holding all twenty-two episodes of THE POMEGRANATE HOUR on VHS rip, plus the *_POMEGRANATE_HOUR.md* notes Rick has printed and bound himself, plus a small Polaroid of Elicia Duchane (un-veiled, standing in the shop's front window, taken in 2019 when she came to Cosmic Comics in person to deliver a hard drive of the episodes to Rick). Rick has not, before this Polaroid, owned a photograph of Elicia. He has not, since this Polaroid, taken one.
- **The phone.** The store's landline. The phone the gentleman from Beaumont uses. The phone is a 1989 AT&T two-line desk phone, beige. The phone has been at the back counter for thirty-three years. The phone has been replaced exactly once, in 2007, when lightning struck Linden Boulevard's transformer; Rick replaced the phone with an identical 1989 AT&T unit from the back-room storage. The store has never used a different phone.
- **The back-room couch.** A salvaged green velvet couch the band uses for break. Rick has, in the cushions, an accumulation of band-related detritus — guitar picks, broken strings, a lyrics-page Diego left there four months ago, a half-finished setlist in the bassist's hand from a show that did happen, a small pinch-zine the bassist made and slid between the cushions as a joke. The pinch-zine, when retrieved, is a four-page minicomic by the bassist titled *"WHAT THE DRUMMER DOESN'T KNOW ABOUT THE FOURTH RECORD."* The minicomic is funny. The minicomic is also accurate. The minicomic, when read together with Diego's PHASE III #11, opens the chapter of why the band has been hiding the fourth record from Diego specifically.
- **The brown manila envelope behind the slushie machine** at the Kwik Stop, which is on a similar back-counter shelf at Cosmic Comics in a separate copy — Rick and Sam coordinate, by Rick's quiet arrangement, on the manila-envelope drop system. Anyone in Harmony Creek who has something to leave for someone else can leave it in either envelope. The envelopes get checked daily by Rick and twice-weekly by Sam. The envelopes are, by Rick's count, the most-used informal communication channel in Harmony Creek.
- **The vintage tour-poster wall.** Rick has, on the wall behind the back counter, a small framed grid of nineteen tour posters from regional bands. The grid includes: a Gallatin Band poster from January 14 (the Lake Charles show — the one whose ticket stub is on Sam's corkboard); a Casserole and the Wet Hens poster from a 1989 Houston show; a Sludge Pastoral poster from Halsey Studios' tenth-anniversary in 2014; a Beaumont 9 collective potluck poster from August 1986; an Evergreen Company show poster from the lower French Quarter venue in 2007; and a small handmade poster announcing **"PHILIP DAIGLE'S RETIREMENT FROM NEXCORP RESIDENTIAL SOLUTIONS · GOING-AWAY PARTY · COSMIC COMICS · MARCH 14, 2017 · BYO HOPE."** The retirement-party poster is, by Maya's eventual close inspection, drawn in her father's hand. He drew it himself. The party happened. Maya, age eight, was there. She does not remember.
