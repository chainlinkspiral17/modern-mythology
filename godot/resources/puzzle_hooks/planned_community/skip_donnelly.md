# SKIP DONNELLY · accumulation
## NexCorp Gas & Go · Gallatin Ave · across the intersection from the Kwik Stop

Thirty-nine. Receding hairline he disguises with a backwards cap when off duty and accepts with weary dignity when on. A vape habit he picked up to quit smoking and that has, three years on, become an end in itself. Divorced two years. Lives alone in a one-bedroom on the south side of the Harmony Creek apartment complex. Shift supervisor at the NexCorp Gas & Go since 2018. Promoted to that role from "register attendant" by a regional manager who liked Skip's "low affect" and his "willingness to look at his phone rather than at things." The promotion was, by NexCorp's internal personnel-graph, an explicit selection for non-interference.

Skip is not a bad person. Skip has spent the last seven years trying, with varying success, not to be a person whose presence demands moral attention. He has, by the time of vol6 ch1, been on the verge of failing at this, on purpose.

He is one of Diego Ramos's eleven NexCorp employees whose badge clips Diego has been collecting. Skip has been losing his badge clip on purpose. Skip has been signaling. Skip is the witness Diego had decided he could trust.

Skip does not accumulate the way the others accumulate. Skip's accumulation is small, embarrassed, and — when finally examined — extremely consequential.

## I. THE LOCKER

NexCorp Gas & Go employee locker #4. Skip's locker. The combination is Skip's ex-wife's birthday backward. He has not changed it since he set it in 2018.

The locker is the size of an upright Coke fridge. Skip keeps in it:

- a clean spare polo (NexCorp navy with the embroidered Residential Solutions logo)
- a pair of black slip-resistant work shoes
- a small box of nicotine patches he doesn't use anymore
- his vape kit (the one he uses) plus a backup kit (sealed, never opened, "in case the first one breaks")
- a NexCorp Voice newsletter from the most recent quarter
- a small framed photograph of his ex-wife — Lacey — and their dog Mr. Bunch (the dog is alive and lives with Lacey in San Antonio now)
- a magazine he has not opened in a year (a *Field & Stream* he picked up at the doctor's office and meant to read)
- a roll of duct tape
- a printed-out tarot reading he found on the floor of the breakroom three weeks ago

### the NexCorp Voice newsletter

Quarterly internal employee newsletter, 8 pages, glossy, full-color, professionally produced.

**Spring issue contents (the one in his locker):**

> *Page 1 — letter from the regional director. The director is named **TANYA HORNE.** Her smile in the printed photograph is the smile of a woman who has practiced smiling for photographs since she was in middle-school student council. The letter celebrates Q1 financials. The letter does not mention the resident-payment scheme. The letter does mention a "community engagement initiative" called **GOOD NEIGHBORS GOOD GROUND** that involves NexCorp employees performing small "service hours" in Harmony Creek Estates. Skip has done four hours of service. Skip's service consisted of standing at the Harmony Creek HOA welcome table beside Mrs. Pimentel at the meeting that became S01E15 of the Pomegranate Hour. Skip was the man in the back of the room in the NexCorp polo at the meeting. He did not, by his account afterward, register that he was being filmed.*
>
> *Page 2 — employee spotlight: BENNY CRUZ, sixteen years with the company, drives a NexCorp van on the resident-payment route. Benny is the van driver from S01E15. The article does not say what Benny does. The article says Benny "represents NexCorp's commitment to community-level service" and includes a small photograph of Benny smiling at the wheel of his van. Benny's smile is harder to read than Tanya Horne's.*
>
> *Pages 3-4 — a Q1 community-events calendar. The calendar lists, in small print at the bottom: "internal-only events" with a single asterisked event labeled "GOOD GROUND COORDINATION MEETING — every other Thursday — 7 PM — invitation only." Skip has not been invited. Skip has noticed.*
>
> *Page 5 — a small classified-ads section, internal to the company, where employees post things for sale or trade. Skip has, on close reading, noticed that one of the classifieds — "for sale: 1991 Ford Bronco, low miles, runs well, garaged" — has been running, with the same description and the same phone number, in every issue of the NexCorp Voice for the past three years. The phone number is, on Skip's check, not a working number. The classified is a placeholder for something else. Skip has not, until vol6 ch1, articulated what the something else is.*

> *Page 6 — a small interview with a senior NexCorp executive about "Phase III" — the easternmost build of Harmony Creek Estates. The executive is unnamed. The interview is generic. The interview includes one specific detail: the Phase III "site preparation" is expected to "honor the historic significance of the ground beneath the development by incorporating selected period elements into the master plan." The phrase "historic significance" is, by Skip's careful reading, doing a great deal of work.*

> *Pages 7-8 — birthdays, anniversaries, a small "thank you" section. Skip is in the birthdays section. His coworkers have not, in his entire NexCorp tenure, ever wished him a happy birthday.*

### the printed tarot reading

A single sheet of printer paper, folded in quarters, found by Skip on the breakroom floor three weeks before vol6 ch1. He picked it up. He read it. He folded it. He has not, since picking it up, decided whether to throw it away. The sheet has been in his locker for three weeks.

The sheet is a printout from a website Skip does not recognize — the URL at the bottom of the page is **WUFR.RECEIVERS.NET** — a domain that, on Skip's later check, does not resolve. The printout is a three-card spread:

> **PAST: The Tower**
>
> *what you built was built on something. what was built on it has fallen. you knew this. you are not alone in knowing this.*
>
> **PRESENT: The Eight of Cups**
>
> *you are walking away from a table you set carefully. the cups on the table are full. you are leaving them. nobody is asking you to come back.*
>
> **FUTURE: The Hermit**
>
> *the lantern you carry is cracked. the cracked panel is what lights your way. the road you walk is not lit by anyone else. that is appropriate. that is the chapter.*

The printout is unsigned. The reading is, by Skip's quiet acknowledgment to himself, accurate. He has been walking away from the table. He has been carrying his own cracked lantern. He has not, in any conversation, named what the Tower was.

The reading is, on close inspection of the printer paper itself, printed on the same paper stock Cosmic Comics uses for their photocopier output. The reading was printed at Cosmic Comics. Skip has not, before this episode, been to Cosmic Comics. He does not know who left the reading on the breakroom floor. He has, since picking it up, started noticing the same paper stock in other small places — on the manila envelope behind the slushie machine, on the printed copy of Sam's *Gas Station Observations* zine he has been quietly buying every issue of, on the back of the receipt the bassist of the Gallatin Band hands him when buying gas. The paper, by Skip's slow inference, is everyone's.

## II. THE EX-WIFE'S CD — *"things i used to like before you · for skip · march 2023"*

A burned CD-R Lacey mailed to Skip the spring after their divorce was finalized. The CD arrived in a small padded envelope at his apartment, no return address, postmarked San Antonio. The disc is labeled in Lacey's hand on the silver of the disc itself, with a fine-tip Sharpie:

> *"things i used to like before you · for skip · march 2023"*

There is no case insert. There is no track list. There is no note. The disc is, on Skip's first play, a 78-minute mix of:

- songs Lacey loved in college (early-2000s pop punk, a handful of singer-songwriter tracks, two Sigur Rós tracks, a Modest Mouse album cut)
- songs from a wedding-DJ playlist they used at their own wedding in 2017
- songs from the year before they met
- one song — track 12, three minutes long — by an artist Skip does not recognize. The track is a single voice singing what sounds like a children's song. Skip cannot, on the playback, identify the words. The track might be in another language. The track might be in English and might be sung by someone whose voice has been processed to be illegible. The track is, by Skip's repeated listening, the only track on the disc he has begun to suspect Lacey did not put there.

Skip has played the disc, by his quiet private count, approximately seventy times in two years. He has not, in two years, written Lacey back to thank her. He has, however, decided that the disc is the kindest thing she ever did for him and that he does not, accordingly, get to write back about it.

### track 12

The unidentified track. Three minutes long. A single voice. No instrumentation except a faint underlying hum at 60 Hz with a slower 0.3 Hz envelope — the meta-game's careful listener will identify the hum as identical to the Carla Vega's-noise hum on Carla's protest-zine cassette.

The voice sings what is, on Skip's hundredth listening, identifiable as the song the void mother hums in S01E03 of The Pomegranate Hour and on her cassette tape **THE WORLD — for M.** in the rewritten S01E21. Skip has not seen The Pomegranate Hour. Skip has not heard the void mother's name. Skip has, however, been holding the song in his small private accounting as the song that arrived without explanation on a disc from his ex-wife.

The voice on track 12 is, by direct comparison to the void mother's hummed cassette, the void mother's voice. The recording is, by Skip's later inquiry, not on any commercial release. The recording is not on YouTube. The recording exists, by the meta-game's careful accounting, in three places: (a) on the cassette in Marelle and Elicia's apartment, (b) on a master tape in Halsey Studios' archive (the void mother visited Halsey Studios once in 1989; the engineer who recorded her was the engineer's father; the master is on a shelf labeled "VOID MOTHER · 1989 · DO NOT TRANSFER"), and (c) on Skip's CD-R from his ex-wife.

The meta-game permits the player to identify how the recording got from Halsey Studios to Lacey. The chain, on careful tracing: Lacey's older brother is the bassist of the Gallatin Band. The bassist has, on his own quiet schedule, access to the Halsey Studios archive. The bassist found the tape four years ago. The bassist made a digital copy. The bassist gave the digital copy to Lacey because Lacey had been having a hard year. The bassist did not, at the time, know that Lacey would put it on Skip's CD-R two years after he gave it to her.

Lacey did not know who was singing on track 12. She knew the song was the kindest thing the bassist had given her. She decided Skip needed it.

- **Cross-reference track 12 with Marelle's cassette in vol5 / Pomegranate Hour S01E21** → confirms the void mother's voice

It has been broadcast, in fragments, to nearly every receiver in the chapter. Skip has, in some sense, been on the network without knowing.

## III. THE UNDER-THE-TILL FOLDER

A small manila folder Skip keeps under the cash drawer at his register. The folder contains, by Skip's careful unspoken accounting, the documents he has chosen not to throw away even though throwing them away would be safer.

### contents

- the printed-out tarot reading (also a copy; the original is in his locker)
- a single receipt from the Kwik Stop, dated nine months ago, for one half-gallon of milk and one box of crayons, total $7.84, paid in cash by a customer Skip has not been able to identify, found by Skip in the Gas & Go parking lot at the end of a Saturday shift — Skip has kept the receipt because the receipt has, on the back, a small pencil drawing of a chair. The drawing's caption, in pencil, in a child's hand: *"this is my chair. — m.d., age 7."* The chair is recognizably the chair from Maya Daigle's *General Store* page 88. Maya was eight when she drew it on this receipt. Skip does not know Maya. Skip has, however, kept the receipt for nine months because the chair is, in some sense Skip has not articulated, the chair he has been failing to sit in.
- a small Polaroid of the black pickup with Louisiana plates, parked behind the car wash on a Saturday afternoon, with the white NexCorp van beside it and Benny Cruz at the van's window receiving an envelope from the pickup. Skip took the Polaroid himself, from inside the Gas & Go, with a single-use camera he bought from the back-counter display three years ago for this exact purpose. He has not developed any of the other thirty-eight exposures on the camera roll. He has, on impulse, developed this one. The development was done by a small mail-order Polaroid service Skip does not remember selecting. The Polaroid arrived in the mail two weeks ago in an envelope with no return address.
- a small folded piece of butcher paper that, when opened, has a hand-drawn map of the Gas & Go parking lot with seven marked positions where Skip has, in the past three years, witnessed the same envelope-exchange between the pickup and Benny's van. The marks are in red. Skip drew the map himself, on a slow Wednesday night, three months ago. He did not, at the time, intend to do anything with the map. He has, however, not thrown the map away.
- a brown envelope, sealed, addressed in Skip's own hand to **"R. — Cosmic Comics — Linden Strip Mall — please pass to whoever can use it — S.D., reg. 4."** Skip wrote the envelope three weeks ago. He has not delivered it. The envelope contains: the Polaroid, the map, the receipt with the chair drawing, the printed tarot reading, a small one-page typed letter from Skip explaining what he has seen and what he has not done about it. The letter is, by Skip's quiet careful accounting, his resignation from the part of his life that has been about not interfering. He has not, in three weeks, decided whether to deliver the envelope. He intends to deliver it on his next day off, which will be the Tuesday after vol6 ch1.

## IV. THE MIXTAPE — *"untitled · for someone · 1999"*

In Skip's nightstand drawer at his apartment, at the bottom under a pile of receipts and a single unread copy of *Catch-22* from his high-school senior year, a single Maxell XLII cassette. The case insert is hand-cut from a piece of college-ruled notebook paper. The insert has, in Skip's adolescent handwriting (Skip was nineteen when he made the tape), the title: **"untitled · for someone · 1999."**

Skip made the tape for a person he was, at nineteen, falling carefully in love with. The person was a coworker at the Houston restaurant where Skip washed dishes during his community-college summer. The person's name has been carefully forgotten by Skip across the twenty-six years since.

Skip did not give the tape to the person. Skip lost his nerve. The summer ended. Skip went back to his community college, and the person moved to New Orleans, and Skip never saw the person again.

Skip kept the tape in the nightstand drawer of every apartment he has lived in since 1999. The tape has been with him for twenty-six years. It is, by the meta-game's recognition, the longest-held single object in his possession.

### the voice memo at track 15

A 1:30 voice memo Skip recorded into the dual-cassette deck microphone, alone in his college-student apartment, at midnight on the night he finished the mixtape. The recording is Skip at nineteen, his voice slightly higher than it is now, careful, embarrassed, sincere.

> *"hey. so. okay. this is — this is a tape. i made you a tape. i don't — i'm not good at saying things in person. i wanted to give you a tape. that's — that's what this is. okay so the tape has fourteen songs on it that i love. the first side is the songs i listen to in the morning. the second side is the songs i listen to at night. you'll know which is which without me telling you because the tape sides are labeled SIDE A and SIDE B which is, obviously, what tape sides are labeled. okay. um. so. the reason i wanted to give you a tape is i. um. i think you're. i think you're a good person. i think you're a — a person i would want to be in a room with for a really long time. i'm sorry if that's a weird thing to say. i don't — i don't know if you want to be in a room with me. i'm not — i'm not asking that. i'm just — i wanted you to have the tape. i wanted you to have the songs. okay. um. that's. that's all. um. — skip donnelly. august 1999."*

The voice memo ends. The cassette ends shortly afterward.

Skip has not, in twenty-six years, played the tape. The tape has been in the drawer. He has, by his quiet private accounting, also not — in twenty-six years — written the voice memo's careful sentence to anyone else.

The tape sits.

## V. DETRITUS — *the small things*

Skip's apartment is sparsely furnished. He does not, by his own assessment, accumulate. The few items the meta-game permits the player to surface in his apartment:

- a Polaroid corkboard with three pictures — one of his ex-wife Lacey at their wedding, one of Mr. Bunch the dog, one of Skip's late mother. He does not look at the corkboard often. He cannot bring himself to take any of the three pictures down.
- a kitchen drawer of takeout menus, only one of which (a Cajun-Vietnamese place called **MARIE-TUẤN'S** in Houma) he has actually called
- on his coffee table, a stack of Sam Miller's *Gas Station Observations* zines, all eight issues, in order, neatly stacked. Skip has been buying them at the Kwik Stop since issue #1. He has, by his careful private accounting, also tipped Sam an extra five dollars at the register every time he buys a zine, by buying a Lone Star tallboy he does not drink and not asking for the change. He has, accordingly, contributed approximately forty dollars to Sam's zine-printing fund. Sam has noticed. Sam does not, by her own assessment, know what to do with the noticing.
- a single small ceramic frog, intact, marble-sized, on the windowsill above his kitchen sink. Skip does not know where the frog came from. The frog was in his apartment when he moved in three years ago. He did not throw the frog out. He has watered the frog twice, by mistake, when watering a small basil plant he keeps on the same sill. The frog does not, by his observation, require water. The frog has stayed on the windowsill for three years.
- a torn piece of brown paper, folded in his wallet, that he found in the gas station parking lot one Wednesday two months ago. The paper, when unfolded, has a single line of handwritten text in a hand he does not recognize: *"skip — when you are ready, write to the medium. you will be ready soon. — f."* Skip does not know what the medium is. Skip has not, in two months, thrown the paper away. The paper is, by the meta-game's substrate-network accounting, his invitation to the network. The paper has been waiting for him to accept.

### gallery unlocks gated by the small things

- **Notice the ceramic frog on the kitchen windowsill** → confirms Skip as a receiver. The meta-game permits the player, at this point, to mentally place Skip among the hundred and forty. He has, in some sense, been a receiver since he moved into the apartment three years ago. He has been a receiver without knowing.
- **Unfold the torn paper from his wallet** → unlocks Skip's invitation to the medium-network. The meta-game permits the player to facilitate Skip writing his first letter through the postal route. The letter, when written and slid under the brown manila envelope at Cosmic Comics, will be received by Elicia Duchane in Mauriceville approximately three weeks later. She will write back. Skip will have, for the first time in his adult life, a correspondent who is not asking him for anything except his careful attention. The chapter, in some sense the deck has been preparing for since vol1, has been quietly waiting for Skip's small careful turn.

## CLOSING NOTE

Skip Donnelly is not a hero. Skip Donnelly is not, by the meta-game's deeper accounting, an antagonist. Skip Donnelly is a man who has been doing what a corporate-personnel-selection algorithm chose him to do for seven years, who has accumulated, in spite of himself, evidence and resonances and small careful objects, and who has, by the time of vol6 ch1, been on the verge of choosing to no longer be selected by the algorithm.

His accumulation is, accordingly, smaller than the others. The smallness is the chapter. The chapter is, by its own internal logic, that small accumulations are also the chapter's accumulations. The medium has the postal route. The kettle is on. Skip's frog has been on his windowsill for three years.

The chapter has been held.
