# MAYA DAIGLE · accumulation
## Cosmic Comics back room · Harmony Creek Estates · 16 years old

Sixteen. Short curly hair, dyed dark purple at the tips. Black-frame glasses. A small constellation tattoo on the inside of her left wrist that her grandmother does not know about. Lives with her grandmother **CONNIE DAIGLE** at lot 7 of phase I — one of the original 1991 Harmony Creek lots. Her father — **PHILIP DAIGLE**, alive but not present, calling her from a place she does not name — has been a phantom in her life since she was nine. Her mother is in Graustark, Louisiana, with her second husband; Maya spends every other Christmas there. She works at Cosmic Comics for Rick, on weekends and after school, sorting the DO NOT SORT YET pile with the patient indifference of a sixteen-year-old who has been given the kind of task adults use to keep teenagers busy.

She is a careful, observant, fiercely intelligent kid. F.T. (Frasier Temple) has been writing to her for nine months. She has not told anyone.

This is what she has.

## I. THE ZINE SERIES — *"NEWS FROM HARMONY CREEK"*

Maya's signature zine. Twenty-three issues across two years. Quarter-letter size (3.5 x 4.25 inches), eight pages each, photocopied at the Cosmic Comics machine on Sunday mornings before Rick comes in. The zines are sold for one dollar each at the back counter; the dollars go into a small jar Maya labels **"THE FUND"** without ever explaining what the fund is for.

Each issue is a single observational piece — a person, a place, an object — rendered as a hybrid of text, comix, and small map fragments. Maya works in fineliner pen and grey marker. Her style is, by Rick's quiet opinion, the best draftsmanship in the regional minicomics scene.

She does not, anywhere in the zines, name herself as the author. The byline reads: **"by M.D."**

### issue contents (selected)

**Issue #6 — "THE WIDOW AT LOT 14."**

Mrs. PIMENTEL, the welcome-table greeter from S01E15 (The Pomegranate Hour · The Devil), lives at lot 14. Maya's zine on her is a small portrait — three pages of text, two pages of comix, a small hand-drawn map of Mrs. Pimentel's daily walk around the development. The portrait is sympathetic. Maya likes Mrs. Pimentel. Mrs. Pimentel has, for two years, been quietly bringing Maya a small plate of homemade cookies on Maya's birthday. Maya has noticed that the cookies arrive without a note, with the kind of brown wax paper that the cookies are wrapped in, and that the paper has, on careful examination, the same fiber as the paper Maya's grandfather used to use in the funeral parlor he ran in Graustark until 1994. Mrs. Pimentel and Maya's grandfather knew each other.

The zine does not say so. The zine says: *"Mrs. P. brings the cookies on the day. she does not say who they are from. I have, however, identified the paper. some chapters get themselves told by the wrapping."*

**Issue #11 — "THE BACK COOLER AT THE KWIK STOP."**

A small companion zine to Sam Miller's *Gas Station Observations* #3. Maya and Sam know each other through the zine network. Maya has, with Sam's permission, drawn a comic-strip adaptation of Sam's prose piece on the back-cooler customer. The strip is six panels:

- panel 1: a wide shot of the Kwik Stop's back cooler with a man in a golf shirt standing in front of it
- panel 2: a close-up on the cooler's reflection, in which we see — for the first time — that the reflection contains *the back of the cooler's own surface,* an infinite recursion of itself
- panel 3: a close-up on Sam at the register, not looking at the man, eating a Hot Pocket
- panel 4: the man's face, in three-quarters, his eyes on the cooler glass
- panel 5: in the cooler glass, Sam is visible — but in panel 5's Sam reflection, Sam is looking directly back at the man through the glass, even though in panel 3 Sam was not looking up
- panel 6: a final wide shot, identical to panel 1, but the back cooler is now empty of beer

The strip is captioned at the bottom in a small box: *"adapted with permission from a piece by s.m. (sam, hello) · m.d. · summer."*

**Issue #15 — "THE GRANDMOTHER WHO VACUUMS IN PATTERNS."**

Maya's most autobiographical zine. The subject is **CONNIE DAIGLE,** Maya's grandmother. The zine documents Connie's habit of vacuuming the living room in patterns — four beats on, four beats off, the vacuum's whine on a 4/4 rhythm Connie has not, by Maya's questioning, ever consciously noticed but performs without fail every other afternoon.

The zine includes:

- a small ink drawing of Connie at the vacuum, mid-pattern
- a transcribed beat-grid, four bars, with the vacuum's on/off notated as percussion
- a note: *"I have, for two years, been hearing this beat as a thing I assumed was a coincidence. I have, in the last six weeks, identified the beat as not a coincidence. the beat is a known beat. it is the beat WUFR transmits at quarter-volume on its sub-carrier on Thursdays at 3 PM. my grandmother does not, by any rational measure, listen to WUFR. she does not own a shortwave radio. she does not, when asked, know what WUFR is. but the beat is the beat."*

Maya does not, in the zine, propose a theory. She does, in the zine's small final panel, draw her grandmother at the vacuum, in profile, with a single line of static rising from her left ear like a thought bubble. The static is unlabeled. The zine ends on this image.

**Issue #19 — "THE PHANTOM AT 892 ASHBERRY."**

A long zine, twelve pages instead of eight, about the empty house at 892 Ashberry Drive. Maya has been walking past 892 on her way home from Cosmic Comics for a year. The house is, by tax records, owned by a holding company. The house has been unoccupied — by Maya's careful observation — for at least eight years. The house's exterior is maintained: lawn mowed, windows clean, mailbox empty. The house's interior is, by Maya's various nighttime peerings through the front window, completely empty. No furniture. No carpets. No light fixtures. No drapes.

The zine documents Maya's twelve months of observation. It includes:

- a hand-drawn floor plan of the house's visible interior from the front window
- a small comix sequence in which Maya, in the strip, considers breaking in and decides not to
- a list of vehicles that have stopped at 892 across the year (Maya's grandmother does not let her ride her bike past 11 PM; Maya respects this; the list is, accordingly, daytime-only). The list includes: a black F-150 (Diego's), a white sedan with no front plate (the Sunburn from the Kwik Stop), a NexCorp Residential Solutions van (twice in one week, then never again), a beige Buick (Mrs. Pimentel), and — once, on a Tuesday afternoon — *a small grey sedan driven by a woman in a long black blouse and a pencil skirt, whose face was obscured by what Maya, examining her drawing afterward, identified as a veil of cut cassette tape.*

The grey sedan stopped for three minutes. The driver did not get out. The sedan drove off.

Maya did not include the cassette-tape-veil drawing in the zine. She included a small dashed-line outline of the driver in profile, with the caption: *"the unidentified visitor of June 7th. recorded for the record. not yet matched to any known party."*

She has, however, in a separate sketchbook (Item III in this accumulation), drawn the driver's face. The face is veiled in the sketch. The veil is the veil.

**Issue #22 — "THE FATHER WHO CALLED."**

Maya's most personal zine. Three pages of text, no comix. The zine is the account of the Tuesday call from her father, six weeks before vol6 ch1 — the call Maya has not told anyone in Harmony Creek about. F.T. mentioned this call in his message to her. The zine documents the call.

> *my father called me on a Tuesday afternoon at 3:14 PM. he was scared when he said it. he said please. he said he'd send someone. a man named philip roberts drove up to get me. I went back with him to graustark for three days. then I came back here.*
>
> *what my father said on the call — I have not transcribed in full. I am not yet ready. the parts of the call I am willing to record here are:*
>
> *1. he asked me to stay inside grandma's house for the day.*
> *2. he said he would send someone.*
> *3. he said the someone would say a specific phrase when he arrived so I would know to go with him. the phrase was "the kettle is on."*
> *4. he said he loved me. he had not said that on a phone call in nine years.*
> *5. he said: "maya — if I cannot come back, I have left things for you in the back room of cosmic comics. rick will know where. ask him only when you are ready."*
>
> *I have not asked Rick yet. I am not yet ready. — m.d.*

Maya printed twelve copies of issue #22. She put them in the back-room manila envelope at the Kwik Stop, in the Cosmic Comics rack, and in the cypress tree mailbox on Linden. She did not put one in the Harmony Creek HOA clubhouse rack. Mr. Sage would have removed it within six hours and Maya knew this. The zine reached, by careful distribution, only sympathetic readers.

**Issue #23 — "F.T."** *(in progress; pencils complete; the photocopier hasn't seen it yet)*

The zine on her anonymous correspondent. The pencils document F.T.'s nine months of messages, the small details he has revealed about himself, and the moment in his most recent message — the morning of vol6 ch1 — when he stated three things in sequence and the third was *"Diego Ramos is alive."*

Maya has not yet decided whether to publish this issue. She has, however, drawn it. She has drawn F.T.'s likeness from his small detail-clues: an older man, late forties, glasses, working in a rust-girdered workshop with a CRT monitor showing a heart-rate trace. The likeness is, by separate cross-reference with the meta-game's vol5 Magician hotspots, Frasier Temple's likeness within a small margin of error. Maya has drawn him without ever seeing him.

She has dated the zine in pencil: **"to publish — when I have permission."** The permission, the meta-game permits us to discover, will be given.

## II. THE LONG-RUNNING COMIX — *"GENERAL STORE"*

Maya's most ambitious project. A graphic novel she has been drawing for four years, since she was twelve. Currently 187 pages. Drawn on standard 8.5 x 11 sheets of printer paper she takes from Cosmic Comics' supply (Rick has noticed; Rick does not say anything; Rick has, in fact, deliberately ordered an extra ream every quarter for the past three years to ensure her supply). Stored in a single hardback portfolio she keeps under her bed.

Maya has shown the comix to three people: Rick, Sam Miller, and (in his messages) F.T. She intends to publish it as a single trade paperback when she is twenty-one. Rick has agreed to host the print run at Cosmic Comics. F.T. has not promised anything but has noted, in his most recent message, that he would like to read it when she is willing.

### the comix

**"GENERAL STORE"** — a graphic novel about a fictional Louisiana parish general store called CLAIMETTE'S, run by a fictional matriarch named ETIENNETTE CLAIMETTE, set across forty years of small-town life from 1962 to 2002. The store is the chapter's centerpiece. Every character in the parish, every chapter, every event, passes through the store. The store is the chapter's omniscient narrator. The store is, in the comix's gentle conceit, also a hologram — a hologram that nobody in the parish realizes is a hologram, including Etiennette herself.

The comix is, by Rick's quiet assessment, *"the chapter of vol6, rendered in Maya's hand, in advance, without Maya yet knowing she is rendering it."*

Selected scenes:

**Scene — "the man in the golf shirt, 1971."**

A man in a golf shirt enters Claimette's. Etiennette is at the counter. The man looks at the back cooler. Etiennette, from the counter, observes the man's reflection in the cooler glass. Etiennette and the reflection make a small acknowledgment. The man takes nothing. The man leaves.

Etiennette, after he leaves, writes a single line in her ledger: *"that one is on the corridor."*

(Maya drew this scene in 2022. She did not, at the time, know about the back-cooler customer at the Kwik Stop in 2025. The scene's prefiguration is a coincidence the meta-game does not require us to explain.)

**Scene — "the boy who drew comics, 1984."**

A young boy — eleven, brown skin, careful hands — sits at a small table in Claimette's reading area, drawing his own comic on a piece of butcher paper. Etiennette brings him a sweet tea. She does not charge him. She says, over her shoulder as she returns to the counter: *"chéri, your hand will be famous. don't waste it on people who don't deserve it."*

The boy is, on close inspection of his hand-drawing-his-comic, the boy who in 2025 will be Rick at Cosmic Comics. Maya has rendered Rick's childhood — including his particular pencil-grip — without ever having asked him about it. Rick, when he saw this page in 2023, sat down at the back counter and did not speak for nine minutes.

**Scene — "the wedding, 1991."**

Two characters in Claimette's, a young woman in a green-and-burgundy dress and a young man in a clean suit, buying wedding bands from the small jewelry case. Etiennette completes the sale. The young woman is, on close inspection, the woman in S01E03 (The Pomegranate Hour · The Empress) — the void mother. The young man is, on close inspection, the chairman of S01E04 — Dante. The wedding bands they purchase are not the wedding bands either of them wore. The wedding bands are the wedding bands they would have worn, in another chapter, that they did not buy. Etiennette, after the sale, does not write a ledger note. She closes the case. She locks it. She does not reopen it.

(Maya drew this scene in 2023. She does not know either Dante or the void mother. She has not been to the model home. She does not know S01E03. She has, however, drawn the scene with the same emerald-and-burgundy palette of the painted Empress card.)

**Scene — "the dropout, 1996."**

A young man — late teens, lanky, his clothes clearly slept in — enters Claimette's looking for work. Etiennette assesses him. She does not hire him. She gives him a sandwich and a sealed envelope containing twenty dollars and tells him, in a careful tone: *"chéri, go to the studio at the back of the orchard. tell them I sent you. they will let you tape your drums in their barn after dinner. that is what you need. not a job."*

The young man is the bassist of the Gallatin Band, fifteen years before he founds the band. The studio at the back of the orchard is Halsey Studios. The barn the young man tapes his drums in is the barn the Gallatin Band records the third record in. The Gallatin Band's existence, by Maya's careful tracing in *General Store,* originates from a single sandwich and twenty dollars Etiennette Claimette gave to a kid in 1996.

(Maya drew this scene in 2024. She does not know the Gallatin Band's history. The bassist's recollection of how he ended up at Halsey Studios — recorded in his liner notes for the band's debut LP — does, however, include the phrase: *"an old lady at a general store in southern Louisiana told me where to go. I never got her name. I still owe her twenty dollars."* Maya has not read the liner notes. The meta-game requires us to discover, slowly, that *General Store* is in some sense recovering an actual history through Maya's drawing of a fictional one.)

## III. THE SKETCHBOOK

Hardcover, A5, black, the cover marked in white-out marker: **"NOT FOR PUBLICATION · NOT YET · M.D."**

Maya's private sketchbook. The book she draws in when she is not making zines or working on *General Store.* The book is, by her own quiet account, where she puts everything she is not yet ready to publish.

The book is 144 pages. Approximately ninety pages are filled. The contents — selected:

- a portrait of Sam Miller at the Kwik Stop counter, drawn from memory after a visit. The portrait has Sam's smile, which Sam has not, on any sketch of herself, recognized as her own.
- a portrait of Diego Ramos behind the kit at Halsey Studios. Maya has been to Halsey Studios once, with Diego, in October. She drew him from the back. The drum kit is anatomically correct. The drumstick in his right hand is in motion, mid-strike.
- the cassette-tape-veil drawing from issue #19 (the woman who parked at 892 Ashberry). The drawing is detailed. The veil is rendered as approximately eighty strands, falling to chest level. The eyes through the veil are calm. The mouth is closed.
- a series of small thumbnail sketches of every NexCorp Residential Solutions employee Maya has seen in Harmony Creek. The thumbnails are labeled by date and location. There are sixty-three thumbnails. Maya has been keeping the count.
- a single page of Father's-Voice transcriptions — direct quotes, in Maya's hand, from the Tuesday call from her father. Maya did transcribe the call after all, in the sketchbook, not in the zine. The transcription is six hundred words long. It is not in publication. It will not be published.
- a small drawing of the bungalow at 892 Ashberry, with a question mark in pencil at the front door
- a drawing of Rick at Cosmic Comics, sitting on the back-counter stool, his hands folded on the counter, his expression the expression of a man who has been waiting nine years to be asked a particular question. Maya drew this from life. Rick saw her drawing it. Rick did not, when she finished, ask to see the drawing. Maya has decided that he knew without seeing it that he was the subject.
- a single drawing of F.T. (Frasier Temple). The drawing is, as noted in zine #23's pencil notes, made from his message details. The drawing's caption, in pencil: *"my best guess. — m.d."*
- a single drawing of a man's wrist — left wrist, faded tan band where a watch was — with no body attached, floating on the page. The drawing is small. The caption: *"the wrist on diego's photographs. drawn separately because the wrist is, in some sense, separate. — m.d."*

## V. DETRITUS — *the closet box*

A single wooden box, approximately 12 x 8 x 6 inches, in Maya's bedroom closet. Maya's grandmother does not know about the box. The box was a gift from her father — the last in-person gift, from when Maya was seven. The box is the chapter's private chest.

### contents

- the mixtape (item IV above)
- the printed-out F.T. chat logs (twenty-seven months of correspondence, printed at Cosmic Comics on Sundays, folded and stored in chronological order)
- a small Polaroid of Maya's father at age twenty-six, in front of a fishing boat on the Sabine. The Polaroid is the last photograph Maya has of her father. The boat in the background is, on careful inspection, the same boat Anya escaped from Vung Tau on in 1975. Maya does not know this. The meta-game permits the player to identify the boat by cross-referencing the photograph with S01E06's Anya backstory. Maya's father knew the Nguyễn family.
