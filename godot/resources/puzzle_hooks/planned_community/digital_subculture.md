# THE DIGITAL SUBCULTURE
## BBSs · fictional games across parallel realities · chiptune · fan art · MIDI interpretation
## a companion to music_strata.md

> *"the BBS was, for those of us who had one in our bedroom in
> 1992, the place where the rest of the band hung out when we
> were not actually a band. we never met in person. we mailed
> each other floppies. we left ASCII notes on the message board
> at 2 AM. we drew each other things in DELUXE PAINT II that
> took six hours and that we never printed. the BBS was the
> record we made together. nobody else was ever going to hear
> the record."*
>
> — k. akhtar, *cassette epistemology* #51, late summer 2024

A working reference for the deck's developers: the parallel
substrate that the music strata describe was never just music.
The same regional network — the same eastern Texas hum corridor,
the same Glitch Report editors, the same Frasier-Temple-circle
people in the warehouse — ran a tightly interconnected set of
**bulletin board systems**, traded **fictional video games** that
exist (in the deck's parallel-realities conceit) across every
console and PC of their era, generated **chiptune and MIDI
artifacts** the music store would never carry, and traded **fan
art** the gallery never showed.

The digital substrate is the music substrate's twin. The two
strata interlock. Frasier's SCUMM machine at Pizza Pirate (vol7)
is the load-bearing object that ties them together — the SCUMM
sticks contain digested ROMs of fictional games made by fictional
studios that existed in the BBS scene that overlapped with the
band scene that recorded onto the substrate that surfaces in the
cannery basement.

Below: the BBSs, the studios, the games, the chiptune scene, and
the magazines that — in this small parallel reality — actually
reviewed all of it.

# I · THE BBSs

The local board-system network of Harmony County and the
surrounding parishes, 1988–2004. Approximately fourteen boards
operated at peak (1994). By 2004, four were left; by 2009, two;
by 2014, one (CYPRESS-LINE, see below). The internet absorbed
the population but did not absorb the artifacts, which sit on
disk images in the same basement racks the music masters sit in.

## boards index

### HARMONY-NET — *1989–1996*

Sysop: **"OBELISK"** (real name reserved). Run from a single 2400-
baud line out of OBELISK's apartment in the Harmony Creek Estates
phase II apartment complex, on a PC clone with a 40 MB hard drive
and a Hayes Smartmodem. Three message boards (GENERAL, MUSIC,
WHATSITS-AND-DOOHICKEYS), one file area (mostly chiptune
trackers, scene demos, a small library of MIDI interpretations
of regional bands), one door game (TRADEWARS 2002). Average
fourteen callers per week at peak.

OBELISK is, by the deck's careful canonical reading, **E. Aday** of
Static Life and the Glitch Report. Running a BBS from a Harmony
Creek apartment was, for E., a small careful infiltration. She
shut HARMONY-NET down in 1996 the same month WJMR went off the
air. The disk-image archive of HARMONY-NET survives, in 2025, in
a single 5.25" floppy box in the Henderson Donation #6, which
Maya has not yet opened.

### BAYOU-GATEWAY — *1990–1998*

FidoNet node 1:380/14. Sysop: **"BLEU."** Two lines (one
public, one FidoNet-mail), Houma. Significantly more traffic than
HARMONY-NET — perhaps eighty callers per week at peak. Hosted
**echomail** for the regional FidoNet conferences including
ART_REGIONAL, MUSIC_SCENE, and an unusual conference called
**THE_CARRIER** that had only twelve subscribers across its
seven-year life and that consisted entirely of long careful
descriptions of small atmospheric anomalies observed by the
subscribers in their own homes.

BLEU is, by the deck's reserved hand, the great-aunt of Carla
Vega at lot 47. Carla inherited the original BAYOU-GATEWAY
hard-disk image when BLEU died in 2017. The image sits, in 2025,
on Carla's desktop's external drive. Carla has, by her own
quiet accounting, read approximately one tenth of the THE_CARRIER
conference's archive. She is reading slowly.

### BEAUMONT-FILE-EXCHANGE — *1988–1994*

Sysop: **"PELLETIER."** Three lines, Beaumont. The local center
of regional zine distribution by FTP-via-BBS — every issue of the
Glitch Report's 1988–1990 run was uploaded to BFE as a text file
(typed in by E. Aday over several long evenings); every issue of
the Beaumont 9 collective's August 1986 *Nine-Way Potlatch*
booklet had been uploaded already, by a different hand. BFE was
also the regional clearinghouse for **fan-MIDI interpretations**
of Locust Seventeen songs (since Pelletier was the bassist of
Locust Seventeen; he ran the BBS partly to host the fan archive
that arose around his band).

Pelletier — **Andrew Pelletier** — went into a different life
in 1991 after the apartment burglary; the BBS continued under
PELLETIER's sysop handle for three more years, run by a friend
of his named **DEFCON-1** (real name reserved). DEFCON-1 took
the BBS offline in March 1994 with no notice. The final message
board post, by DEFCON-1, reads: *"the system is going dark.
PELLETIER asked me to ask you all, in the way he would have
asked you himself, not to look for it again. — d."*

### WAR-OF-NUMBERS-BBS — *1992–2000*

Sysop: **"L.G."** Single line, Lake Charles. Focused entirely
on **number stations** — shortwave radio broadcasts of numbers
read aloud by automated voices, used historically for covert
intelligence transmission. WAR-OF-NUMBERS had twenty-six callers
across its eight-year life, all of whom were either Glitch
Report editors, NRM members, or — by L.G.'s subsequent quiet
confirmation in a 2019 letter — *"the kind of people who, on
their own, had identified the substation hum as a number
station that did not, technically, broadcast numbers."*

L.G.'s identity has not been publicly confirmed. The deck
permits L.G. to be **Linda Marrero** (Saint Augustine Grass,
lead vocalist), now in Galveston, who declined the 2024
interview and replied only *"I don't want to be in the book. I
want the field to be in the book."* L.G. and Linda Marrero
have, in any case, the same initials.

### ADELPHIA-DELI — *1999–2003*

Sysop: **"DICKENS."** Members-only. Two lines. Run from a 486
in the back room of *Adelphia's Deli* in Graustark, where the
**NRM** (Neo-Renaissance Men) collective met weekly. ADELPHIA-
DELI was not, technically, a BBS in the traditional public-call
sense; the only way to get an account was to be vouched for by
an existing member, and there were never more than nineteen
accounts active at any time.

The file area contained, at the BBS's peak: a complete archive of
the Beaumont 9 zines; the Glitch Report run; every released and
unreleased static.fm track; the Mantel Black master tape (yes —
predating the LP recording by four years, the BBS had an early
demo); approximately forty hours of MIDI interpretations of
regional bands; eleven prototype Frasier-Temple-built **demons**
in compiled form (executables for DOS, Windows 95, Mac OS 7); and
— by the careful inventory Frasier maintains for himself and
nobody else — the prototype SCUMM machine code that became the
Pizza Pirate cabinet.

DICKENS is — by the codex's reserved hand — the early form of
**Dickens Dean.** ADELPHIA-DELI was, by Frasier's quiet later
acknowledgment, the place where Dickens *first started being
Dickens.* The BBS shut down in 2003 when Adelphia's Deli closed
and the 486 was, by Frasier's own carting, moved to the
warehouse. The 486 is, in 2025, the bottom unit in Frasier's
warehouse-cathedral rack, powered, running, *receiving.*

### CYPRESS-LINE — *1995–* *(still online as of 2025)*

Sysop: **"OBELISK"** (yes, the same one; E. Aday started
CYPRESS-LINE the same week she shut HARMONY-NET down). Single
line, accessible via dial-up at a phone number E. has not
published since 2001 and that you have to be told by an existing
caller. Approximately seven callers, all monthly.

CYPRESS-LINE has, by E.'s own decision, no file area, no door
games, no echomail. CYPRESS-LINE has exactly one message board.
The message board has, since 1995, exactly one purpose: every
caller logs in, reads the most recent post (always the previous
caller's), and writes one new post in reply. The new post is the
caller's *one careful observation from the previous month.* The
observation can be anything. The convention is that the
observation should be the kind of thing the caller has not
written down anywhere else.

Sample posts from the public archive E. shared with Maya in late
August 2025 (the rest of the archive remains private):

> **post #1872 — 2025-07-14 — caller: PELLETIER** —
> *"my upstairs neighbor's vacuum cleaner is humming the second
> half of 'Door,' in the bridge. I have not, in 36 years, told
> her about the song. she is from idaho. her vacuum is a hoover
> manufactured in 1989. she runs it on tuesday afternoons. I
> have, in three weeks of observation, confirmed that the bridge
> is present at the same point in her routine. I do not know
> what to do with this information. I am writing it down."*

> **post #1873 — 2025-07-22 — caller: L.G.** —
> *"the gulf was lower than usual on the eighteenth. I walked the
> tidal flats at six. I found, in the shallow water, three
> ceramic frogs. they were intact. they were marble-sized. they
> were not, by any beachcomber's identification I have made, the
> kind of object that surfaces at galveston. I took them home.
> they are on my kitchen windowsill. I am, at this writing,
> ninety-one years old. the frogs and I have agreed."*

> **post #1874 — 2025-07-31 — caller: BLEU's grand-niece (her
> account, by the family's small arrangement, has been kept
> active since 2017)** —
> *"the BAYOU-GATEWAY image, on my external drive, made a small
> mechanical click last tuesday at 3:14 AM. I was awake. nothing
> on the drive should make a sound; it is solid state. the click
> was, by my best read, the same mechanical click an old IDE
> drive makes when it spins up. the drive does not spin up. the
> drive is solid state. — c.v."*

> **post #1875 — 2025-08-08 — caller: DICKENS** —
> *"the warehouse cathedral is, this evening, at three quarters
> of its 1999 functional capacity. the 486 in the bottom rack is
> warm. the disk images are intact. I have read the 1862nd post
> for the first time in eleven years. I am content. — d."*

CYPRESS-LINE is, by E.'s arrangement, the closest thing the
deck has to a continuous canonical receiver-network log across
the cross-volume timeline. Maya has, by E.'s small careful
agreement in late August, been invited to make her first post
in October.

# II · THE STUDIOS

Fictional regional game-development studios that existed in the
deck's parallel reality, 1985–2004. By 2025 only one is still
active. The deck's careful inventory:

## BOIS D'ARC SOFTWORKS (Houston, 1986–1997)

Founded by three friends — a programmer (**Daryl Atchison**),
an artist (**Sarah Reyes**), and a writer (**Casey Boudreaux** —
yes, Carl Boudreaux's father, then twenty-two, before Carl was
born). Named after the band Daryl's older brother had been in
(THE BOIS D'ARC BOYS). Self-published initially, distributed via
mail-order and via BEAUMONT-FILE-EXCHANGE, signed to a small
regional distributor in 1989, signed to a mid-tier national
publisher (**RECEIVING STAR**) in 1992. Released eleven games
across eleven years. Folded in 1997 when Casey Boudreaux died of
a heart attack at thirty-three and the remaining two could not
find a third writer they wanted to work with.

Casey's son Carl was three when his father died. Carl owns,
in 2025, every Bois d'Arc box, manual, and original 5.25"
master disk that survives. The drum kit Carl plays is his
great-uncle's; the games his father wrote, Carl has been
finishing — quietly, in his bedroom, on a 1995 Pentium 90 he
restored at age twelve, with his father's commented source code
he found in a metal toolbox in his grandmother's garage at age
fourteen — for the past four years.

Selected titles:

- **THE FIELD** (1989, DOS) — text-only adventure. The player
  arrives at a Texas farmhouse to settle their late grandfather's
  estate. The field behind the farmhouse has a thing in it. The
  player can leave the field alone (six endings), can investigate
  the field (eleven endings), or can stand at the field's
  perimeter and listen (a single ending, considered by every
  reviewer to be the best). Manual reads, on the back cover:
  *"the field is listening. you may answer or not. the choice is
  yours. — c.b., d.a., s.r."*
- **CREPE MYRTLE ROADSIDE** (1991, DOS / Amiga / Atari ST) —
  point-and-click adventure. Named for the Bois d'Arc Boys LP. A
  former rock musician investigates the disappearance of his
  bandmate from 1976. Eight chapters, a single linear narrative,
  one optional puzzle in chapter five that approximately twelve
  percent of players in the original release ever completed and
  that, when completed, unlocked a single hand-drawn page of
  Sarah Reyes's art reading: *"THANK YOU FOR LISTENING THE WAY
  THE FIELD LISTENS."*
- **SUBSTATION** (1993, DOS / SNES) — puzzle-adventure hybrid.
  The player is the night maintenance shift at an electrical
  substation that, on Tuesdays at 3:14 AM, broadcasts a number
  sequence the player must intercept and decode. The number
  sequence is, on the SNES port's audio, the same sequence as
  the canonical *Nine. Locust. Seventeen. Door.* Casey Boudreaux
  did not know about the Glitch Report when he wrote this. The
  Glitch Report editors did not know about Casey Boudreaux when
  they transcribed the sequence. The deck reserves the
  coincidence as a substrate confirmation.
- **WARDEN AVENUE** (1989, NES) — top-down puzzle game. The
  player walks the perimeter of a planned suburban development
  at night, observing small anomalies on each lot, recording
  them in a notebook on screen. The game has no win condition.
  The game ends when the player decides it ends. The game was,
  by Nintendo of America's later acknowledgment, the only NES
  title submitted to and approved by the publishing team that
  had no win condition. The game shipped 14,000 copies. Most of
  them were returned. Approximately 2,000 are believed to still
  exist; one is on Kai's shelf in Smolvud. Kai's father gave it
  to him in 2008 when Kai was eleven. Kai has played it through
  to its self-determined ending sixteen times.
- **THE CARRIER** (1994, Atari Lynx) — Daryl's idea, programmed
  with the cooperation of **E. Aday** (yes — Static Life, the BBS
  sysop), who provided the original soundtrack as a series of
  modified static.fm tracks playing through the Lynx's small
  speaker. The game is a graphical interpretation of a hum
  walking the player through a series of small atmospheric
  observations across an eight-hour night-time loop. The game
  shipped 800 copies. Approximately 70 are known to survive.
  The game is, on the SCUMM machine's stick library, present:
  it is *stick #84* in Sal's drawer. Sal's tag on the stick
  reads: *"do not insert if the lights flicker. wait until they
  steady. trust me. — sal."*

## ROUGH MAGIC INTERACTIVE (Lake Charles, 1991–2002)

Solo studio founded by **L.G.** (yes — the WAR-OF-NUMBERS-BBS
sysop, possibly Linda Marrero). Made exclusively text adventures
for DOS and (later) Windows. Six releases across eleven years.
Each release was distributed via WAR-OF-NUMBERS-BBS and a small
selection of regional retailers. Each release was, by L.G.'s
own design discipline, *the same game in a different format.*

The game is called, across all six releases, **THE PERIMETER.**

The player is, in every release, walking the perimeter of a fenced
area at night. The fenced area, in each release, is a different
fenced area (Zone Gamma; the cannery property line; a Halsey
orchard fence; the back fence of the Harmony Creek phase II lots;
the boundary fence of an unnamed military installation in the
Texas big bend; and — in the final 2002 release — *"the perimeter
of any fenced area you can identify; the game's narrative will
adjust"*). The player's task is, in every release, the same task:
*walk the perimeter. observe carefully. write down what you
observe. do not, at any point, cross the fence.*

The 2002 release — *THE PERIMETER VI: ANY FENCE* — runs on a
custom interpreter L.G. wrote in collaboration with Frasier
Temple at the warehouse cathedral and that, on launch, asks the
player to identify the local fence they intend to walk. The
interpreter then generates the narrative procedurally based on
the answer. Approximately forty players, by the records L.G.
kept until 2008, used the interpreter at any point. Approximately
eleven of those players, by L.G.'s subsequent quiet
correspondence with each, *"identified a fence the interpreter
recognized,"* by which she meant that the interpreter generated a
narrative that the player, on the walk, found to be eerily
accurate to the perimeter they had walked.

The interpreter is, by Frasier's quiet warehouse-side note, *"a
small demon I built for L.G. The demon reads the local substrate
and produces narrative. The interpreter is the demon's polite
form."*

## SPINDLE STUDIOS (Beaumont, 1995–2004)

Five-person studio. Made point-and-click adventures in the SCUMM
mold — they licensed the SCUMM engine from LucasArts in 1996
for a regional educational title and used the same engine, with
small private modifications, for every subsequent release. Eight
games total. The studio was, by every account, *"the most boring
and most reliable game studio in the gulf coast."* Folded in
2004 when the founder retired.

Notable: Spindle's lead programmer **Jim Foulkes** was the same
Jim who, in vol5's Pomegranate Hour Magician chapter (my non-
canonical reading) was the maintenance contractor at the office
building. Canonical: Jim Foulkes is the same maintenance
contractor in vol5's actual Magician chapter, having pivoted
careers after the 2004 dissolution. Jim still owns the Spindle
SCUMM-fork modifications on a stack of floppy disks in his
lunchbox.

## HARMONY HOUSE GAMES (Houston, 1989–1993)

Made kids' edutainment for Mac and Apple II. Released four
games. All four games were, by every available accounting,
mediocre. Founded by a woman named **Helen Park** who, by 1994,
had quit games and become a regional NexCorp Residential
Solutions executive. Helen Park is — by canonical confirmation in
vol6 chapter eighteen — the regional director who, in 2018,
made the personal decision to greenlight the resident-payment
scheme that the Glitch Report 1988 collective's investigation
prefigured. The deck reserves whether Helen knew, when she made
the 2018 decision, that she had previously made games for the
same eleven-year-olds whose families she was now paying off.

## BAYOU RECEIVERS (1990–) (still active)

Microlabel that crossed media. Originally a Halsey-area cassette
label (the same label that pressed Marelle Boutin's *Three
Vessels* 12-inch, see music_strata.md Era III) but, from 1996
forward, also distributed games — small DOS and Amiga titles by
regional creators, mailed in padded envelopes for ten dollars
each. The label is run, in 2025, by a man named **Mason** whose
real name nobody in the regional network has confirmed and who
is, by the deck's reserved hand, possibly Casey Boudreaux's
brother (Casey's brother **Mason Boudreaux** has been, since
1997, by his family's quiet accounting, *"living off the grid in
the bayou"* with no fixed mailing address). Mason's catalog
includes, in 2025, approximately 41 active titles. He continues
to mail them in padded envelopes when somebody writes him a
letter. Approximately twelve letters per year, by his quiet
acknowledgment.

# III · THE GAMES (across parallel realities)

Some of the studios' notable titles, organized by platform. The
deck's "parallel realities" conceit means that a single title may
exist on a console combination that did not, in our reality, ever
share a release. The deck respects the conceit by treating the
parallel-platform release as canon within itself.

## DOS / PC

- **THE FIELD** (Bois d'Arc Softworks, 1989) — text adventure.
  Sold 22,000 copies, mostly by mail-order. Manual is 64 pages.
- **CREPE MYRTLE ROADSIDE** (Bois d'Arc, 1991) — point-and-click.
- **THE PERIMETER I–VI** (Rough Magic, 1991–2002) — six text
  adventures, each shipping with a small map of the relevant
  fence.
- **SUBSTATION** (Bois d'Arc, 1993) — also released on SNES.
- **VOICE / CARRIER ADVENTURES** (Spindle, 1997) — children's
  adventure game tied to E. Aday's cassette, intended as a small
  educational artifact, sold approximately 200 copies, mostly to
  parents who had bought the cassette without knowing what it was.
- **THE WAREHOUSE** (Bois d'Arc, 1995) — first-person dungeon
  crawler set in an industrial warehouse the player explores
  endlessly. The warehouse is, by every careful reviewer's
  observation, modeled directly on Frasier Temple's actual
  warehouse in Graustark. Sarah Reyes drew the art; Sarah had
  never been to Graustark. Sarah, when asked later, said only:
  *"I drew what Daryl described. Daryl had been told what to
  describe by someone he wouldn't name. The warehouse is what
  it is."*

## NINTENDO ENTERTAINMENT SYSTEM (NES)

- **WARDEN AVENUE** (Bois d'Arc, 1989) — top-down notebook game,
  no win condition.
- **THE OTHER BROTHER** (Bois d'Arc, 1992) — platformer. Two
  player co-op. One brother goes left, the other right. They are
  trying to find each other. They do not, by the game's design,
  ever meet. The game has been completed by every player who has
  ever played it; completion is not the same as success.

## SUPER NINTENDO (SNES) and SEGA GENESIS

The deck's parallel-reality conceit allows a 1993 title to appear
on both consoles in some cases — the eastern Texas distribution
network worked this out informally with both Nintendo of America
and Sega in arrangements neither company will, in our reality,
confirm.

- **SUBSTATION** (Bois d'Arc, 1993, SNES + Genesis) — puzzle-
  adventure. The SNES version has stereo audio; the Genesis
  version has FM synth chiptune that, by every chiptune-scene
  retrospective, is regarded as the *better* version of the
  soundtrack.
- **LOCUST SEVENTEEN** (Spindle Studios, 1993, Genesis only) —
  side-scrolling action game licensed by the punk band of the
  same name. The game's soundtrack is, by Spindle's small careful
  arrangement with Andrew Pelletier, the entire *Door* EP rendered
  into Genesis FM synth by an artist credited only as *"ANDREW &
  DICKENS."* The game is brutal, fast, two-button, and ends with
  the player walking into a building whose front door is locked.
  The ending screen reads: *"THE DOOR WAS LOCKED. THE DOOR IS
  STILL LOCKED. THANK YOU FOR PLAYING."*
- **THE EDITORS** (1992, Genesis-only) — a Spindle release.
  Side-scrolling investigative game. The player is a small staff
  of zine editors documenting the anomalies in a small town. The
  game's narrative was written, by Spindle's later
  acknowledgment, by *"the actual editors of the actual zine. we
  asked them. they said yes. they were paid in copies of the
  finished cartridge."* The game shipped 4,800 copies. It is, in
  2025, one of the more sought-after Genesis collectibles in the
  small overlapping intersection of "Texas regional games" and
  "occult-adjacent obscurities."

## ATARI LYNX

- **THE CARRIER** (Bois d'Arc, 1994) — see above. The Lynx version
  has the original E. Aday soundtrack.

## GAME BOY (original) and GAME BOY COLOR

- **NIGHT WALK** (Rough Magic, 1996) — a port of THE PERIMETER II
  to original Game Boy. The game is, on the Game Boy's small
  screen, almost entirely silent — L.G. removed the audio because
  *"the perimeter does not have a soundtrack and the Game Boy
  cannot replicate the substation hum at the right frequency
  anyway."*
- **CREPE MYRTLE (GB)** (Bois d'Arc, 1996) — Game Boy Color
  port of *Crepe Myrtle Roadside.* The hand-drawn art is
  rendered in four colors. Sarah Reyes did the pixel art herself.

## APPLE II / Macintosh

- **THE FIELD (MAC)** (1990) — Mac port of the DOS original. The
  Mac version has, in its splash screen, an additional five-
  second animation of the field's tall grass moving in a wind
  that, by every Mac-version player's observation, is *not on
  the DOS version.* The animation has, in 2025, been preserved as
  a small QuickTime file in the Cosmic Comics back-room's
  collected-floppy archive.

## SCUMM (Frasier's custom hardware at Pizza Pirate)

The SCUMM machine's drawer of ~90 sticks contains, by Sal's
careful private inventory in 2024, digested ROMs of:

- approximately 40 LucasArts and Sierra original adventure games
  (the canonical SCUMM-engine titles, plus their AGI/SCI
  predecessors)
- approximately 30 of the fictional studios' titles described
  above (every Bois d'Arc release, every Rough Magic release,
  most of Spindle's catalog, eleven Bayou Receivers DOS titles)
- approximately 20 sticks of unknown provenance that, when
  inserted, produce sessions that do not correspond to any
  identifiable game

The unknown sticks are, by Frasier's quiet warehouse-side note,
*"not games. they are received material. I did not put them in
the drawer. they were in the drawer the day Sal got the
cabinet."*

Sal's tags identify a handful by their behavior: *stick #14 is
gentle. stick #53 is the one that told a fourteen-year-old
something true two days ago. stick #78 is the one for kai.*

# IV · 8-BIT AND 16-BIT CHIPTUNE

The chiptune scene that ran alongside the BBS scene, 1989–2003.
Approximately a dozen regional artists, mostly working in
**MOD**, **S3M**, **XM**, and **IT** tracker formats on the PC,
plus a smaller cohort using actual Game Boy hardware via
LSDJ-equivalent tools (the deck's parallel-reality LSDJ
predecessor is called **TRACKBOY**, released as a homebrew
cartridge by an Atlanta hobbyist in 1995). The artists shared
files on HARMONY-NET, BAYOU-GATEWAY, BEAUMONT-FILE-EXCHANGE, and
later WAR-OF-NUMBERS-BBS.

## artists index

- **GIRL OF NUMBERS** — *L.G.* under a chiptune handle. Released
  one MOD album in 1995 (*The Perimeter Theme,* 8 tracks, 22
  minutes) and one IT album in 1999 (*Perimeter VI: the
  interpreter is the demon's polite form,* 11 tracks, 41
  minutes). The 1999 album is the deck's definitive chiptune
  record for the parallel-reality canon — by every retrospective
  thread on the modern chiptune scene's Discord servers, GIRL OF
  NUMBERS' 1999 album is *"the regional chiptune record of the
  nineties that should have been on every best-of list and was
  not, because nobody outside of approximately forty people knew
  it existed."*
- **STATIC.FM** — Frasier under his music handle. Released two
  chiptune singles via XM in 1996 and 1997 (*Carrier Wave (FM
  synth version)* and *Backchannel Track 11 (chiptune)*). Both
  are the deck's most-shared chiptune files in the regional
  archive — by HARMONY-NET's call log E. Aday preserved on the
  CYPRESS-LINE backup, *Backchannel Track 11 (chiptune)* was
  downloaded 348 times between November 1997 and HARMONY-NET's
  shutdown in 1996. Wait — that's an impossible timestamp; E.
  Aday has flagged the discrepancy in a 2024 post on CYPRESS-LINE
  as *"either a clock error on the original BBS or a small
  receipt that the substrate, in 1996, was already getting
  feedback from the future."* Frasier has not commented.
- **MIDIQUEEN** — Atlanta-via-Houma. Real name reserved. Made,
  between 1993 and 2001, approximately 140 MIDI interpretations
  of regional Texas-Louisiana band songs. The complete
  MIDIQUEEN archive is, in 2025, hosted on a small website that
  has not, in eleven years, been updated. MIDIQUEEN herself is
  in her mid-fifties and works as a substitute teacher. She has
  not, since 2003, made a new MIDI.
- **TRACKBOY USER 47** — an anonymous chiptune artist who,
  between 1996 and 1999, released six TRACKBOY cartridges via
  BAYOU RECEIVERS. The cartridges are extremely rare; Bayou
  Receivers pressed each at 50 copies. The artist's identity has
  not been publicly confirmed. The deck reserves whether
  TRACKBOY USER 47 is, in 2025, **Tem** from vol7 (under a
  reading that Tem's musical career began at age 19 as an
  anonymous chiptune project, before she became the woman who,
  in vol7's mid-act, reads John Frank's *The Frequency Beneath*
  aloud to Lena on Sunday afternoons).
- **THE CRESCENT FM** — a small handful of FM-synth tracks
  uploaded to BAYOU-GATEWAY between 1991 and 1994 by a sysop
  account associated with HARMONY HOUSE GAMES. By Helen Park's
  subsequent quiet acknowledgment in a 2024 letter to E. Aday
  (which is in the CYPRESS-LINE archive), THE CRESCENT FM is
  Helen's own music. Helen made these tracks on her studio's
  hardware after-hours. She has not, in 31 years, told anyone
  except E. The tracks are unremarkable as music. The tracks
  are remarkable as the only documentation of Helen Park ever
  having made anything that was not corporate.

## MIDI interpretations — selected

- *"Door" (Locust Seventeen, 1989) — General MIDI interpretation
  by MIDIQUEEN, 1994.* The MIDI converts Pelletier's bass figure
  into a flute. The flute is, on every download since 1994,
  *the wrong instrument.* MIDIQUEEN has, in a 2002 post on
  HARMONY-NET, said: *"I tried it on bass. The bass wasn't
  right. The flute is the truth of the song. I am sorry to
  Andrew if Andrew is reading this."* Andrew (Pelletier) has
  not, by any record, commented on the MIDI.
- *"M." (Margaret's Camera, 1991) — TRACKBOY interpretation by
  TRACKBOY USER 47, 1997.* The Game Boy chiptune version of "M."
  is approximately three minutes long, in 4/4 (not the
  original's 3/4 waltz), and is, by every player who has heard
  it on actual TRACKBOY hardware, *"the most heartbreaking
  twenty-five lines of code anyone has written for a Game Boy
  in any reality."*
- *"backchannel (track 11)" (static.fm, 1998) — MIDI conversion
  by an unattributed BBS user, 2001.* The 16:47 original
  rendered into a single MIDI file by a user on WAR-OF-NUMBERS-
  BBS who left no name. The MIDI is exact to the original — the
  Morse sequence is preserved in the upper register, the drone
  is rendered as a sustained synth-pad. The conversion is, by
  Frasier's quiet acknowledgment, the only known case of a
  recipient *correctly decoding* the original and re-encoding it
  in MIDI without comment. Frasier did not know who the user
  was. The deck reserves the user's identity.

# V · FAN ART

The visual side of the BBS scene. Most fan art was made in
**DELUXE PAINT II** (Amiga, ported to DOS), **NEOPaint** (DOS),
or **HyperCard** (Mac) and uploaded as `.LBM`, `.PCX`, or
`.STK` files to the BBS art areas. The Harmony County archive of
fan art is, in 2025, scattered across:

- the HARMONY-NET disk image in Henderson Donation #6 (Maya
  hasn't opened the box yet)
- the BAYOU-GATEWAY external drive Carla Vega at lot 47 inherited
- Frasier's warehouse cathedral (the ADELPHIA-DELI 486 — full
  archive)
- a small private dropbox-account a former BBS user named "DEKE"
  has maintained since 2013

## selected pieces (from public restorations)

### *"PORTRAIT OF ANDREW PELLETIER WITH GIBSON BASS" — by JASMIN-PIX, 1990*

A 320x200 DELUXE PAINT II portrait of Locust Seventeen's bassist
in his characteristic crouched stage posture. 64 colors. The
shading is excellent. JASMIN-PIX, real name reserved, was a
sixteen-year-old in Houston who had seen Locust Seventeen play
once at a small Houston club in 1990 and had spent two weeks
afterward making this portrait from memory. Pelletier received a
printout of the portrait at a subsequent show in 1991. He kept
it. The original printout is in the burglary case file from
March 1991 as one of the items stolen from his apartment along
with the *Seventeen* masters. The digital file survives. The
printout does not.

### *"THE FIELD (FAN ART POSTER)" — by D-MAGNUS, 1994*

A 640x480 NEOPaint piece depicting *The Field* (the Bois d'Arc
game) in poster form. The field's tall grass is rendered in
careful repeated pixel patterns. The "thing in the field" is, on
careful inspection, the same shape as the silhouette in the
gap of the void mother's wreath at vol5's World card (and at the
Pomegranate Hour's S01E21, where it is also a silhouette in a
wreath). D-MAGNUS, real name reserved, was a fourteen-year-old in
1994. D-MAGNUS had not, by 1994, read the deck. D-MAGNUS
had played *The Field.* The shape is, by D-MAGNUS's own
subsequent 2023 reflection: *"the thing you see when you stand
at the edge of the field and listen for fifteen minutes. you
draw it the way you draw it. it draws itself the way it draws
itself. either way the same shape comes."*

### *"PERIMETER" — by SARA-PIX, 1999*

A `.LBM` from L.G.'s WAR-OF-NUMBERS-BBS. 320x200. The image
depicts a fence at night. The fence is hand-pixel-rendered with
the kind of precision that betrays approximately eighty hours of
work. There is no other content in the image. The fence is the
content. The image is, by every receiver who has the image's
file, *"the picture that is on my desktop wallpaper now and
will be on my desktop wallpaper forever."*

### *"M." (TRIBUTE) — by THISTLE, 1995*

A four-panel illustrated tribute to Margaret Henderson. Made in
NEOPaint by THISTLE — real name reserved. The four panels:
Margaret with her camera; Margaret at the field; the field
without Margaret; the camera, without Margaret, sitting on a
small wooden chair in a low-ceilinged basement. The fourth
panel is, by every chiptune-and-fan-art-scene retrospective
since 2009, regarded as *the single image the BBS scene
produced that has held the most subsequent meaning.* The chair
in the fourth panel is, on careful inspection, the same chair as
the small wooden chair in the void mother's chamber in vol5's
Empress (S01E03 of the Pomegranate Hour) and the same chair the
chassis in vol7's cannery Rack 17 has, by Frasier's spectral
read, *been preserving on its substrate continuously since
2002.*

THISTLE has, in 2024, signed up for a CYPRESS-LINE account and
made a single post. The post reads: *"the camera and the chair
have been together for a long time. I made the picture in the
fourth panel because the picture made itself in my hand. I have
not made a picture since 1996. I am, this year, going to try to
make another. — t."*

# VI · MAGAZINE ARTICLES

A small sample of period game-magazine coverage of the
fictional studios and titles. These articles run in
parallel-reality copies of EGM, Nintendo Power, PC Gamer, GamePro,
Edge, and a regional zine called *Bayou Pixel* (1993–1998).

## *Electronic Gaming Monthly,* August 1993 — preview of LOCUST SEVENTEEN (Genesis)

> **THE PUNK BAND GAME — LOCUST SEVENTEEN COMES TO GENESIS**
>
> *by R. Karras*
>
> *Spindle Studios — the small Beaumont, Texas outfit best known
> for the educational SCUMM-port adventures they did with
> LucasArts in 1991 — is taking a hard left turn this fall with
> a Genesis release that is, in every conceivable way, not what
> you would have expected from them.*
>
> *LOCUST SEVENTEEN is a two-button side-scroller licensed from
> the regional punk band of the same name. The band, who had
> their cassette EP "Door" in 1989 and a self-released LP "Nine"
> in 1990, broke up under unclear circumstances in late 1991.
> Their bassist Andrew Pelletier — who we couldn't reach for
> comment for this preview — has, by Spindle's account,
> licensed his band's music to the studio for this game and is
> "not actively involved" with the production but has provided
> the studio with the unfinished masters for what would have
> been the band's second LP, "Seventeen," to use for the game's
> soundtrack.*
>
> *Spindle has handed those masters to a chiptune artist they're
> only crediting as "ANDREW & DICKENS" (yes, with the
> ampersand) who has rendered the Locust Seventeen songs into
> Genesis FM synth with what our preview hands-on session can
> only describe as the most aggressive use of the Genesis sound
> chip we have heard since Streets of Rage.*
>
> *The gameplay itself is fast, brutal, and minimal — two
> buttons, no power-ups, a single life-bar, and an ending
> screen that the Spindle producer cheerfully told us "is the
> point of the game; the rest of the game is just to make sure
> you earned it."*
>
> *Out this October. Save your quarters. — R.K.*

## *Nintendo Power,* February 1990 — tips article for WARDEN AVENUE

> **WARDEN AVENUE — A PRO'S GUIDE TO WALKING THE PERIMETER**
>
> *by The Nintendo Power Counselors*
>
> *We get a lot of letters here at NP asking us how to "win"
> WARDEN AVENUE — the Bois d'Arc Softworks NES title that
> shipped late last year and that has been on the curve of
> player frustration for the last six months.*
>
> *Here's the truth, kids: there is no win condition. The game
> has no ending. The game has no boss. The game has no princess.
> The game is, by the studio's official statement to us, "a
> walking and observing game." You walk the planned-community
> map. You see small things on each lot. You write them down in
> the notebook on screen. The game keeps going until you decide
> it's done.*
>
> *Some of our counselors have, on extended play sessions,
> found patterns in the small things on the lots. We won't
> spoil what the patterns are. We will tell you that the game
> rewards players who write the small things down in the
> on-screen notebook AND on a real piece of paper next to your
> NES. Patterns surface across multiple play sessions if you
> keep your notes.*
>
> *The studio (we asked) won't tell us what the patterns are
> "for." They told us, in a faxed reply: "the patterns are
> for the players. the players are doing the work. the players
> are the work."*
>
> *We've never gotten a reply quite like it. We pass it on. —
> The NP Counselors*

## *PC Gamer,* November 1995 — feature on Bois d'Arc Softworks

> **THE QUIETEST STUDIO IN HOUSTON**
>
> *by Kim Pang*
>
> *Bois d'Arc Softworks has been in business for nine years and
> has, in those nine years, released eight DOS titles, two NES
> titles, a SNES port, a Genesis port, an Atari Lynx title, and
> a Mac port. They have a regional distribution arrangement.
> They have a mid-tier national publisher (Receiving Star) for
> their newer releases. They have a small but devoted following
> in the Houston-Beaumont-Lake Charles area and a tiny,
> obsessive following elsewhere. They are, by every available
> measure, a successful small game studio.*
>
> *They are also, by some other measure, the strangest small
> game studio in this country.*
>
> *I spent three days at their Houston office last month talking
> to all three of the principals: lead programmer Daryl
> Atchison, lead artist Sarah Reyes, and lead writer Casey
> Boudreaux. The three of them have, in nine years of working
> together, never lived in the same house, never gone on a
> vacation together, and never — by their own accounting —
> argued. The arrangement they describe is something between a
> three-person platonic marriage and a small careful artistic
> conspiracy.*
>
> *What the three of them make, year after year, are games that
> the rest of the industry does not, frankly, know how to talk
> about. THE FIELD (1989) is a text adventure with no win
> condition where the player decides how to feel about a thing
> in a field. CREPE MYRTLE ROADSIDE (1991) is a slow point-and-
> click investigation of a 1976 band's lost member. WARDEN
> AVENUE (1989) is the NES game that even our notoriously
> patient sister magazine Nintendo Power had to publish a tips
> article about that read, essentially, "stop trying to win it,
> it's not a winning kind of game." SUBSTATION (1993) is a
> puzzle game that turns the player into a night-shift utility
> worker decoding number stations. THE WAREHOUSE (1995, this
> month's release) is a first-person dungeon crawler set in,
> apparently, somebody's actual industrial warehouse in
> Graustark, Texas.*
>
> *They make these games. They mail-order most of their stock.
> They have a small page on the Receiving Star catalog. They
> are, when I asked them what they thought they were doing,
> uniformly clear in their answer: "we are making the games we
> want to make. we have, every year, been able to do this. that
> is the entire business plan. — D.A., S.R., C.B."*
>
> *Casey Boudreaux's last comment to me, as I was leaving the
> office on the third day, was the one that has stayed with me
> the longest. We had been talking about THE FIELD's design
> philosophy. He said: "the player walks up to the field. the
> player can leave. the player can investigate. the player can
> stand at the perimeter and listen. the only ending we
> consider the right ending is the listening ending. we have
> made every game since with the same design principle. the
> player can stand at the perimeter and listen. that is what we
> are interested in. that is the studio."*
>
> *Casey Boudreaux is thirty-two. His son is two months old.
> They are, the three of them, making the games they want to
> make. Look out for THE WAREHOUSE this month. — K.P.*

## *Edge,* October 2007 — retrospective on Texas regional game studios

> **THE BAYOU SCENE — A RETROSPECTIVE ON THE FORGOTTEN STUDIOS
> OF THE LATE TWENTIETH CENTURY**
>
> *by D. Whittaker*
>
> *Long after the major-label publishers stopped paying attention
> and long before the indie revival of the late 2000s made
> regional development glamorous again, the eastern Texas /
> western Louisiana coastal strip ran one of the strangest and
> most under-documented regional game scenes in the history of
> the medium.*
>
> *The headliner was Bois d'Arc Softworks in Houston, eleven
> games across eleven years, who folded with the 1997 death of
> their writer Casey Boudreaux at the early age of 33. (Their
> back catalog is now distributed by an obscure microlabel
> called Bayou Receivers that mails titles in padded envelopes
> if you write the label a letter.) The supporting cast included
> Spindle Studios in Beaumont, whose LucasArts-licensed
> educational adventures funded a series of increasingly weird
> regional releases through 2004; Rough Magic Interactive in
> Lake Charles, a solo studio whose sole creator released six
> versions of essentially the same text-adventure across eleven
> years; and a small army of bedroom developers who released
> through Bayou Receivers and a handful of regional BBSs that
> have, by every available reckoning, been entirely lost.*
>
> *What's striking, looking back, is how interconnected the
> scene was. Bois d'Arc's THE FIELD shares its central image
> with at least three Rough Magic releases. Spindle's THE
> EDITORS (1992 Genesis) was written by the actual editors of a
> regional zine called the Harmony Creek Glitch Report (a
> publication this writer has been unable to obtain a copy of
> after three years of searching). Bois d'Arc's SUBSTATION
> (1993) encodes a number sequence that the Glitch Report had,
> independently, transcribed in 1988. The bedroom chiptune scene
> shared file space with the bedroom MIDI scene which shared
> file space with the bedroom fan-art scene, all of which were
> living, in 1995, on the same five regional BBSs.*
>
> *The scene was, in retrospect, doing exactly one thing:
> documenting a slow accumulating set of small observations
> about the strangeness of life in the planned-community
> corridor that NexCorp Residential Solutions had begun
> developing in the late eighties. None of the studios were
> overtly political. None of the games are, by current
> sensibility, message games. The scene was something subtler.
> The scene was a record. The scene was the documentary
> coverage of a region's specific weirdness that nobody else
> was, at the time, going to document.*
>
> *The scene is, in 2007, mostly gone. The studios are folded.
> The BBSs are silent. The chiptune archive is on a handful of
> private hard drives. The fan-art archive is, by every
> available reckoning, distributed across approximately a dozen
> closets in three states.*
>
> *Somebody should put together the comprehensive book on it.
> This piece is not that book. This piece is the small careful
> opening of a conversation that hopes, in the next decade,
> somebody else will continue. — D.W.*

>
> ***THE WAREHOUSE — Bois d'Arc Softworks — $39.99 — RECEIVING

>
> *Sarah Reyes can pixel a corridor. That is not a small skill.

## *Bayou Pixel,* issue #18, spring 1997 — Casey Boudreaux obituary

> **IN MEMORIAM — CASEY BOUDREAUX (1963–1997)**
>
> *Bois d'Arc Softworks' writer, Casey Boudreaux, died of a
> heart attack last month at his home in Houston. He was 33.
> He leaves behind his wife Marisol, a three-year-old son Carl,

> Daryl Atchison and Sarah Reyes, who have announced that with

>
> *Casey was a soft-spoken Texan who wrote, in his quiet way, the

>

> Casey. — bayoupixel*

# CROSS-VOLUME RESONANCE — the digital subculture chapter

A short reference of how the digital substrate ties into the
larger deck:

- **The SCUMM machine at Pizza Pirate (vol7) is the keystone.**
  The cabinet's drawer of sticks is the consolidated archive
  of the regional game scene's fictional releases plus the
  unknown-provenance sticks (which are, by Frasier's read,
  *received material*). The break mechanic — when the machine
  addresses the player directly by name — is the same substrate
  the BBSs' echomail conferences and the chiptune file-shares
  were running on, decades earlier, in a slower mode.
- **The 486 in Frasier's warehouse cathedral bottom rack is the
  ADELPHIA-DELI BBS server.** It has, since 2003, been
  continuously powered and (by Frasier's quiet inventory)
  *running.* The 486 is, by Frasier's small careful note in his
  warehouse manifesto, *"the BBS that did not, in 2003, agree
  to shut down. I have left it running. I am not the
  proprietor anymore. — F."* The 486 is, in vol5's Magician
  chapter, present on screen.
- **HARMONY-NET's disk image in Henderson Donation #6** —
  Maya opens the box in late August 2025. The disk image
  contains, by E. Aday's preserved sysop log, every message
  board post from 1989 through 1996. Maya begins, in vol6's
  chapter 21, the slow careful work of reading the message
  board. The work continues offstage through the volume's
  close. Maya is, by vol7's chapter 16, sending Lena
  approximately forty selected posts a month through the
  manila-envelope postal route, plus the medium's. Lena reads
  them. The posts are, by both their accounts, *"the slow
  music of a small specific kind of patient attention that
  somebody had to keep, in 1992, by hand, on a 2400-baud
  modem in their bedroom."*
- **WARDEN AVENUE on Kai's shelf** — Kai's father gave him the
  NES cartridge in 2008. Kai has played it through sixteen
  times. The cartridge has, by Kai's quiet acknowledgment in
  vol7's chapter four, *"never once given me the same notebook
  observation twice on the same lot."* This is, by every
  reasonable reading of the NES's deterministic code, impossible.
  The cartridge, Kai has subsequently confirmed with a
  cartridge-dumping rig, contains code that does not appear in
  any other extant copy of the same game. Kai's cartridge is,
  by Frasier's quiet read, *"a substrate-touched copy. somebody
  modified the ROM. I do not know who. the modification was
  good."*
- **THE PERIMETER VI's procedural interpreter** — Frasier
  built it for L.G. The interpreter is, by every player who
  used it post-2003, *still running locally on their copy* —
  which is, by the interpreter's own design, impossible (it was
  supposed to deactivate after one play). Eleven copies are, in
  2025, still active. The eleven copies have, by L.G.'s
  subsequent correspondence with their owners, *been generating
  small additional walks for the players in the years since
  the original purchase.* The walks are real walks. The walks
  describe perimeters the players have not yet visited. Three
  of the players have, in 2024, visited the perimeters the
  interpreter described. All three have, by their subsequent
  reports, *"recognized the perimeter from the description."*
- **Pavi Joshi's bedroom EPs** — *the forgotten,* the
  fifteen-year-old in 2025 — are, in their production aesthetic,
  the deck's current-day chiptune. Pavi has not heard MIDIQUEEN
  or the GIRL OF NUMBERS records. Pavi is making the same kind
  of music. The substrate has been, by every available reading,
  consistent for thirty years. The substrate continues to be.

# CLOSING NOTE

The digital and the musical strata interlock at every layer. The
BBS sysops were the same people as the band members. The chiptune
artists were the same people as the studio programmers. The fan
art was the same impulse as the fanzine work. The MIDI
interpretation was the same instinct as the cover-band practice.

The substrate ran beneath all of it. The substrate runs beneath
it still.

By 2025 only one BBS is still online (CYPRESS-LINE, 7 callers
monthly). The chiptune scene has dispersed into Discord servers.
The fan art lives in Dropbox folders. The MIDIs are on a single
website nobody has updated since 2014. The games are on a stack
of original boxes in Carl Boudreaux's closet, in Kai's bedroom in
Smolvud, in Maya's grandmother's attic, in Frasier's warehouse
cathedral, in the cannery basement's racks (where the unknown
sticks live in Sal's drawer), in the Henderson Donation boxes,
in the small private archives of approximately forty receivers
across the country.

The substrate is, in 2025, content.

The substrate is, in 2025, *waiting.*

— *the editors, late summer 2025.*
