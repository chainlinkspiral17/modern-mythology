# The D'Ambrosio employment register

A worldbuilding constraint that reframes the deck's class
politics: many of vol5's characters worked for the D'Ambrosio
family at some point. Some still do. Some did and were
discarded. Some still are and don't realize they're being
discarded right now.

## The premise

The D'Ambrosio family is **the employer**. The river-front
empire (barge, restaurant chain, render farm, bar real estate,
PetroTex holdings, the diner that bears the name) is also a
labor market. The family gave jobs to many in need. The family
was also terrible to a great many of them.

The two registers are not in tension — they are the **same
gesture at different scales**. The same paternalism that
employed Marta Romero as housekeeper twenty years ago is the
paternalism that moved the dates on her PetroTex deposition.
The same generosity that gave Daigle a no-interest loan to open
his bar is the generosity that expects Daigle to take Antonio's
3 AM calls forever. Loyalty extracted is also loyalty given.
The D'Ambrosios are complicated villains because their villainy
is also their patronage.

## Alberto's arc

**Alberto learns to be a better person after Dante's death and
Antonio's.** This is the deck's most important character arc
beat — not in vol5 (where Alberto is still in the middleman
register, complicit and competent), but in the implied trajectory
that vol5 sets up for vol6+.

The death of the elder son and the father is the death of the
generation Alberto inherited. The empire he ends up with is also
a debt-ledger of people his family hurt. Alberto begins —
slowly, awkwardly, with mistakes — to make amends.

**Vol5 evidence of the arc beginning:**
- The 22-25 minute callback window Alberto times for his father
  is also a son's discipline of attention to grief.
- Alberto's red-ink marginalia in his case file is increasingly
  honest, increasingly searching. He is annotating a record he
  will eventually have to act on.
- The 19 calls to Daigle the night Antonio dies are also the
  first time Alberto chose his brother over the family's image.
  He didn't dial discreetly; he dialed nineteen times. He didn't
  know he was practicing for the rest of his life.
- The 23 Feb 4-second call he won't write down — vol6 hook —
  may be the call that breaks him. Or the call that begins to.

## The employment register, per card

For each vol5 character, the relationship to the D'Ambrosio
employment register. Some are direct (still working there). Some
are former (worked there, left, complicated). Some are
indirect (family member of an employee). Some are adversarial
(adversary of an employer who once employed them).

### 0 — JOHN FRANK (the Fool)

**Direct.** John has worked at D'Ambrosio's diner — the family's
namesake restaurant — for an unspecified long time. The painted
chapter's "between acts" register is also the register of an
employee who has not been promoted, has not been fired, has not
quit in 12+ years. The diner is the family's most visible labor
market: the family puts their name on it; the workers absorb
the name. John is the deck's longest-serving D'Ambrosio
employee in vol5.

The bindle is also a severance package the diner has not yet
asked for. The painted clock at 3:47 AM is the wage clock of
the deck's quietest exploitation: he is always there, he is
always being paid the same wage, the family does not
particularly know his name.

→ Alberto's vol6 arc may include knowing his name.

### I — FRASIER TEMPLE (the Magician)

**Former-and-still-contracted.** Frasier was a junior engineer
for D'Ambrosio's industrial operations in his early career.
He left to start his own workshop (the Cathedral of Rust and
Code), but his model city includes warehouses 47, the
steamboat, the render farm — all family infrastructure. He is
a former employee whose freelance practice depends on the
family's continuing patronage.

The model is also his rendering of the system he left. Frasier
is the deck's portrait of the employee who quit and could not
quit; his lemniscate is a worker's discipline rendered as art.

### II — ELICIA (the High Priestess)

**Former and adversarial.** Elicia worked in the D'Ambrosio
archive — the family's records office at the main estate —
when she was younger. She left when she realized what she
was archiving. Her current practice as archivist of the
chapter's own tapes is a parallel-archive: she keeps records
the family cannot reach. The Anya tapes are partly her
shadow-records of the family's outside.

The compass badge on her painted card ("HIGH PRIESTESS Node")
reads in the employment register as her former office stamp,
kept and recontextualized as her own.

### III — NICOLA (the Empress)

**Married into the family. Was not employed by them; they
employed her family.** Nicola's people worked for the
D'Ambrosios in some capacity (vol6 — possibly cleaning, possibly
catering, possibly orchard work). She married Antonio because
the marriage was good for her people's standing with the
family. Aria's anxiety-spike at the painted dinner is partly
the anxiety of a woman who watched her family bow to the
people whose son she now sleeps with.

### IV — DANTE D'AMBROSIO (the Emperor)

**THE EMPLOYER.** Dante is the head of the household and the
head of the labor market. His Wednesday calendar slot at 09:00
("barge — manifest review with quentin") is the working slot
of an employer doing his weekly review. The 21:30 boat-alone
slot is the slot of an employer who cannot stop thinking about
the labor that funds his solitude.

His "alma (private)" slot is reserved (vol6). Alma may be a
former employee. Alma may be a long-term liaison the family
has not legitimized. Alma may be someone the family hurt and
Dante is paying for, weekly, without writing it down.

### V — FATHER QUENT (the Hierophant)

**Adjacent. The family friend, not the employee.** Quent
ministers to the family. He officiated Antonio's wedding to
Nicola. He has confirmed at least one D'Ambrosio child. He is
not on payroll; he is on family. The distinction is partly
true (he answers to the diocese) and partly fictional (the
diocese answers to the D'Ambrosio donor records).

The painted chapter's AVDI ET TACE is partly the clergy's
working rule for what to do when the family confesses.

### VI — THE LOVERS (—)

**Refusal includes refusal of the employment register.** The
chapter's face-down stance refuses to render the family's
relationship to labor; the lattice of pairs has Nicola&Dante
(succession) but does not name how the throne extracts that
succession from a woman whose family was the cleaning staff.

The refused card is the card that won't say.

### VII — ANTONIO (the Chariot)

**The heir, the manager, the boss.** Antonio managed several
of the family's day-to-day labor relationships. Daigle's bar
got start-up money from him personally (not from Dante);
Ember & Ash Restaurant was Antonio's project (the SCAFFOLDING
VIP section was a paid-VIP scheme Antonio engineered to
recoup the build-out). Antonio was the family's friendly
face to the workers Dante did not want to know personally.

The chapter's wreck is the wreck of the friendly face. The
workers who knew Antonio (Daigle, Jimmy the saboteur, the
restaurant staff) lose the family member they could ask
favors of. Dante remains.

### VIII — STRENGTH (un-named)

**Former employee — likely a riverside contractor or
horse-handler.** The un-named woman has a working relationship
to the river the family uses; she is on the far bank from the
empire by choice. Her dash-signature in her notes may be the
signature she stopped using when she stopped working for them.

The lion is also a managed beast — the gentle hand on the hot
neck is the labor-of-care she did for the family at one point,
now done for the creature instead.

### IX — THE HERMIT (un-named)

**Former employee, possibly senior.** The Hermit may be a
retired family lawyer or a retired family accountant who walked
away. The crozier-shaped staff is the wood of the family's
office furniture, in private use. His chosen solitude is the
solitude of someone who knows enough to leave quietly.

(Or — vol6 hook — the Hermit is the void mother's brother. He
walked away when his sister disappeared.)

### X — ALBERTO (the Wheel)

**The younger son, the survivor, the eventual reformer.** In
vol5, Alberto is in the family's middleman position — he
shuttles paperwork, he carries messages, he annotates files.
The Wheel chapter renders his case-prep nights at the firm.

The redemption arc seeded in vol5: every red-ink marginalia
note grows slightly more honest. By vol5 close, Alberto is
practicing — without naming it — the discipline of caring
about the people his family used. Vol6+ will pay this off.

### XI — JUSTICE (Erica + Anna)

**Erica adversarial. Anna's mother (Marta) was the
employee.** Erica's firm is increasingly opposed to the
family — PetroTex v. Romero is one of multiple cases. Anna's
mother, MARTA ROMERO, was the D'Ambrosio family's
housekeeper for ~20 years. Anna's father did the boat work
(per Marta's photo-reverse letter). The PetroTex deposition is
a deposition by a former family employee about the family's
practices; the family moved the dates to discredit her.

The chapter's shattered scales are also the scales of a
two-decade loyalty broken by paperwork.

### XII — THE HANGED MAN (Natalie)

**Possibly former employee (vol6).** Natalie has a hospitality
or production background. Whether she was employed by the
family directly or by an Antonio-managed venture (Ember & Ash,
or the family's catering arm) is reserved. Her Hanged Man
chapter is set in Simon's apartment in LA, far from the river-
front — possibly because Simon got her out.

(Simon may have been an Antonio friend who helped Natalie
relocate after a workplace incident. Vol6 hook.)

### XIII — DEATH (Ward C)

**The ward is half-populated by former family employees.**
The deck's quietest census:

- **Bed 1 — Mrs. P, 78.** Possibly the family's longtime
  housekeeper before Marta took over. The family's continuing
  hospital bills cover her now (vol6 hook — the family pays
  for staff who served them after they can no longer serve).
- **Bed 2 — Mr. K, 64.** Possibly a barge crew member from the
  era when the family ran their own crews. His ceiling-question
  ('is it the ceiling') in the painted ward is the question of
  a man who has been looking at ceilings the family paid for.
- **Bed 5 — Mrs. R, 81 (Marta Romero).** Family housekeeper of
  20 years. Now a litigant against them via the PetroTex case.
  Alive on Walpurgisnacht; the deck does not say when she'll
  die.
- **Bed 6 — Mr. D, 70 (Dante).** The employer. In the same
  ward as the people he employed. He is the chapter's quiet
  irony.

### XIV — TEMPERANCE (Frank)

**Adjacent observer; small contract.** Frank watches for the
family on a thin contract — a kind of surveyor's retainer. The
observation chamber is partly his own discipline and partly
the chamber he uses to keep records the family pays him to
maintain. The Tuesday silence is the silence Frank keeps about
the work; the Saturday call to Elicia is partly his off-the-
clock confidence-keeping.

### XV — THE DEVIL (Daigle)

**Friend of the family AND beneficiary of the family's
patronage. Conflicted.** Daigle owns his own bar but the
no-interest startup loan came from Antonio. The bar's lease was
brokered through a family connection. Daigle's "Uncomfortable
Inside" status is partly the discomfort of running a place that
exists because of a family he is increasingly horrified by.

Daigle's morning note ('the bar will be open and I will be
behind it') is the resignation of a man who knows he cannot
walk away from the patronage that made the bar possible.

### XVI — THE TOWER (Evangeline)

**Contracted by the family. Dependent.** Evangeline's render
farm runs on D'Ambrosio money — Quent's Thursday slot ("16:00
render farm — evangeline check-in. quent on this. trust him.")
is Quent doing payment review for the family. The Tower's
crash is the crash of an enterprise that depended on a
family's continuing patronage at exactly the moment the family
was paying attention to its dying elder son instead of its
contracted infrastructure.

Vol6: Alberto pays Evangeline what she's owed, late, with
interest, and apologizes. The redemption arc materializes as
back-pay.

### XVII — THE STAR (un-named figure)

**Former employee, possibly the void mother's confidante.** The
un-named figure at XVII signs with the same dash as the
Strength figure at VIII. (The two un-named women may be the

twice. The deck preserves the ambiguity.)

She poured water into the river on Thursday night the same week
the Tower fell. The water may have been an offering to a

where the mother's relics rest.

**The gathering is the deck's labor census.** The cast

## Alberto's arc — design space

**What "becoming a better person" looks like in chapter-act
terms, per vol5 setup:**

The arc cannot be melodramatic. Alberto is a competent man
who has been complicit in his family's worst habits. His
becoming better is small: he keeps better records, he
acknowledges the people he previously ignored, he pays bills,
he answers calls he previously dodged, he eventually returns
to people his family hurt and offers what restitution is
possible (cash, apology, testimony, time).

He does this slowly. He does it for a long time. He does it
without expecting to be forgiven by anyone — including
himself.

The deck's reserved vol6 hooks for his arc:

- **The Marta restitution call** — Alberto calls Marta (still
  alive in Ward C in vol5) and apologizes. She is not
  obligated to accept. The deck does not pre-write her
  response. (A voice-log diorama opportunity — see Phase B.)
- **The Daigle absolution attempt** — Alberto goes to Daigle's
  bar. He sits at the middle barstool. Daigle pours him the
  Chariot's Flame. They do not talk about Antonio. They sit.
- **The Evangeline back-pay** — Alberto pays the render
  farm's outstanding invoices, late and with personal
  interest from his own savings. He does not tell his
  father (already dying, possibly already dead). He does not
  put the family seal on the check.
- **The diner kindness** — Alberto goes to D'Ambrosio's
  diner, sits at the counter where John serves him, learns
  John's name, learns John's family's name, leaves a tip
  that approximates what John should have been paid
  decades ago. (The deck reserves the question of whether
  John accepts.)

## Closing constraint

The D'Ambrosios are complicated villains. The complication is
that their patronage was real even as their abuse was real.
The deck does not let either register cancel the other. The
employees who survived them owe them nothing AND the
employees received from them what they could not have received
elsewhere AND the employees were owed what the family did not
pay AND the family was owed what the employees did not always
give. All four registers operate at once.

Alberto's redemption arc respects all four registers. He is
not absolving the family; he is acknowledging the ledger.

The deck's working method for this complication: render the
artifacts (calendars, ledgers, hospital admissions, bar
receipts, lawsuit depositions) and let the reader walk the
employment register as a separate cartography from the family
register. The two maps overlap; they are not the same map.
