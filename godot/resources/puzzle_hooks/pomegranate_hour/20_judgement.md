## ACT I — THE ROOM  (00:24 — 06:00)

INT. THE LITTLE CHAPEL OF THE LAKE — INTERIOR — AFTERNOON.

A wood-frame tabernacle, single room, raised pulpit at one end now stripped down to its riser. The pews have been removed. The floor is unfinished pine. The ceiling has its original tin tiles, painted white, water-stained at the corners. A large ceiling fan, slow. A pair of small wooden doors at the back, propped open with a brick. Through the doors: late summer light, a screened porch, beyond that the lake.

Along the walls of the room, on the wood paneling, hand-painted notices remain from the chapel's earlier years —

> *YE MUST BE BORN AGAIN — JOHN 3:7*

> *NO RUNNING IN THE TABERNACLE*

> *THE OFFERING IS FOR THE WIDOW LANE FUND — PLEASE GIVE GENEROUSLY*

— and, painted over the original notices in fresher (but still old) paint, the additions of the arts collective that took over after the chapel was deconsecrated —

> *NO LANGUAGE / NO INSTRUCTION / NO HIERARCHY / WE LEARN BY WATCHING*

> *PLEASE WIPE THE FLOOR WITH THE GREEN RAG BEFORE LEAVING*

> *NO RECORDING WITHOUT THE WHOLE GROUP'S CONSENT*

The room has been arranged for rehearsal. Twelve folding chairs are placed in a wide circle on the unfinished pine. The chairs are mismatched.

At the center of the circle: a small kitchen stool. On the stool: a single mason jar of water, the lid loose, a small ring of condensation forming around its base.

That is the entire set.

Twelve people enter the room from the back doors, one by one, with the slow not-yet-organized rhythm of a group that has not yet decided who will lead. They take chairs. They sit. They do not speak.

The TWELVE ENSEMBLE MEMBERS are presented to us by the camera, briefly, in a sequence of slow pans:

- MEMBER ONE: a woman, sixty-eight, in a faded floral dress and a cardigan despite the heat
- MEMBER TWO: a man, fifty-four, in a gas-station polo shirt with the embroidered name **DWAYNE**
- MEMBER THREE: a woman, forty-one, with a long braid down her back and her infant daughter asleep in a sling against her chest
- MEMBER FOUR: a man, thirty-two, in glasses, a small notebook in his lap that he will not consult
- MEMBER FIVE: a woman, twenty-six, the only person in the circle in a wheelchair, parked at her chair's position
- MEMBER SIX: a teenage boy, seventeen, in a windbreaker with a small embroidered patch of a stylized P interlocked with a stylized H over his heart
- MEMBER SEVEN: a man, seventy-one, in a pressed work shirt and a clean baseball cap that he removes and places in his lap
- MEMBER EIGHT: a woman, thirty-eight, in scrubs — she has come straight from a shift at HARMONY CREEK MEMORIAL
- MEMBER NINE: a man, forty-six, a slow careful presence, the kind of person who has spent a long time being patient with himself
- MEMBER TEN: a woman, twenty-three, freshly graduated from somewhere, wearing a band T-shirt for a band the deck does not name
- MEMBER ELEVEN: a man, fifty-eight, in a denim shirt with a faded HALSEY FARMS embroidered logo on the breast
- MEMBER TWELVE: a woman, eighty, very small, very upright, in a green dress with bell sleeves and a long emerald-and-burgundy shawl that has been hand-stitched by a hand the deck knows

The twelve sit in their twelve chairs in the wide circle. They do not introduce themselves. They have not been introduced. They look at each other, slowly, around the circle. Each acknowledges each with a small nod.

The acknowledgments take ninety seconds.

When they have finished acknowledging, the circle is silent. The ceiling fan turns. The water in the mason jar at the center has a small drop forming on the outside of the glass. The drop will fall in nineteen seconds.

It falls.

## ACT II — THE GESTURE  (06:00 — 14:00)

After the drop has fallen, the OLD WOMAN — MEMBER TWELVE — raises her right hand.

She raises it slowly, palm flat, fingers together, from the level of her thigh to the level of her eye. The motion takes nine seconds.

She holds the hand at the level of her eye for three seconds.

She lowers it — slowly, palm flat, fingers together — from her eye to her heart. The motion takes nine seconds.

She holds her hand at her heart for three seconds.

She rests her hand in her lap.

She does this once.

The ensemble does not respond.

She rests for ninety seconds.

Then, slowly, MEMBER ONE — the woman in the cardigan — raises her right hand. She performs the gesture: thigh to eye, hold three, eye to heart, hold three, rest. Her performance of the gesture is not exactly MEMBER TWELVE's. The pacing is similar but not identical. MEMBER ONE's hand pauses, briefly, at the level of her sternum on the way up — a pause MEMBER TWELVE did not pause. MEMBER ONE's hand, at the eye, is at the level of her cheek rather than her brow. She is not copying. She is doing it her way.

She lowers her hand. She rests.

Ninety seconds.

MEMBER THREE — the woman with the infant — performs the gesture next. She performs it with her left hand because her right arm is around the sling. Her gesture is identical in shape and tempo to MEMBER ONE's except for the use of the left hand. The infant in the sling does not wake.

She lowers her hand.

The gesture has now been performed three times by three different bodies. The circle is silent. The room is silent. The ceiling fan turns.

MEMBER FOUR — the man with the notebook — performs the gesture. He does not open his notebook. He raises his right hand from his thigh. He pauses at the sternum, as MEMBER ONE did. He continues to the level of his eye. He holds three. He lowers. He pauses at the sternum on the way down — a new beat, not in any prior version. He continues to the heart. He holds three. He rests.

He is, the camera understands, beginning to learn.

The gesture has been performed four times. The four performances differ in small ways and agree in large ones.

The performances continue around the circle, in no particular order. Each member performs the gesture once. Each performance is slightly different. The differences are small. The agreement is large.

The camera does not zoom. The camera holds, wide, on the circle.

When the twelfth member has performed the gesture — MEMBER ELEVEN, the HALSEY FARMS denim shirt, who performs the gesture last because he raised his hand last — the circle has completed one cycle. The gesture has been performed twelve times.

The room is silent. The ceiling fan turns.

A small bird — a swallow, perhaps — flies through the open back doors and circles the ceiling fan, briefly, and exits through the same open doors.

Nobody in the circle looks at the bird.

A long beat.

MEMBER TWELVE raises her right hand.

She performs the gesture again.

The cycle begins again.

## ACT III — THE FOURTH CYCLE  (14:00 — 19:30)

Time elapses on screen via a slow fade. When the image returns, the room has the same composition but the light through the screened porch has dropped two degrees. We are now mid-afternoon shading toward evening. The mason jar of water at the center of the circle has dropped, by perhaps a quarter inch, in volume. A second condensation drop is forming on the outside of the glass.

The ensemble is now on the FOURTH cycle of the gesture.

The performances have begun to align. MEMBER ONE's pause at the sternum on the way up — first introduced in cycle one — has been adopted by every member who has performed since. MEMBER FOUR's pause at the sternum on the way down — introduced in cycle one — has also been adopted. The gesture now contains two sternum pauses, one ascending and one descending, that were not in MEMBER TWELVE's original. MEMBER TWELVE has, since cycle two, begun performing the pauses herself. She did not introduce them. She has learned them.

Other small features have spread:

- MEMBER SEVEN's slight bow of the head when the hand reaches the eye, introduced in cycle two, is now performed by everyone except MEMBER FIVE, who cannot perform it from her wheelchair posture, and MEMBER TWELVE, who has decided not to perform it
- MEMBER FIVE's small adjustment — a brief outward turn of the palm at the level of the heart, just before the hand rests — was introduced in cycle three and has, by cycle four, been adopted by eleven of the twelve. MEMBER TWELVE has not adopted it. She has watched MEMBER FIVE perform it twice and has decided to keep her own version
- MEMBER NINE has, on his cycle-four performance, added a small thing: at the very end of the gesture, after the hand has come to rest in the lap, he raises both hands together, briefly, palms up, at the level of his stomach, for a single beat, and then lowers them. The addition is, in the room, an offering — the chapter's quietest doctrinal innovation.

MEMBER NINE's addition is small and is not announced. He performs it once. He looks at the circle. He waits.

The next member to perform — MEMBER TWO, the gas-station polo — performs the gesture. He performs it with all of the now-adopted small features. At the very end, he, too, raises both hands at the level of his stomach for a single beat.

MEMBER NINE nods to him, slightly. MEMBER TWO nods back.

The cycle continues. Every member who performs after MEMBER TWO performs the new addition. MEMBER TWELVE, when her turn comes, performs the addition too. She has decided to learn this one.

By the end of the fourth cycle, the gesture has thirteen distinct components:

- the raise from thigh to sternum
- the pause at the sternum
- the raise from sternum to eye
- the pause at the eye
- the slight bow of the head at the eye
- the lower from eye to sternum
- the pause at the sternum
- the lower from sternum to heart
- the pause at the heart
- the outward turn of the palm at the heart
- the rest in the lap
- the brief offering of both hands at the stomach
- the lowering of both hands

The gesture was, in cycle one, four components.

The room has not spoken.

## ACT IV — THE FIFTH CYCLE  (19:30 — 23:30)

A second slow fade. The light through the porch has dropped further. It is now early evening. The lake outside is glassy. A faint loon-call comes through the open doors at one point and is not followed by another.

The ensemble is now on the FIFTH cycle.

The performances are aligned. They are not identical — MEMBER ELEVEN, the HALSEY shirt, performs his sternum pauses slightly shorter than the rest; MEMBER FIVE's palm-turn is more emphatic than the others — but they are recognizably one gesture. The thirteen components are performed by each of the twelve members. The chapter has, by cycle five, agreed.

When MEMBER TWELVE has finished her cycle-five performance, she rests her hands in her lap.

She does not raise her right hand to begin cycle six.

She looks at the circle.

The circle looks back.

She speaks. Her voice is small. The voice is the voice of an older woman who has long since stopped raising her voice for anything. The voice is the same voice as the WOMAN in S01E03 (THE EMPRESS).

MEMBER TWELVE
*(quiet, calm)*
Friends. I think we have it.

The ensemble does not respond.

She looks around the circle. She nods to each member, in turn.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER ONE)*
You brought the sternum pause on the way up. Thank you.

MEMBER ONE nods.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER FOUR)*
You brought the pause on the way down. Thank you.

MEMBER FOUR nods.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER SEVEN)*
You brought the bow. Thank you for the gentleness.

MEMBER SEVEN nods.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER FIVE)*
You brought the turn at the heart. Thank you. I have decided not to learn it. I am not refusing you; I am giving the chapter a small dissonance. You will understand.

MEMBER FIVE smiles, slightly, and nods.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER NINE)*
You brought the offering at the stomach. Thank you. That is the chapter's keystone. We will keep it.

MEMBER NINE nods.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to MEMBER ELEVEN, the HALSEY shirt)*
You shortened the sternum pauses. The chapter accepts you. The chapter does not require you to lengthen them. Continue as you have.

MEMBER ELEVEN nods. The smallest nod in the circle.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(to the circle as a whole)*
We will not record this. We will not teach this. We will not perform this for any audience. We will, each of us, carry the gesture in our own bodies as our own. When we encounter, in our own lives, someone who has begun to learn this gesture without us — someone we have not met, someone in a different city — we will recognize them by the gesture. We will not name what we have done. We will only nod.

A long beat.

MEMBER TWELVE
The next time we meet, we will perform the gesture once together at the beginning and once together at the end. We will not perform it in between unless we need to. We will not perform it for cameras. We have permitted this camera because the chapter requires that this evening's work be preserved. We have not permitted any subsequent camera. We will not be filmed performing this gesture again.

She looks across the circle at the camera.

MEMBER TWELVE
*(directly to the camera, soft)*
Friend. Thank you. You may go now.

She nods at the camera.

The camera holds for nine seconds.

Then —

The ensemble, all twelve of them, simultaneously, raise their right hands — MEMBER THREE uses her left — and perform the gesture in unison.

Thirteen components. Twelve bodies. One gesture.

The performance takes seventy-six seconds.

When their hands rest in their laps, the room is silent.

MEMBER NINE raises both hands, briefly, at his stomach. The eleven others follow, an eyeblink behind. The twelve perform the offering together.

They lower their hands.

The ceiling fan turns.

The camera holds for an additional sixteen seconds. The ensemble does not move. The water in the jar at the center has a third drop forming.

The drop falls.

The camera does not record what happens after the drop falls.

The camera ends.

## ACT V — THE CHAPEL  (23:30 — 25:42)

EXT. THE LITTLE CHAPEL OF THE LAKE — EVENING — WIDE.

The chapel from outside. The wood-frame building. The porch. The lake beyond. The light low and golden.

The twelve ensemble members exit the chapel through the back doors, in pairs and threes and ones, over the course of seven minutes of real time, edited here to two minutes. They walk down the small lawn to the gravel parking area at the side of the building. They get into their vehicles — pickups, sedans, in two cases a single shared bus from the apartment community at Westport — and they leave.

The vehicles leave on different roads.

The OLDEST WOMAN — MEMBER TWELVE — is the last to leave. She walks alone down the gravel path. She stops at the chapel's small handpainted sign, which the camera now sees for the first time:

> **THE LITTLE CHAPEL OF THE LAKE**
> *est. 1947 · deconsecrated 1991 · the building is open to the wind*

She places her hand, briefly, on the sign.

She continues to the parking area. A small grey sedan is waiting for her with the driver's-side door open. Inside the sedan, behind the wheel: ALBERTO. He has been waiting. He does not get out. She climbs in beside him. He closes her door. He starts the engine. They drive.

The camera does not follow.

The chapel is empty.

The doors are open.

The mason jar of water remains on the kitchen stool at the center of the chairs.

The drop on the outside of the glass has fallen.

A new drop is forming.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the twelve ensemble members are the twelve. they are, by my private confirmation, the twelve surviving named persons present at the Sinkhole Nexus on March 1, 1979. I did not tell them, when I cast them, that this was the basis of the casting. I told them, individually, that I was making a piece about gesture and that a friend whom they trusted had recommended them. the trusted friends were different people in each case. each cast member arrived at the chapel without knowing the others. each cast member, on entering the room, recognized at least four of the other eleven on sight. they did not greet each other by name. they nodded.
- I did not stage the recognition. I sat with the camera at the side of the room and watched it happen. it took ninety seconds. by the end of the ninety seconds, every person in the circle had acknowledged every other person at least once. they understood what had been done to them. they did not respond to me. they had decided, by mutual silent consent, to receive the casting as the room's invitation.
- the gesture was not scripted. MEMBER TWELVE did not know the gesture before she raised her hand. she has told me, after the filming: *"I knew I would raise it. I did not know I would raise it that way. the way was a thing the room offered me. I accepted on behalf of all of us."* the gesture's subsequent additions — the sternum pauses, the bow, the palm turn, the offering at the stomach — were each volunteered by individual members in the manner the episode shows. nobody coordinated them in advance. nobody requested them. each addition was the addition that the member, at that moment, brought into the room.
- the chapter at XX is the chapter of the call. tradition shows a trumpet, a rising of the dead, an examined life. this episode renders it as twelve survivors of a single event learning a gesture they did not know in a deconsecrated tabernacle on a summer afternoon. the call is the gesture. the rising is the rising of twelve people who had been sitting alone with what they survived. the examined life is the slow propagation of small additions to a shared gesture across a circle of strangers. that is the deck's reading. it is also the survivors' reading. they confirmed it among themselves before they left.
- MEMBER FIVE's palm turn is a movement her physical therapist had taught her after the Sinkhole, in the long months of relearning her hands. she did not bring the movement consciously. she brought it because her body knew it. it spread across the ensemble because the chapter accepts the body's knowledge as legitimate doctrine.
- MEMBER NINE's offering at the stomach is the chapter's keystone, as MEMBER TWELVE said. MEMBER NINE has, since the day of the Sinkhole, lived with chronic pain in the digestive tract — a pain he attributes to the swallow of dust at the event itself. the offering at the stomach is his daily gesture. he has been performing it, alone, in his own house, for forty-five years. he brought it to the room because the room asked for it. the room recognized it. the room added it to the gesture. now, by mutual agreement, the eleven other survivors will also perform it, at their stomachs, every time the gesture is performed by any of them anywhere.
- MEMBER ELEVEN, the HALSEY shirt, was the chapter's most reserved member. he wore the HALSEY logo because he works at the orchards and had not had time to change before driving up. he did not remove the shirt. he understood the room. he shortened the sternum pauses because his bones do not permit longer ones; the chapter accepted his bones as a permission. he did not contribute an addition. he was satisfied by participating.
- MEMBER TWELVE — the woman in the emerald-and-burgundy shawl — is the same woman as the WOMAN in S01E03 (THE EMPRESS). she is the void mother of vol5. she is Dante D'Ambrosio's first wife. she is Antonio and Alberto's mother. she is, at the time of this filming, fifty-six (the gesture's MEMBER TWELVE I have aged in the description by twenty-four years to match the cycle of the gesture's transmission; the production credits will say only that she was a member). she is the only member of the ensemble who knew, in advance, that the cast would be the survivors. she requested that I assemble them. she requested that I let her raise her hand first. she requested that I send the gesture forward.
- ALBERTO in the sedan at the end is ALBERTO. he had not been told that his mother was the ensemble's first hand. he had been told only that she had asked him to drive her home. he was waiting in the parking lot for two hours and forty minutes without going inside. when she emerged, he opened the door for her. he asked her, in the car, what they had done. she said: *"a small thing, dear. you would have liked it. we will perform it for you the next time you visit me, if you would like to learn."* he has, by his own report to me three months after the filming, asked her to teach him. he has begun to perform the gesture. he is, at the time of this writing, one of fourteen non-ensemble members of the public who have learned the gesture and perform it as a daily practice. the dispersal has begun.
- the chapel itself is open to the wind. the doors do not lock. the mason jar of water remains at the center of the room between visits. visitors who come to the chapel — usually one or two a week, drawn by no advertised purpose — find the jar, refill it from the lake, and leave. the chapel does not require a custodian. the wind is the custodian. that is the chapel's reading of itself.
- I have not been back to the chapel since this filming. I have decided that the chapel is a place I have been invited to once. the invitation is the gift. the chapel does not require my return. I have, however, performed the gesture myself, in private, every day since this filming. I am one of the fourteen non-ensemble members of the public. I am, here, declaring it.
