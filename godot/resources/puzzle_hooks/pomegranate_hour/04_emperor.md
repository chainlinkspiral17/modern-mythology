## ACT I — THE GRILL  (00:24 — 06:30)

EXT. BACKYARD PATIO — LATE AFTERNOON.

A large flagstone patio behind a single-story brick house in Westport. The yard is established — a magnolia in one corner, a satsuma in the other, a Saint Augustine lawn going to seed. The patio has, at its far end, a brick fire pit roughly six feet across, with a heavy steel grate, a deep bed of mesquite charcoal already glowing. Beside the pit: a small folding side table holding a stack of plates, a small white pitcher, and a kitchen cloth folded in quarters.

In front of the grill: THE CHAIRMAN. Late sixties. Crisp blue button-down rolled at the sleeves, khaki shorts, leather sandals. A canvas apron over the shirt — the apron is hand-printed across the chest in faded letters: **CHEZ HÉLÈNE · CHEF'S TABLE · 1979.** The apron is not from a restaurant currently in operation. The apron belonged to a working sous-chef in a New Orleans Creole kitchen the year before the kitchen lost its head chef.

THE CHAIRMAN holds a long-handled fork in his right hand. The fork is not for the steaks; he turns the steaks with tongs. The fork is for emphasis.

He turns slowly to face the camera.

He does not speak.

He turns back to the grill.

On the grill: six bone-in ribeyes, four inches thick, dry-aged. Each one has been seared on one side and is currently being seared on the other. The fat is rendering. The smoke is thick. The smoke smells, the audio bed implies, of fat and mesquite and a deeper undertone — what we will come to understand, by act III, is the marinade.

A small mason jar on the side table contains a thin russet liquid with herbs floating in it. The jar's lid has been removed. The jar is labelled in a careful old hand:

> **the family · 3 onions · 4 cloves · sherry · file · cayenne · bay**
> *make a day ahead. cover with cheesecloth, not lid. let it think.*

The recipe is not in the chairman's hand. The recipe is in the hand of the WOMAN at S01E03 (the void mother). She wrote it on a small index card in the mid-1980s. The card has been kept in the patio's drawer ever since.

THE CHAIRMAN brushes the marinade onto the steaks with a small bouquet of fresh bay leaves tied at the stems. He does this carefully. He has been doing this for forty years.

He sets the bouquet down.

He turns to the side table. He lifts the pitcher. He pours water into a tall glass. He sets the glass down at the grill's edge. He does not drink.

A small bell rings from inside the house — a single soft chime.

He looks up.

A small voice from inside, distant, calls a name. The name is faintly heard through the open patio door. The name is **"Hélène."** It is a child's voice calling, perhaps, an aunt — or maybe a dog. The name is not for the chairman.

He returns to the grill.

The first guest is arriving.

## ACT II — THE GUESTS  (06:30 — 14:00)

EXT. PATIO — CONTINUOUS.

A SIDE GATE clicks open. The first guest enters. They are an OLDER MAN in a pressed guayabera shirt. They carry a covered dish.

The OLDER MAN crosses the patio. He sets the covered dish on the long folding table that has been set up to the chairman's left. He does not greet the chairman with words. He places his right hand briefly on the chairman's shoulder. The chairman acknowledges with a small nod.

The OLDER MAN takes a position at the patio's edge.

A second click. A WOMAN enters — sixties, in a wraparound dress, a pearl necklace. She carries a smaller covered dish. She places it on the long table. She nods to the chairman. She takes a position.

A third. A FOURTH. A FIFTH.

The guests enter, one at a time, over the course of nine minutes. Each one carries something. Each one places it on the long table. Each one acknowledges the chairman with a nod or a touch of the shoulder. None speaks.

By the eleventh guest, the long table is full. The covered dishes are arrayed:

- a casserole that may be jambalaya or dirty rice — the lid is on
- a deep ceramic bowl, dark glaze, covered with a clean towel — file gumbo, possibly, from the steam visible at the towel's edge
- a long oval platter under foil — possibly an étouffée
- a covered Le Creuset round in burgundy enamel — possibly a daube, possibly a fricassee, the cover does not lift
- a wooden cutting board with a damp tea towel over a long shape — a loaf, maybe a stuffed loaf
- a small ceramic dish covered with parchment — pralines, almost certainly
- a thermos of something hot — probably the coffee, served strong with chicory
- a clear glass dish with a vented lid — étouffée's brother, or a maque choux
- a small lidded enamel pot — beans, slowly cooked
- a deep round basket lined with cloth — bread, almost certainly the white loaves from the same family bakery in the lower Garden District since 1947
- a single wide ceramic bowl, uncovered, holding pomegranate seeds in a small careful pile

The eleventh guest takes a position at the patio's edge.

The chairman, still at the grill, has not turned. He has been working.

The patio has eleven standing guests. Eleven covered dishes. Six ribeyes on the grill. No conversation.

THE CHAIRMAN turns from the grill. He surveys the patio. He counts.

He looks at the long table.

He looks at the side gate.

He waits.

The side gate clicks open.

The TWELFTH GUEST enters.

She is a small woman, eighty, wiry, in a chef's coat. The coat is unbuttoned at the throat. The coat is clean but not new. On the breast pocket, a small embroidered name: **HÉLÈNE.**

She is, the deck's deeper layer understands, the head chef of the New Orleans restaurant that lost its head chef in 1979. She did not in fact lose her position; she retired six years later, in 1985, and has been retired ever since. She is a guest at Chairman D'Ambrosio's patio because the chairman's first business venture, in 1971, was a small fine-dining restaurant in Westport called LA TABLE PETITE, and the head chef he hired for the small fine-dining restaurant in 1971 was HÉLÈNE, who agreed to make the trip from New Orleans on the condition that the chairman drive her home on Sundays. The chairman, then twenty-five and freshly inheriting his family's modest holdings, did drive her home on Sundays. He drove her home on Sundays for the year and a half the restaurant existed. The restaurant closed. The chairman moved on to other businesses. HÉLÈNE returned to her own kitchen at CHEZ HÉLÈNE in New Orleans. The chairman never opened another restaurant. He retained the apron.

HÉLÈNE crosses the patio.

She does not set anything down. She has brought nothing.

She crosses to the chairman.

She places her right hand on his cheek. Briefly. Calm.

THE CHAIRMAN closes his eyes for a beat.

HÉLÈNE removes her hand. She crosses to the long table. She lifts the lid of the deep ceramic bowl — the file gumbo, if it is gumbo. She inhales. She replaces the lid. She lifts the clean towel from the wooden cutting board — yes, a stuffed loaf — she replaces the towel. She lifts the foil from the long oval platter. Étouffée. She replaces the foil.

She turns to the chairman.

She nods.

He nods back.

She crosses to the patio's edge and takes a position with the other guests.

The twelve are now arrayed around the patio's perimeter. The chairman is at the grill. The grill's smoke continues to rise.

A small bell rings again from inside the house. A single soft chime.

THE CHAIRMAN looks at his wristwatch.

He turns to the steaks. He removes them from the grill, one by one, with a long pair of tongs. He places them on a clean wooden board on the side table. He covers them with a clean cloth. He sets the tongs down.

He addresses the guests for the first and only time in the episode.

CHAIRMAN
*(low, calm, the voice of a man who has been preparing to speak this sentence for forty years)*
The meat is ready. The other dishes are on the table. The pomegranate is for after. Please serve yourselves.

A long beat.

CHAIRMAN
*(softer)*
I will sit.

He crosses to a single chair at the head of the long table — a heavy carved chair he has not, until this moment, touched. He lowers himself into it.

He folds his hands on the table in front of him.

The twelve guests do not move.

DOG BEAUCOUP — a large brown mutt, the homeowners' dog — wanders out from the open patio door. He smells the air. He looks at the patio of twelve standing guests and one seated chairman. He lies down at the chairman's feet.

Nobody moves to serve themselves.

Nobody eats.

## ACT III — THE WATCHING  (14:00 — 20:30)

The patio holds.

The CHAIRMAN sits at the head of the table. His hands are folded. He looks at the long table. He looks at the twelve guests. He looks at the steaks under the cloth.

He does not lift the cloth.

He does not serve.

The TWELVE GUESTS stand. They look at the chairman. They look at the steaks. They look at each other. They do not move toward the table.

The patio has decided, by mutual silent consent, that the meal is not to be eaten.

The meal is to be looked at.

The meal is to be witnessed.

Time passes. The light through the magnolia changes — the late-afternoon honey deepens toward gold, then toward rose. The mesquite charcoal in the fire pit settles, ticking, into ash. The grease on the cooling grates continues to drip into the embers. The smoke thins. The smell of the food remains.

The CHAIRMAN at one point — fourteen minutes in — picks up the long-handled fork from where he had set it on the side table. He holds it in his right hand. He does not use it.

HÉLÈNE — at her position at the patio's edge — takes one small step forward at minute eighteen. She does not approach the table. She tilts her head. She is regarding the étouffée she placed on the table by lifting the foil. She is, the deck understands, judging her own work in absentia, from across a patio she has not stood in for forty-five years.

She is satisfied with the work.

She steps back to her position.

The OLDER MAN in the guayabera — the first guest — at minute twenty-two takes a different small step. He crosses, slowly, to the side table. He lifts the small white pitcher. He pours water into the chairman's glass. He sets the pitcher down. He returns to his position.

The CHAIRMAN looks at the glass.

He picks it up.

He drinks. Three swallows.

He sets the glass down.

He nods to the OLDER MAN.

The OLDER MAN nods back.

The meal continues to not be eaten.

At minute twenty-four, the camera performs its second movement of the episode (after the opening pan in act I) — a slow zoom in on the single wide ceramic bowl of pomegranate seeds. The bowl is uncovered. The seeds are in a careful pile. The seeds catch the rose light from the changing sky.

The seeds are dark red.

The seeds, in close-up, can be seen to be six in number. Six pomegranate seeds. The pile is small. The pile is deliberate.

The zoom holds for nine seconds.

The zoom withdraws.

## ACT IV — THE GUEST WHO DOES NOT ARRIVE  (20:30 — 23:42)

The light has dropped further. The patio is in the last clear minutes of evening.

THE CHAIRMAN remains at the head of the table.

The twelve guests remain at the patio's edge.

The food remains on the table, uncovered now in several places where the guests had lifted lids, untouched in every place.

The side gate clicks.

Every head turns.

The gate opens.

The thirteenth guest is the LIGHT — the last full red ray of the day cuts across the patio at the height of a person's torso. The light enters through the open gate. It crosses the patio. It lands on the chair opposite the chairman at the foot of the long table.

The chair is empty. The light lands in the chair. The light holds in the chair.

The thirteenth guest is the light, and the chair, and the absence.

THE CHAIRMAN, at the head of the table, looks at the chair. He looks at the light in the chair. He looks at the empty seat.

He raises his right hand — slowly, palm flat, fingers together — from his thigh to his sternum. He pauses. He raises it to his eye. He holds. He bows his head, slightly. He lowers his hand from his eye to his sternum. He pauses. He lowers it to his heart. He holds. He raises both hands, briefly, at the level of his stomach — palms up — for a single beat. He lowers them.

The gesture takes seventy-six seconds.

He performs it once.

The twelve guests do not respond. They watch.

When he has finished, he lowers his hands to the table.

CHAIRMAN
*(quiet, to the empty chair)*
Hélène prepared the gumbo. The étouffée is mine. The bread is from Cohen's. The pralines are the second batch this season; the first batch is in the freezer for Christmas. The beans are slow.

He pauses.

CHAIRMAN
*(softer)*
The pomegranate is for after.

He closes his eyes.

CHAIRMAN
You may take a seat.

The light in the chair fades. The sun has dropped below the magnolia. The chair is now in shadow.

THE CHAIRMAN opens his eyes.

He rises.

He crosses to the empty chair.

He pulls it out from the table, slightly. He sits down in it.

He folds his hands on the table.

He looks across the long table — across the still-untouched meal — at his own empty chair at the head of the table.

He looks at the head chair for a long beat.

He nods to it.

He rises.

He crosses back to the head chair. He sits in it.

The twelve guests, at their positions around the patio's edge, do not move.

DOG BEAUCOUP rises from where he has been lying. He pads, slowly, around the table. He lies down beside the now-empty foot chair, where the chairman briefly sat. He rests his chin on the floor by the chair's leg.

The CHAIRMAN looks at the dog.

The dog looks at the chairman.

The chairman bows his head, slightly.

The dog rests.

The patio holds.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the chairman is THE CHAIRMAN — DANTE D'AMBROSIO. he did not know I was filming. he knew there was a camera on his patio because he agreed to a documentary feature about a Westport patio bbq, framed as a piece about Louisiana hospitality and the family-style meal. he signed a release. he did not know which patio he was on. the patio is the patio of his former home, sold to the Devera family in 1991 after his wife's relocation. the Deveras invited him back, at my request, for the day. they had Sunday plans elsewhere. they left him the patio. he accepted the invitation with what I read as quiet gratitude for the opportunity to grill on this brick again.
- the apron is his. the apron is from CHEZ HÉLÈNE — HÉLÈNE PIERRE-LOUIS's restaurant on Magazine, where she was head chef from 1971 to 1985. DANTE worked at CHEZ HÉLÈNE for four months in 1970, as a busboy, while preparing to open his small fine-dining restaurant LA TABLE PETITE in Westport. CHEZ HÉLÈNE gave him the apron when he left. he wore it through LA TABLE PETITE's eighteen-month run. he has continued to wear it for backyard grilling for forty years.
- LA TABLE PETITE closed because DANTE could not find a way to make the restaurant profitable without compromising the Creole technique HÉLÈNE had taught him. he closed the restaurant rather than dilute it. he moved into other businesses — first food-service supply, then real-estate, then the holdings that would eventually become the D'Ambrosio empire. the empire began at a Creole restaurant that closed because it would not stop being a Creole restaurant. that is the chapter's truest origin.
- HÉLÈNE is real. she is eighty-one. she lives in the lower Garden District. she has not been to DANTE's patio in forty-five years. she agreed to come at my request, on the condition that she not be asked to cook anything. she came in her chef's coat to honor the chairman. the touch on his cheek when she arrived was, by her account afterwards, "what I would have done at the door of the restaurant on a Sunday after a hard service." DANTE has not seen her since 1985. they have not spoken since 1985. they have not exchanged correspondence since 1985. her arrival at this patio, in this episode, is the first physical contact between them in forty years.
- the eleven other guests are eleven members of the broader CHEZ HÉLÈNE professional family — sous-chefs, line cooks, prep cooks, dishwashers, front-of-house staff — who worked at the restaurant during the period of DANTE's apprenticeship in 1970 or during LA TABLE PETITE's run in 1971–72. they range from sixty-four to eighty-three. they came of their own free will when I sent letters to them through HÉLÈNE. they had not seen DANTE in forty-five years either. the food on the long table was their food — prepared in their own kitchens that morning, brought in their own dishes. none of them are paid actors. none of them required releases. they signed releases anyway. they signed them in front of HÉLÈNE, who countersigned each one as witness, in her own hand.
- the marinade in the small mason jar is the marinade. the recipe card was written by the WOMAN of S01E03 (DANTE's first wife, the void mother) in the mid-1980s. she had had it from HÉLÈNE in the early 1970s, when she met HÉLÈNE during the LA TABLE PETITE period. the recipe is HÉLÈNE's family recipe. it was given by HÉLÈNE to her daughter-in-law of the heart. it was given by the daughter-in-law of the heart to DANTE at the closure of the small restaurant in 1972, on the back of a wine list. it was rewritten by DANTE's wife in the 1980s, in her own clearer hand, when she found the wine list folded into one of DANTE's books. it has lived in this patio's drawer ever since.
- the food was not eaten. I had not anticipated that the food would not be eaten. the lack of eating was the chapter's choice — the chapter belonged to the guests and they chose to set the meal and to witness it without consuming it. by my private inquiry afterward, I learned that the eleven non-HÉLÈNE guests had agreed among themselves, in the parking lot before entering through the side gate, that they would not eat. they had not consulted HÉLÈNE. when she arrived and saw the table, she understood. she did not, by their report, even consider eating.
- the food was taken home by the guests at the end of the patio session. the food was redistributed, cold or reheated, at five separate dinners in five separate homes over the following days. nothing was wasted. HÉLÈNE took the étouffée and ate it the next evening with her grandson. the chairman ate one of the ribeyes, cold, the next morning at his kitchen counter, alone, off a paper plate.
- the gesture in act IV that the chairman performs at the empty chair is, of course, the gesture from S01E20 (JUDGEMENT). DANTE has been performing it, in private, for some time. he learned it from a member of the JUDGEMENT ensemble — MEMBER NINE (the chronic stomach pain, the offering at the stomach as keystone) — whom DANTE encountered at a survivor's small private memorial on the tenth anniversary of the Sinkhole. MEMBER NINE performed the gesture quietly during the memorial. DANTE asked him about it. MEMBER NINE taught him in the parking lot afterwards. DANTE has performed it daily ever since. he did not know, until I told him after the patio filming, that it was the gesture of the Judgement ensemble. he had thought it was a private gift between him and the man who taught it to him. when I told him, he was quiet for a long time, and then he said: *"that's appropriate. that's appropriate. yes."*
- the bowl of six pomegranate seeds. six is the count the chairman has been keeping privately for many years. it is the count of his own household's losses at the Sinkhole, in his own private accounting. the count is not the count the deck's other characters keep (the un-named figure's +IV and her —; HÉLÈNE's own losses; the official rolls' figure). it is his count. the seeds are for after because the meal is the meal of the survivors, and the after is the after of the count, and the after has not come yet, because the count is six and the chair at the head of the foot of the table is in some sense the seventh.
- the side gate did not click in act IV. nobody opened the gate. the light at sunset cuts across the patio at that angle every clear evening in May. I knew this. I chose the date. the light is the thirteenth guest. the deck does not require the thirteenth guest to have a body. the chair received the light. the chairman performed his gesture. the chair held what could be held.
- the chairman has not invited me back to the patio. I have not asked to come back. I am not sure that he understood, at the time of the filming, that I am the daughter of the woman who left the records office in the early 1990s. I am not sure that he does not know. I have decided, for the present, not to clarify. I am, however, aware that the chair at the head of the table at the foot of the table will at some future date be his, on the day his wife re-enters the patio. she has not, at the time of this writing, re-entered. she has not been invited. she is welcome.
