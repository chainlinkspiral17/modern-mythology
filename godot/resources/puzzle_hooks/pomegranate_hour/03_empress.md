**arcana:** III — THE EMPRESS
**runtime:** 22 min
**writer / director / host:** Elicia

## COLD OPEN  (00:00 — 00:24)

Black. Title. Host's nod.

Black. 60 Hz hum begins, mixed quieter than usual. Underneath the hum: the faint sound of growing. The growing is not a metaphor. It is a sound — soft, fibrous, just at the threshold of hearing.

## ACT I — THE SITTING ROOM  (00:24 — 06:30)

INT. HARMONY CREEK ESTATES — PLAN IV (VICTORIAN) — SITTING ROOM — DAY.

A maximalist front parlor. Emerald-green damask wallpaper that has been painted over, in patches, with small fields of burgundy where the original pattern has chipped. A grand piano upright against the inside wall, lid down, sheet music on top of the lid — a single hand-copied piece titled, in pencil, *"For the room, who knows the song."* A large brass mirror over the mantel. The mantel is crowded with objects:

- a pewter pitcher
- three pomegranates, partially desiccated
- a small ceramic figurine of a woman with a swollen belly, the figurine's glaze cracked
- a row of mason jars with various interior contents — dried wildflowers in one, a small amount of cloudy honey in another, what appears to be soil in a third
- a Polaroid photograph leaned against a candlestick, face-down

The floor is a wide-plank pine, varnished, scratched. The room smells, from inference, of beeswax and overripe fruit.

The room contains, in addition to its furniture and its objects, a great deal of art on the walls that has not been bought.

The art on the walls is not professionally made.

- a large irregular oval of canvas, stretched over a homemade wooden frame, painted in layers — a green field, a red dot, a hand outlined in pencil at the center
- a series of small framed drawings on yellowed paper, each one a small wildflower in graphite, signed in the lower-right corner with the letter *M* and a small heart
- a found-object assemblage in a glass shadow box on the wall above the piano: three pomegranate seeds in arrangement, a tiny silver bell, a strip of fabric with the printed letters *HALSEY*, a tooth (small, possibly milk)
- a long horizontal hand-stitched banner above the doorway, in cross-stitch on linen, that reads: **WHAT IS GROWING BELOW IS GROWING BECAUSE I LET IT.**

The art is the work of the same hand. The hand is not in the room.

In a large wingback chair, near the fireplace: A WOMAN — NICOLA'S MOTHER-IN-LAW, fifty-six, very heavily pregnant, in a long emerald-and-burgundy dress with bell sleeves. Her dress is the dress the deck's painted Empress wears at vol5 III. Her hair is pinned up. Her hands rest on her belly.

She is not in this episode by accident. She is the deck's reserved figure. The deck refuses to render her face in vol5. This episode also refuses to render her face — but only because her face is unrendered, in the show home, by her own choice. She is turned slightly away from the camera. We see her hair, her hands, her dress, her belly, the edge of her jaw.

She is humming, very faintly, the song on the piano sheet music. The song does not exist as published music. She is the source.

She humms for forty-three seconds.

She stops.

She listens.

The growing sound — the sound that is in the audio bed of the whole episode — becomes momentarily louder. From below the floor. Beneath the wide-plank pine.

She places her right hand flat on the floor.

She is on a chair, so the hand reaches down and out, palm-down on the varnished pine, her arm extended.

She holds her hand there.

She speaks for the first time in the episode. Her voice is calm, the voice of an older woman who has long since stopped raising her voice for anything.

WOMAN
*(to the floor)*
Slowly, friend. We have time.

The growing sound subsides.

She lifts her hand.

She returns it to her belly.

She resumes humming.

## ACT II — THE BASEMENT THAT IS NOT A BASEMENT  (06:30 — 13:00)

INT. KITCHEN — DAY — CONTINUOUS.

The kitchen, through an archway from the sitting room. A small wooden island, scarred. Copper pans hanging from a wrought-iron rack. A vintage stove. A modern refrigerator that does not match the rest of the room. On the refrigerator door — held by a single round magnet — a printed flyer:

> **HARMONY CREEK ESTATES — PLAN IV — VICTORIAN**
> *3 BR / 2.5 BA · finished hardwood throughout · NO BASEMENT*
> *(community-wide policy: structural concerns with the underlying clay strata)*
> *contact your NexCorp Residential Solutions liaison for inquiries*

THE WOMAN enters the kitchen from the sitting room. She moves with the slow weight of late pregnancy. She places a kettle on the stove. She lights the burner. She crosses to a heavy oak floorboard — the floorboards in the kitchen are darker and wider than in the sitting room — and kneels with great care.

She places her ear to the floor.

She listens for eighteen seconds.

She rises.

She crosses to a kitchen drawer. She removes a small claw hammer. She returns to the same plank. She kneels again.

She works the claw between two boards. She pries gently. The plank lifts. Beneath it: another plank, identical, fitting into a notched frame. She lifts the second plank. The second plank is hinged.

Beneath the second plank, what is exposed is not soil. What is exposed is not concrete. What is exposed is a small chamber, lit faintly from below, of dimensions the deck does not measure. The chamber is approximately a foot deep — and within the foot of depth, the room is somehow much taller. The depth-perception is wrong in the way Lynch's red room is wrong. The eye reads the small square opening in the floor as a window into a much larger space.

Inside the chamber:

- a small carpet of growing things — soft green shoots, six inches tall, in soil that has organized itself around the chamber's edges
- a small wooden chair, child-sized, positioned at the center of the chamber
- on the small wooden chair, a CHILD-sized figure made of stitched fabric and dried flowers — a doll? a self-portrait? a placeholder? The figure does not move and is not animated. The figure is hand-made. The figure's stitched mouth is closed. The figure's stitched eyes are open. The figure wears a tiny green-and-burgundy dress that matches THE WOMAN's.
- behind the figure, against the chamber's back wall (which is, again, not at the depth the floor allows), is a piece of Halsey orchard timber — the same wood used in the floorboards above. The timber has been carved, by the same hand that made the figure, into a low arch. The arch is the entrance to something the camera does not show.

THE WOMAN does not lean into the chamber. She kneels beside the opening.

WOMAN
*(to the figure)*
You are growing.

The figure does not respond.

The shoots in the soil sway, very slightly, in a current of air that is not present in the kitchen.

WOMAN
*(soft)*
Your father will be pleased.

She lowers her right hand into the chamber. She does not lower it all the way to the bottom; she lowers it to about half the depth the eye reads as the chamber's depth. Her arm vanishes from the kitchen at the level of the floor and reappears, in the chamber, at the level her body would suggest — but her hand is closer to the figure than the geometry would permit.

She touches the figure's stitched cheek with one finger.

She withdraws her hand.

She closes the hinged plank.

She replaces the upper plank.

She tamps it down with the heel of her hand.

She rises, slowly, holding the counter for support.

The kettle on the stove begins to whistle.

She removes it from the burner.

She pours hot water into a small ceramic teapot. The teapot is the same color as the wallpaper.

She returns to the sitting room.

## ACT III — THE WALLS, THE WALLS  (13:00 — 18:30)

INT. SITTING ROOM — DAY.

THE WOMAN sits in the wingback chair. She holds her tea in her lap. She does not drink. The tea steeps.

The camera, which has held a relatively wide framing for the entire episode, performs its second movement of the episode (after the act II close-up on her hand in the chamber): a slow lateral track along the parlor wall to the left of the fireplace.

The track reveals, as it moves, additional outsider artwork that has been on the wall all along but has not been the focus.

The art reads, in sequence, as:

- a folded paper accordion of small landscape watercolors — each a small image of the same wooden gate marked HALSEY · PRIVATE, painted in subtly different seasonal lights. There are twelve panels. They have been folded into an accordion. The accordion's first panel reads, in handwriting: *"the year by the gate. each picture is the same gate. the gate does not move."*
- a series of three identical canvases, each painted in three layers — first the green field, then the figure of a woman in a long dress, then a layer of white paint over the entire canvas, mostly translucent, with the words *"NOT YET"* scratched through the white with a pin
- a wooden cuckoo clock, modified — the cuckoo has been removed and replaced with a small figurine of a hand. At the hour, the hand emerges and points downward. The clock is currently at 1:48 PM. It does not chime at 1:48; it chimes at every six minutes past the hour, regardless of which hour it is, because she has rewired it that way. She has explained this to nobody.
- a tall narrow assemblage in a homemade frame: a black-and-white photograph of a small girl, maybe seven, with a cropped haircut, looking at the camera with no particular expression. Above the photograph, a piece of orchard timber. Below the photograph, a single dried pomegranate seed glued to the matte. Beneath the pomegranate seed, in pencil, the words *"this is who I was."*
- a small wooden box on a shelf, no glass, no door, containing five black-and-white snapshots of the same girl at five different ages — seven, twelve, sixteen, twenty-three, thirty-one. The girl is the same girl. The girl ages. The thirty-one-year-old in the last snapshot is recognizably young, and recognizably the eventual face of THE WOMAN in the chair — she has aged twenty-five years since the last snapshot was taken
- a single framed photograph at the end of the wall-track: a wedding photograph. A man and a woman. The man is recognizable as a younger DANTE D'AMBROSIO. The woman is THE WOMAN, in her thirties. The wedding is at the door of a small church the deck has not before rendered. The frame is hand-carved, by the same hand that made everything else, from orchard timber.

The lateral track ends. The camera holds on the wedding photograph for nine seconds.

THE WOMAN'S voice, from off-frame:

WOMAN
*(quiet)*
The frame is from his family's orchard, before they sold the orchard to Halsey. I was happy at this picture. We did not yet have a son. We had not yet lost the first son. The orchard was still the orchard.

She is speaking to the camera. She is speaking to the documentarian. She is not turning to face the camera. She is in her chair.

WOMAN
You may be wondering whether I am a real person.

A long beat.

WOMAN
*(small, dry)*
I have decided to leave that question with you.

She picks up the teapot and pours her tea, finally, into the small ceramic cup she has been holding. She drinks.

WOMAN
*(after the sip)*
The house knows the song because I have sung it to the house. The garden under the floor knows my voice. The figure in the garden is a kind of placeholder for the chapter the deck does not finish. I am told that the deck does not finish the chapter on purpose. I have made the chapter's small version myself, and I keep it in the floor, and it does what it does.

She returns her cup to the saucer.

WOMAN
I have been pregnant for years.

A pause.

WOMAN
The pregnancy is the chapter the deck does not finish. I have decided to remain pregnant rather than let the deck finish it without me.

She looks down at her belly.

WOMAN
I have decided this. I am not asking the deck to honor it. The deck honors what it honors. The painted Empress at vol5 will not be me. The painted Empress will be Nicola, who married my elder son, who I did not raise. The void where I would have been in the deck will remain a void.

She places her hand on her belly.

WOMAN
The deck refuses to render my face for an honest reason. The reason is that my face is what I have decided not to give the chapter. I have given the chapter the chair, the dress, the wall, the wallpaper, the song, the figure in the garden, the timber, the pomegranates, the teapot, the assemblages, the photograph at the end of the wall.

She turns her head, slightly — for the first time in the episode — and we see, briefly, the edge of her mouth. She is smiling. The smile is small. The smile is not for the camera.

WOMAN
*(soft)*
The face is mine.

She turns away again.

The camera holds.

## ACT IV — THE GROWING  (18:30 — 21:42)

INT. SITTING ROOM — LATE AFTERNOON.

The light through the parlor windows has gone honey.

The growing sound has been audible throughout the episode at the baseline of the audio track. In ACT IV, the sound rises — slowly, over four minutes — until it is no longer a baseline but a presence. Not loud. Insistent.

THE WOMAN is still in the wingback chair. Her tea is half-finished. The kettle is quiet. The cuckoo clock is at 4:48 PM. At 4:48, the modified cuckoo emerges and points downward, briefly, and retracts.

She watches the cuckoo retract.

She places her tea on the small table beside the chair.

She rises.

She crosses, very slowly, to the kitchen.

She kneels again at the kitchen floorboard. She lifts the upper plank. She lifts the hinged plank.

The chamber below has changed.

The shoots have grown. They are now nine inches tall. The carpet of growing has organized itself, in the time since act II, into a small ring around the wooden chair. The figure on the chair is in the center of the ring.

The figure's stitched eyes are open as before. The figure's stitched mouth — and this is the change — is now slightly open. A small stitch has come loose.

THE WOMAN looks at the loose stitch.

She does not lower her hand into the chamber.

She speaks to the figure.

WOMAN
*(very gentle)*
Not yet, friend. We have time.

She closes the hinged plank.

She replaces the upper plank.

She tamps it down.

The growing sound, after she closes the floor, subsides — but does not entirely vanish. It continues, very low, at the baseline.

She returns to the sitting room.

She lowers herself, slowly, into the wingback chair.

She picks up her tea.

She begins humming the song.

The cuckoo clock advances toward 5:09.

At 5:09, the modified cuckoo emerges, points downward, and retracts. The clock chimes the small mechanical chime it always chimes at 5:09.

THE WOMAN continues to hum.

The 9:09 cable interference begins to rise, faintly, in the audio — three hours early in the diegesis. The interference is not loud. It is the first time the 9:09 interference has not occurred at 9:09 in the episode's diegesis. It comes early because the chapter is the chapter of a woman who has been pregnant for years and whose room is in a different time than the room outside the room.

The interference subsides.

The growing sound subsides.

The 60 Hz hum continues.

She continues to hum.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the show home is real. PLAN IV (VICTORIAN) was the original model home for HARMONY CREEK ESTATES' first phase. it has been re-staged six times since its original buildout. between the fourth and fifth re-staging, the home was occupied by the family of the woman in the chair. they had been moved into the house under a Halsey Farms / NexCorp Residential Solutions joint employee-relocation program that has no public name. they were the third family in the home that year. the family was relocated to a different floor plan one month before I filmed.
- the family did not take their things. their things are the things in the house. the staging team did not remove them. NexCorp's compliance representative reportedly walked through the house, looked at the maximalist parlor, looked at the outsider art on the walls, looked at the floorboards she had pried up and re-set, said nothing, and signed a notice that the home was "not currently suitable for showing." the house has not been shown again since. it has, however, not been emptied. somebody is paying the utility bills. I do not know who.
- the woman in the chair is the woman in the chair. she returned to the house, by my count, eleven times during the year I had access. I filmed three of the eleven returns. she came in by the kitchen door, which she had a key to. she sat for between one and four hours. she tended the things she had grown beneath the kitchen floor. she left without disturbing the staging in the rest of the house.
- the chamber under the kitchen floor exists. I have measured it. it is, by physical measurement, a recess approximately fourteen inches deep in the subfloor. the recess is interior to the home's slab, which itself has been adjusted from the standard slab for this floor plan to accommodate the recess. the recess is plumbed in a way I cannot explain. the recess is lit from below by a light source I cannot locate. the camera reads the depth as much greater than fourteen inches. the eye reads the depth as much greater than fourteen inches. this is not a CGI effect; it is what the chamber does to the eye. I have seen it in person. I have not lowered my hand into it.
- the figure in the chamber is the figure. the figure is hand-stitched. the figure is now, at the time of this writing, three years older than the figure was at the time of filming. the figure has continued to receive small alterations — a new stitch here, a new petal pinned to the dress there — between her visits. I do not know whether she comes only when I am not there or whether she comes also when I am there. she has the key.
- the outsider art on the walls is hers. she has been making it for forty years, beginning when she was sixteen. the pieces date from across the decades. she did not bring them to the house. the house brought them. I do not have a better explanation. when she was relocated, the art remained on the walls — and additional pieces have appeared in the spaces between her recorded visits. she has not confirmed authorship of the new pieces. I have not asked.
- the wedding photograph at the end of the lateral track in act III: she is the bride. the groom is DANTE. the wedding took place in 1981, at the small Saint-Adolphe chapel that no longer exists, in a parish that has been since absorbed into the Westport diocese. the marriage produced ANTONIO (vol5 VII, dead) and ALBERTO (vol5's redemption arc, ongoing). she was relocated to her own residence under a household-resettlement arrangement that occurred between the first and the second son. the deck does not say what she calls the arrangement, and neither does she. she has not lived in a D'Ambrosio property since 1991. she has not visited Dante in any of his residences since 1992. she has, by her own count, called him twice — once in 1996, once in 2002, both calls placed from public payphones. the calls' duration was, in each case, four seconds. she hung up before he could speak. she wanted, she has told me, only to hear that he was alive.
- the pregnancy is the chapter she does not finish. she has been pregnant — in some sense the deck reserves — for thirty-four years. she is not biologically pregnant. she is pregnant the way the chapter is pregnant. she carries the chapter the deck refuses to render. her body, when she sits in the wingback in the parlor of the show home, holds the chapter as her own. when she stands and crosses to the kitchen, the chapter goes with her. when she lifts the kitchen floor and tends the small figure in the chamber, the chapter receives the gentleness that biological motherhood would have received. she has decided to live as the unfinished chapter rather than to release the chapter to be finished by people who have not earned the finish.
- I have not asked her permission to air this episode. I have, however, given her a hand-numbered VHS rip of the episode in advance. I left the tape in her kitchen, on the wide oak board above the chamber. she replaced it, three weeks later, on the same board, with a small handwritten note that read: *"you have my face. you can have my chair. the floor is mine. the figure is mine. air whichever of the rest you would like."* I have aired no shot of the floor's lift or the figure's stitched face above the closing frame. the closing frame is the frame of the wedding photograph. that is the only face she has given me, and she gave it the deck.
- the cuckoo clock chiming at 5:09 instead of 9:09 is the chapter's signature. the chapter does not finish. the chapter is held. the chapter is in a different room than the room outside the room. the room's clock is therefore in a different time than the room outside the room. the 9:09 interference, when it occurred, occurred at 5:09. it has the right pitch and the right duration. it just chose to arrive early. the room does what it does.
