Black. Title. Host's nod.

Black. The 60 Hz hum begins, riding under a faint analog video roll — the sub-second flicker of a CRT being switched on.

## ACT I — THE STUDIO  (00:24 — 06:00)

INT. CHANNEL 14 STUDIO — EVENING — WIDE.

A small room converted from what was, two decades earlier, a feed-and-grain office. A single wood-paneled wall painted matte black behind the broadcast set. The set: a wooden desk, an upholstered chair, a brass crucifix on a stand at the chair's right elbow, a pewter pitcher of water, a single rotary push-button telephone with a long curled cord.

In the chair: FATHER COSGRAVE, late sixties, in a black shirt and white Roman collar. The collar is starched. His hair is steel-gray, parted on the right. His glasses are wire-frame. His hands rest on the desk, folded.

Above the set, an old kinescope sign reading **ON AIR** glows red.

Below the kinescope: a small studio clock with hands. It reads 8:30 PM.

A single STUDIO CAMERA — operated by NOBODY — sits on a tripod at the front of the room. Its tally light is on. It is broadcasting.

Inside the camera's viewfinder feed (which we cut to in PIP for the first ninety seconds), the lower-third reads in chyron:

> **CHANNEL 14 · LIVE**
> *"Conversations with Father Cosgrave"*
> *sponsored by HALSEY FARMS — local since 1948*

In the corner of the chyron, a small additional line of type rolls in slowly:

> *call in: 318-555-1414*

FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the camera. He does not smile. He does not frown. He says, in the warm low cadence of a man who has done this for nineteen years —

FATHER COSGRAVE
Good evening, friends. We are on the air.

He pauses three seconds.

FATHER COSGRAVE
This is Father Cosgrave, here as I am each Tuesday and Thursday at half past eight, in the small studio in the old Westport Feed Building, where we sit together for a brief hour and take your questions.

He lifts the pewter pitcher. He pours water into a tall glass that we had not previously noticed at his right elbow. He sets the pitcher down. He sips.

FATHER COSGRAVE
We have, as is customary, no agenda this evening. We will go where the calls take us. I have, as is customary, the door closed and the lights out in the front of the building. Mrs. Boudreaux at the switchboard down the road in Lake Charles has assured me that the lines are open.

He looks at the rotary telephone.

The telephone rings.

He picks up the handset on the second ring.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(into the handset, calm)*
Good evening. You're on the air.

WOMAN'S VOICE  *(through the handset, audible because the studio mic catches the receiver's bleed)*
Father, this is Lena.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Lena. Good evening.

LENA
*(audibly nervous)*
Father, I've been worried about my son. He hasn't called me since Easter and his wife says she doesn't know where he is.

FATHER COSGRAVE
And where is the last place you know him to have been.

LENA
*(after a long pause)*
He was driving to Beaumont.

A small click in the audio. FATHER COSGRAVE does not visibly react. He waits.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Lena, do you have a recent photograph of your son?

LENA
Yes, Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Hold the photograph in your hand while we speak. Look at his face while you tell me what you remember about the morning he left.

LENA describes a Tuesday morning in March — eggs at the table, a small argument about money, her son loading a bag into a truck. As she speaks, FATHER COSGRAVE looks down at the desk. He is not taking notes. He is listening with his eyes closed.

When she finishes, he says —

FATHER COSGRAVE
Lena. The Lord has given parents a particular kind of attention. It is not the attention of police. It is the attention of memory. You will keep his face in your memory as it was on that morning. You will not let the more recent fear overwrite the older love. That is the work.

LENA
*(quiet)*
Yes, Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE
If he calls you, you will not be angry. You will be glad. You will be ready to be glad. That is also the work.

LENA
Yes.

FATHER COSGRAVE
God bless you, Lena.

LENA
God bless you, Father.

He sets the receiver back in the cradle.

He looks at the camera.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(soft)*
Hold on, friends. We'll take another call after a brief moment.

He lowers his eyes to the desk.

In the chyron's lower-third, the small phone number continues to roll.

## ACT II — THE CABLE INTERFERENCE  (06:00 — 11:30)

Cut to: a different angle. Wider. We see now that the camera in the front of the set is one of two. A second camera, smaller, sits on a small shelf at the back of the studio behind FATHER COSGRAVE's shoulder. This second camera's tally light is OFF.

A separate dial above the kinescope sign reads **STUDIO MONITORS — INPUT B.**

For the entire body of the episode, the studio is broadcasting from CAMERA A. The studio monitors are receiving from INPUT B — a feed that has nothing to do with the broadcast. The feed is the program that CHANNEL 14 itself is currently broadcasting, monitored back into the studio off the air so the priest can see what's going out.

We cut to the studio monitor's screen, a small color CRT on a side table.

The CRT shows the broadcast: FATHER COSGRAVE at his desk, slightly delayed. Standard. The chyron reads as expected.

Then —

The CRT image flickers. The screen rolls — a single horizontal scan line of static — and for one frame, the broadcast image is replaced by a different image: an interior of a hospital ward. Six beds, two empty, the pink dusk light through east-facing blinds. There is no chyron. There is no audio.

The image is replaced by the broadcast. The studio CRT shows FATHER COSGRAVE at his desk again.

In the broadcast, FATHER COSGRAVE has not moved. He has not seen the studio CRT.

The interference: half a second.

In the studio, somewhere off the broadcast feed, a small mechanical click — like an old-style relay closing.

The phone rings again.

FATHER COSGRAVE picks up.

FATHER COSGRAVE
You're on the air.

THE CALLER  *(a man's voice, calm, slightly southern, slightly amused)*
Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE pauses for a half-beat. His face does not change.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Yes, friend.

THE CALLER
This is the gentleman from Beaumont.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(after a beat, the warmth still in his voice)*
We've spoken before.

THE CALLER
We have. We speak most weeks.

FATHER COSGRAVE
We do.

THE CALLER
*(quiet, almost playful)*
I was hoping you might have something new for me this evening.

A pause. FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the camera, briefly. His expression does not change.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(measured, the same nine words he says every week)*
The door was locked. The door is still locked.

THE CALLER
*(a small laugh, almost pleased)*
Father.

THE CALLER
You said the same thing last week.

FATHER COSGRAVE
The door has not changed.

THE CALLER
*(thoughtful)*
What about the building.

A long silence.

FATHER COSGRAVE
The building has not changed.

THE CALLER
I drove past the building last week.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(after a longer beat)*
You did not.

THE CALLER
I did, Father. I drove past it on a Tuesday. I parked across the street. I watched it for about forty minutes. The door was locked. The door is still locked. You're correct about that. But the windows on the second floor have new blinds, and I could see them being installed from across the street. They were paid for by somebody. Somebody pays for windows.

FATHER COSGRAVE has nothing to say to this.

THE CALLER
Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Yes.

THE CALLER
What I came back from. What I have been on the air with you about. I think there is a way back.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(very gentle)*
There is no way back, friend.

THE CALLER
*(amused)*
You don't know that.

FATHER COSGRAVE
I know what I know, friend.

THE CALLER
What you know is the door, Father. The door is one door. There are others.

FATHER COSGRAVE
The door is the only door I have been asked to attend to.

THE CALLER
That is a particular humility. I respect it.

THE CALLER
Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Yes.

THE CALLER
*(very quietly)*
I want to ask you something I haven't asked before.

A small click in the audio.

THE CALLER
*(quieter still)*
When you went down to Beaumont in 1979, before you were Father — before you took the collar — when you were still working for the family — did you know what was inside the building.

The studio is silent.

FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the camera. The look on his face is not the calm of a broadcaster. The look is the look of a man who has been asked a question he has been waiting nineteen years to be asked, and has been waiting because he had decided that the question would never come, and now that it has come, has discovered that he prepared an answer in 1979 and has not consulted it since.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(quiet, into the receiver, on air)*
Yes. I knew.

The studio monitor — INPUT B — flickers again. For half a second, the CRT shows a different image: a black pickup truck on a country road, headlights on, driving toward the camera. No chyron. No audio.

The image is replaced.

THE CALLER
*(very quietly, after a long pause)*
Thank you, Father.

The line clicks dead.

FATHER COSGRAVE sets the receiver in the cradle.

He picks up his glass of water. His hand does not shake. He sips. He sets the glass down.

He looks at the camera.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Friends. Thank you. We will take a brief moment.

The kinescope ON AIR sign flickers but does not go off.

## ACT III — THE 9:09 INTERFERENCE  (11:30 — 18:00)

The studio sits in silence for forty seconds.

FATHER COSGRAVE does not move.

The studio clock advances to 8:54.

A small wood-and-steel door at the back of the studio opens. A YOUNG WOMAN — late twenties, jeans, a flannel over a tee shirt, headphones around her neck — enters. She is the engineer, MIA. She does not look at the camera. She does not enter the on-air space. She crosses to the studio monitor and crouches in front of it. She inspects the CRT.

She glances at FATHER COSGRAVE. He shakes his head, slightly.

She leaves the studio.

FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the rotary telephone.

The telephone does not ring.

He looks at the camera.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(soft)*
We will continue.

He takes a sip of water.

The phone rings.

FATHER COSGRAVE picks up. He answers in the same warm voice he has answered every call with this episode.

FATHER COSGRAVE
You're on the air.

A YOUNG WOMAN'S VOICE
*(young, half-laughing, audibly slightly stoned)*
Father, hi, I'm sorry to bother you, this is gonna sound stupid.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Not at all, friend. Go ahead.

YOUNG WOMAN
Okay so. I've been getting these calls. On my landline. From a number that doesn't exist.

FATHER COSGRAVE
How do you know it doesn't exist.

YOUNG WOMAN
Because it's only five digits. It rings and the caller ID says like, six-six-six-zero-one. Just five digits. And I pick up and there's nobody on the other end. But then like — like, very faint, in the background — there's somebody.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(careful)*
What does the somebody sound like.

YOUNG WOMAN
It sounds like my mother.

FATHER COSGRAVE pauses.

FATHER COSGRAVE
And your mother is —

YOUNG WOMAN
*(small voice)*
My mother died in '04.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(very gentle)*
Friend, when you pick up the next time, would you say her name out loud.

YOUNG WOMAN
*(after a beat)*
I — I haven't been saying anything. I've been just listening.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Try saying her name. Just once. Do not say it more than once.

YOUNG WOMAN
Why only once.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Because if you say it more than once, you will be telling whatever is on the other end of the call how to use it.

A long silence.

YOUNG WOMAN
*(very quiet)*
Father, that's a fucked up thing to say.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(without judgment)*
It is. I'm sorry. I have been doing this for nineteen years. The advice is the truest I have. Say her name once. Listen for what happens.

YOUNG WOMAN
*(after another beat)*
Okay.

FATHER COSGRAVE
And friend.

YOUNG WOMAN
Yeah.

FATHER COSGRAVE
If you ever want to call this number — not the five-digit number, this one, the channel-fourteen number — you may call it instead. And we can sit on the line together while you do not say anything.

YOUNG WOMAN
*(softly)*
Okay, Father.

FATHER COSGRAVE
God bless you.

YOUNG WOMAN
*(small laugh)*
Yeah, you too.

She hangs up.

FATHER COSGRAVE sets the receiver down.

The studio clock reads 9:08.

A small click in the audio.

The studio monitor CRT flickers — INPUT B —  for nine seconds. The CRT does not return to the broadcast. The CRT continues to roll, scan line by scan line, through a sequence of images:

- a wooden gate marked HALSEY · PRIVATE
- a CRT inside a workshop, displaying a heart-rate trace
- a young woman alone in an apartment shuffling tarot cards
- the same six-bed hospital ward as before, now with three empty beds instead of two
- a single mason jar of honey, label HALSEY · WILDFLOWER · ONE EACH PLEASE
- a black pickup truck pulled over at the side of a dirt road, headlights off, driver invisible
- the lower-third chyron of CHANNEL 14, no priest, no studio, no signal, just the chyron — *call in: 318-555-1414*

The studio clock advances to 9:09.

The 9:09 cable interference rises.

FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the camera.

He raises his right hand to his collar. He adjusts it slightly.

He does not speak.

The studio clock holds on 9:09.

The interference subsides.

The studio monitor returns to the broadcast feed.

In the broadcast feed, FATHER COSGRAVE is at his desk, hand at his collar. The chyron is correct. The chyron rolls. Nothing about the broadcast has changed.

FATHER COSGRAVE lowers his hand.

The studio clock advances to 9:10.

He looks at the camera.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Friends. Thank you for being with me this evening. The lines will close in a moment. If you have a question that we could not get to, you may write to us at the address you'll see on the screen. Lena, your son will call. Friend with the calls — please remember the once. To the gentleman from Beaumont — God bless you, friend. We will speak next week.

He looks down at the desk.

He folds his hands.

He looks back up.

FATHER COSGRAVE
The door was locked. The door is still locked.

He looks at the camera for a long beat.

Then he leans forward, slightly, and removes the rotary telephone's handset off the cradle by half an inch. He sets it back in the cradle. He picks it up again. He sets it back. He picks it up a third time. He sets it back.

The phone has been hung up three times.

He sits back.

FATHER COSGRAVE
Good night, friends.

The ON AIR sign goes off.

The lights in the studio dim.

FATHER COSGRAVE sits at the desk in the half-dark for a long beat. He looks at the camera. The tally light is now off; he is looking at a camera that is not broadcasting. But our camera — the documentary camera — is.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(quiet, to our camera)*
Did you get all of that.

He waits. There is no answer.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(small)*
I thought so.

He stands. He removes his collar. He places it on the desk. He picks up the glass of water. He drinks the rest of it. He sets the glass down.

He walks out of frame.

## ACT IV — THE PARKING LOT  (18:00 — 25:42)

EXT. FEED STORE BUILDING — PARKING LOT — NIGHT.

The building is a small one-story structure with a faded sign that reads, in chipped lettering: **WESTPORT FEED & GRAIN — 1957.** The sign has not been corrected. The building has not been a feed store since 1991. The building is a converted studio for CHANNEL 14.

A single car in the lot: a beige Buick sedan. License plate: Louisiana.

FATHER COSGRAVE exits the side door. He is no longer in his collar. He is wearing a plain blue work shirt under a tan windbreaker. He locks the side door behind him. He stands in the parking lot.

The 9:09 cable interference is no longer audible. The 60 Hz hum is faint.

He walks to the Buick.

He does not get in.

He stands beside the driver's side door for a long beat, his hand on the door handle. He looks across the lot — toward the highway, dark, empty.

He looks across the highway.

Across the highway, parked on the gravel shoulder, with no lights on, is a black pickup truck with Louisiana plates.

The pickup does not move.

FATHER COSGRAVE looks at the pickup.

The pickup looks at FATHER COSGRAVE.

They hold the look for forty-five seconds.

Then FATHER COSGRAVE opens his car door. He gets in. He starts the engine. He pulls out of the lot. He turns left onto the highway, away from the pickup.

The pickup does not follow.

The pickup remains, on the gravel shoulder, headlights off, for the remaining minute and a half of the act.

At 25:18, the pickup starts its engine. The pickup's headlights come on. The pickup turns right onto the highway — the opposite direction from FATHER COSGRAVE.

The camera holds on the empty highway.

The building behind it sits dark. Inside the building, behind the locked side door, the studio monitor — INPUT B — continues to flicker through the same sequence of images. Nobody is watching it. The chyron continues to roll.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the studio is my father's studio. the building is the building my father worked from for nineteen years. the desk is his desk. the brass crucifix is his crucifix. the rotary telephone is the same rotary telephone. it still rings. I had it switched to a non-publicly-listed line for filming.
- FATHER COSGRAVE is not my father. my father (vol6/7 reserve) was a different priest with a different name. FATHER COSGRAVE is a substitute — a friend of my father's, retired, who agreed to perform a single broadcast in my father's chair using my father's mannerisms and my father's nine-word response to the Beaumont caller. he did so on the condition that I not name him in the credits and not air the episode in any market that broadcast my father's original show. CHANNEL 14 closed in 2009. its former coverage area has been honored.
- the call at 14:08 (LENA) is a real call from 1996, archived from the original Channel 14 master tape. her son did call her. the call took fourteen months. she has given permission for the audio's use; her name has been changed.
- the call at 18:48 (the gentleman from Beaumont) is the gentleman from Beaumont. the audio is from 1996. the audio is one of forty-one such calls archived from CHANNEL 14's tapes. the gentleman from Beaumont called my father every week from 1989 until my father's death in 2009. the gentleman from Beaumont continued to call Channel 14 for three additional weeks after my father's death, at the same time, even though no one was answering. the call attempts stopped on the fourth week. the gentleman from Beaumont has not, to my knowledge, called any other public-access program in the southern Louisiana coverage area since 2009. he has, per my private confirmation, called Cosmic Comics in vol6's Harmony Creek Estates strip mall. the voice on the Cosmic Comics phone is the same voice on this Channel 14 tape. RES_SOL_VERIFY in the comments has not yet noticed this.
- the studio monitor INPUT B flicker images are not in the original broadcasts. they are mine. I overlaid them on the studio monitor footage during post-production. they are previews of vol5 scenes that have not yet been painted at the time of this episode's airing. they appear on a CRT that was never broadcast; they are visible only to the priest, to the engineer MIA, and to the documentary camera. the priest has, since seeing the rough cut, asked me where I got them. I have told him I am not sure I got them; I think they got me. he accepted this.
- the YOUNG WOMAN with the five-digit number is real. she called Channel 14 once in 1998. the call is on file. she did not call back. she did not write in. I do not know if she said her mother's name. I have made my peace with not knowing.
- FATHER COSGRAVE's three slow hang-ups at the end of the broadcast are my father's. he did this every Tuesday and Thursday. nobody asked him why. when I was twelve, I asked him. he said: *"because the line ought to know it's hung up. it ought to be told three times. once is courtesy, twice is custom, three times is law."* I did not understand at the time. I have come to think I understand now.
- the black pickup across the highway is the pickup. it parked there on its own. I did not coordinate the shot. it followed CHANNEL 14's signal radius the way RES_SOL_VERIFY follows VEIL.tv's bandwidth. when FATHER COSGRAVE looked at it for forty-five seconds, the look was not in the script. I left it.
- the chapter's title — THE HIEROPHANT — is the chapter of orthodox transmission, of structured doctrine. my father's whole working life was a public-access transmission of an orthodox doctrine. but the deck's reading of the Hierophant, in tradition, includes the secret that the orthodox transmission was never the whole transmission. there was always the call after the call. there was always the door at the back. my father knew this. he kept the door locked. he answered the calls. he hung up three times. he died on a Wednesday in 2009 without saying anything more than he had ever said. this episode is what is left.
