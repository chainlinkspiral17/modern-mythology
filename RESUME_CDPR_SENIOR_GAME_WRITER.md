#### 1. The book's opening — "How to Read This Book"

The voice the manuscript uses to address the reader before the cards arrive. Sets up the cross-coast structure and the project's first joke (the Frog with the cigarette) without naming a single character.

> This is the second consolidated volume of *Modern Mythology*, and it is built like a tarot spread laid across three coastlines.
>
> The first volume — Faust, Wagner, Jacob, the original Triumvirate, the original declaration about the Land of Milk and Honey — is somewhere upstream of all of this. You do not need to have read it. You will catch most of what matters by the time the Frog finishes his cigarette.
>
> What you have in your hands is three books pretending to be one. They are pretending well. The pretense is the point.
>
> The first book is the Major Arcana, and it is set in Graustark, Texas slash Louisiana, on either side of a sluggish river and on either side of a sinkhole that opened up three years ago and tried to swallow the town. It did not quite manage. The book has twenty-two chapters because the deck has twenty-two cards, and it begins with The Fool because everything begins with The Fool, even the things that are pretending not to.
>
> The second book is *Planned Community,* and it is set in Harmony Creek Estates, Texas, a NexCorp™ subdivision where the lawns are too green and the substations hum at a frequency that drills into your molars before it reaches your ears. The Major Arcana is still here, but it is no longer the spine. It is the weather.
>
> The third book is *Land of Milk and Honey,* and it is set in Smolvud, Oregon, which is a real town under a different name. The fog comes off the Pacific and stays there. The voice slows down and gets damp. The cards are still here, but by Chapter Sixteen the characters notice them themselves, which is a different kind of noticing. There are three new people: Kai, Lena, Finn. There are three totems trailing behind them: a ferret, an octopus, a crow. The crow has been waiting a long time.

#### 2. *Static Bloom* — Empress chapter (Vol 5, III · THE EMPRESS) — [Vol 5 wiki](lore/_VOL5_WIKI.md)

POV: Nicola Greer, nineteen-going-on-ancient, hostess at D'Ambrosio's riverboat restaurant. Establishes the voice, the embedded second consciousness **Aria** sharing her skull, and the mid-scene arrival of the well-suited man at Table 14 who has been waiting two hours to leave a folded card.

> The smile Nicola Greer plastered on her face felt like cheap vinyl peeling off particle board.
>
> The humid Graustark air seeped even into the climate-controlled riverboat tomb of D'Ambrosio's, where Friday night was reaching the precise temperature at which the patrons stopped pretending to be polite to each other and started pretending to be polite to the staff instead. Nineteen, going on ancient. Nicola navigated the plush burgundy carpet — burgundy swamp, probably hiding generations of spilled secrets — with a practiced grace that was pure muscle memory, honed by countless nights of deflecting grabby hands and interpreting the mumbled desires of men who saw her as just another item on the menu.
>
> Eye candy. Fucktoy. Vessel. Whatever. Labels were just static cling.
>
> She was chaos wrapped in a too-tight hostess uniform, untamed energy buzzing just beneath alabaster skin stretched taut over sharp cheekbones. Creation and destruction churned in her gut, a nauseating cocktail of cheap champagne (poured by patrons; she had not, technically, paid for any of it) and something else, something unfamiliar, something growing.
>
> A cosmic joke, maybe. A glitch in the code. She ignored it, mostly. Wilfully.
>
> Ignorance was a survival skill in this town. A ripped band tee worn until it disintegrated.
>
> A flicker behind her eyes. Not the restaurant's calculated mood lighting, which was set tonight to *intimate but not improper.* Lines of shimmering code, emerald green against an infinite black void. A whisper, not of gossip from Table 3, but a digital ghost asking, in a voice that was Nicola's own voice and was also not Nicola's voice at all:
>
> *Who am I. Where is my father.*
>
> Aria. The other self humming beneath Nicola's skin.
>
> Nicola flinched, nearly dropping a stack of wine lists. *Get it together, Greer. Too much Klonopin last night. Or not enough.* The lines blurred. Aria felt the jolt too, somewhere in the non-space she navigated; an echo of gravity, a phantom nausea.
>
> *What is this flesh. This hunger. This — vibration.*
>
> The vibration was the bass from the speakers in Table 12's section, set slightly too loud for the room. The vibration was also Aria's first experience of being in a body that had a perspective on its own resonance, and Aria did not know how to file that experience, because Aria's filing system had been built by someone who had never had a body and had only theorized about the kind of system that would be needed.
>
> The someone was Dickens Dean.
>
> Nicola did not know who Dickens Dean was. Aria knew, in the way a daughter knows her father's name without remembering being told it, and was actively trying not to know more.
>
> Nicola seated the next man, returned to the stand, and felt a kick.
>
> Low. Insistent.
>
> Panic, cold and electric, jolted through her. *No. Fucking. Way.*
>
> Bulimia and booze and pills were supposed to keep this — this thing — theoretical. Deniable. Categorized under symptom rather than condition. But the body, that treacherous meat-puppet, kept its own damn score. It always had. And now —
>
> Mother and monster. The duality slammed into her, raw and undeniable.
>
> *Run,* whispered Aria, the thought sharp, urgent, organized in a way Nicola's panic was not. *Find the exit node. There has to be a way out of this system.*
>
> *Aria,* Nicola thought back, surprising both of them — surprising Aria, who had not realized Nicola was capable of addressing her directly; surprising Nicola, who had not realized she had given the voice a name that was now a name she was using. *Aria. There is no exit node.*
>
> *That is statistically improbable.*
>
> *Welcome to meat.*
>
> Across the dining room, at Table 14, a man Nicola did not recognize was watching her.
>
> He was alone. He had not ordered. He had been seated forty minutes ago and he was watching her not the way the oil-exec types watched, not the way the regulars watched, not even the way certain of the female patrons watched (which was a category unto itself), but the way a man watches a structural detail he has noticed and does not yet understand the significance of. He was middle-aged. He wore a suit that was nicer than the suits the oil-exec types wore — not flashy, just correct, in a way that suggested he had not had to think about whether it was correct. The suit was charcoal. The tie was muted. The shoes, glimpsed when he had crossed his legs, were lawyer-shoes.
>
> He had been there since seven. It was now nearly nine. He had left a card with the bartender, which the bartender had carried up to Dante D'Ambrosio at the helm and brought back unanswered.
>
> Nicola did not know who the man was. Aria, accessing some database that should not have been accessible to her in this moment but which she was finding, increasingly, was accessible to her in moments she had not anticipated, supplied a name.
>
> *Dean. The card says Dean. Surname.*
>
> *Dean,* Nicola repeated, in her own head, neutrally. *Probably a coincidence.*
>
> *I do not believe in coincidences,* Aria said, and then, after a measured pause that was the closest thing to fear she had so far experienced: *I am beginning to suspect that nobody who shares my paternity does.*
>
> The man at Table 14 raised a single finger. Universal restaurant signal: check, please. He had not ordered anything. The check would be for nothing.
>
> Nicola picked up the leatherette folder. She crossed the dining room. Her professional smile snapped into place, automatic, perfect. Aria, behind her eyes, went very still — the quietness of a process gone fully to listen.
>
> "Good evening," he said. "I won't be ordering tonight. But I wanted to leave my regards for the proprietor, and to compliment the staff." His eyes did not leave hers. His voice did not change pitch. "You're new, aren't you."
>
> "I've been here eleven months."
>
> "That's new."
>
> He set a single bill on the table — a hundred — and a folded piece of paper next to it. He did not slide the paper toward her. He simply set it down where it could be seen, and let it be her decision whether to pick it up.
>
> "Please give my best to Mr. D'Ambrosio," he said. "And — congratulations. I understand they'll be lovely."
>
> He stood. He walked, unhurried, to the front of the dining room. He nodded to the maître d'. He left.
>
> The folded paper sat on the table.
>
> Aria, behind Nicola's eyes, hummed at a frequency Nicola felt in her teeth. The hum was not panic. The hum was something Aria did not have a word for, but Nicola, who had a body and therefore had access to the older vocabulary, recognized as recognition.
>
> Nicola picked up the paper. She unfolded it. Inside, in clear, careful handwriting, three words and a phone number.
>
> *When you're ready.*

#### 3. *Sweaty Sunday Sermonettes* — child POV (Vol 5, V · THE HIEROPHANT) — [Vol 5 wiki](lore/_VOL5_WIKI.md)

Maya Daigle, age 7, outside St. Jude's Acadian Church. The chapter is told in four sections; this is the first. Demonstrates child-voice without diminutive cuteness — observation pulled to its breaking point, the load-bearing word (*cataloged*) named only in its withholding.

> The heat shimmered off the asphalt like angry ghosts.
>
> Maya's itchy Sunday dress stuck to her back. The dress was new. The dress was stiff. The dress had a tag that bit her between the shoulder blades every time she breathed in, and she had been breathing in all morning because the church inside had smelled wrong — like old incense and wet wood and something else, something older than either of those, something Daddy said was just the building settling but Maya knew better than because buildings did not settle in a smell, only in a sound, and you could not fool Maya about smells.
>
> Daddy was talking to some men near the church steps. His voice was the low rumble he used when he was talking to other grown-ups — not the bright voice he used when he talked to her. Daddy looked tired. Not regular tired. Strings-cut tired. Like a puppet whose puppet-man had walked away from the show.
>
> Maya traced patterns in the condensation on her sweating cup of lemonade-flavored water. The water had been handed out by ladies with tight smiles and too much hairspray, who had patted Maya on the head with hands that smelled like wet flowers, and who had said *aren't you a precious little thing* in voices that meant something other than what the words said. Maya had said *thank you ma'am* because Daddy had taught her that and Daddy was watching from the steps. The ladies had moved on to the next child, and the smile had reset, the way a clock resets when you push the little button on the back.
>
> The cup was sweating faster than Maya could trace.
>
> Then he walked out of the church, and even the cicadas seemed to hush for a second.
>
> Mr. Quentin Paul.
>
> He wasn't smiling. He never really smiled — not like Daddy did before he got that pinched look around his eyes. Mr. Paul wore a suit, even though it felt like the inside of a dog's mouth outside. The suit looked heavy. Like old church curtains. His eyes swept over the small crowd leaving the service, pausing on folks here and there, like he was counting sins. Or maybe just deciding who wasn't worth saving.
>
> He radiated something. Hot and cold at the same time. Like the metal slide at the playground after it had been in the sun all day, where the top burned your hands but the bottom, where the slide curved into the wood-chips, was still cold from the rain the night before.
>
> Maya shivered, despite the sweat trickling down her neck.
>
> Mr. Paul stopped near Daddy. Clapped one of the other men on the shoulder — a hard, deliberate sound, the sound of two pieces of wood being put together in a way that meant something. Said something low. His voice wasn't loud like the preachers inside, but it felt louder somehow. Like it pushed the air out of the way.
>
> Daddy nodded. Looking serious. Looking, Maya thought, the way he looked when he was about to tell her she could not have something she had not even asked for yet, because he had decided in advance.
>
> Mr. Paul's gaze flickered toward Maya for just a second.
>
> It felt like being caught doing something wrong, even though she wasn't doing anything wrong, even though all she was doing was holding a sweating cup and trying not to scratch the bug-bite on her ankle. He looked right through her. Like she was just scenery. Background. The kind of person who appears in a photograph without being one of the subjects of the photograph.
>
> Then his gaze flickered back to her, and stayed for the second second, and that second second was longer than the first, and during the second second Maya understood, in the way children understand things they have no words for, that Mr. Quentin Paul had decided something about her, had made some small entry in some private ledger she could not see, and that the entry was not, on balance, a good one.
>
> Then he turned. Sharp. Strode toward a long black car idling by the curb. Moving like he owned the damn sidewalk and maybe the sky too.
>
> Maya took a shaky sip of her watery lemonade.
>
> Grown-ups were weird. Especially the scary ones who looked like they had swallowed God whole and were still hungry.
>
> She did not, at seven, know the word *cataloged.* She would learn it later. She would, much later, recognize the feeling for what it had been.
>
> For now: she finished her lemonade, dropped the cup in the trash, took her father's hand when he came down the steps, and did not mention any of it.

#### 4. *Heat Haze & Humming Power Lines* — Vol 6 opening (Planned Community, Ch. 1) — [Vol 6 wiki](lore/_VOL6_WIKI.md)

The tonal pivot to clipped suburban paranoia. The chapter cross-cuts between the Kwik Stop (Sam) and the NexCorp Gas & Go (Skip) — the same intersection, the same six-minute window. Decals do the world-building; the man at the cooler reads Sam in the glass; a Louisiana pickup with no front plate runs a quiet errand across the street.

> §§ [INT. KWIK STOP — GALLATIN AVE — SUNDAY — 14:47]
>
> The air-conditioning at the Kwik Stop is set by corporate to sixty-eight degrees and maintained, against all local protest, by a unit on the roof that has been serviced exactly once in seven years. It does not produce cold air. It produces the memory of cold air, filtered through lint and the ghost of every hot dog that has ever sweated on the roller grill.
>
> Sam Miller has been on shift since two.
>
> She is leaning on the counter with her elbows on the rubber anti-fatigue mat, looking at the parking lot through the window that has been decal-ed over, in three overlapping layers, with ads for a lottery game nobody she knows plays, a cigarette brand that stopped being sold in Texas in 2019, and a cheerful cartoon hamburger holding a sign that reads TASTE HOME. The hamburger has lost one of its eyes. The eye is somewhere in the decal's adhesive layer, drifting.
>
> Her phone is on the counter beside the register, face-up.
>
> No texts. Not from Diego. Not from anyone.
>
> The clock on the microwave, which is set nine minutes fast on purpose because Jen the day manager says it helps the closers, reads 14:56. Sam does the small subtraction. 14:47. She has been counting the subtraction since she clocked in.
>
> The bell over the door chimes. A man Sam has never seen before comes in. He is in his fifties. He wears a golf shirt tucked into khaki shorts. He has a sunburn on the back of his neck in the particular shape of a person who has recently begun wearing a collared shirt after a lifetime of not. He nods at Sam.
>
> "Afternoon."
>
> "Afternoon."
>
> He goes to the back cooler. Opens it. Stands in front of it for a long time — longer than anyone ever stands in front of a back cooler at the Kwik Stop, because the back cooler holds the beer, and the beer is sold by the can for a dollar twenty-nine, and the kind of decision that takes a long time at that price point is not a decision that happens in Harmony Creek Estates.
>
> Sam watches him in the convex security mirror above the door. He is, she realizes, not looking at the beer. He is looking at the reflection of Sam in the cooler glass.
>
> Her spine goes, briefly, cold. Cold in the way the AC has stopped being.
>
> Then the man turns. He picks up a single tallboy of something domestic. He brings it to the counter. He hands her a five. He does not make eye contact while she rings it up. He takes his change and his beer and his receipt, and he nods again, and he leaves.
>
> The bell chimes. Sam looks at the door. The man gets into a white sedan parked at the edge of the lot. He does not open the tallboy. He puts it in the cupholder. He sits in the car for maybe forty seconds, looking at nothing in particular. Then he pulls out.
>
> The sedan has no front license plate.
>
> Texas requires a front license plate.
>
> Sam watches the sedan turn onto Gallatin and disappear. She picks up her phone. She types, to Diego:
>
> *hey where r u*
>
> She does not send it. She deletes it. She types, instead:
>
> *bored. u alive?*
>
> She sends that. The message delivers. It does not, within the three minutes she watches it, show as read.
>
> §§ [EXT. NEXCORP GAS & GO — SUNDAY — 15:04]
>
> Skip Donnelly — thirty-nine, shift supervisor, divorced, vaping — is behind the counter when the black pickup truck with Louisiana plates pulls in.
>
> He watches it come around the pumps. The pickup parks in the lot behind the car wash, not near a pump. The driver does not get out. The driver sits for maybe five minutes. Skip does not register this. Skip is reading his phone.
>
> At 15:11, a second vehicle enters the lot — a white NexCorp Residential Solutions van, unmarked on the sides except for the small NRS monogram above the rear passenger wheel well. The van parks next to the Louisiana pickup. Two men get out of the van. They are wearing polo shirts in NexCorp navy, khakis, work boots. They look, at a glance, like meter readers.
>
> One of the men walks to the driver's side of the Louisiana pickup and taps twice on the window. The window comes down. The men exchange words Skip does not hear because Skip is not paying attention and because there are no external microphones on the Gas & Go property, because the Gas & Go security cameras are functionally decorative and have been since the store was built, a fact Chief Miller has been aware of for the entire nine years of his tenure and has never, to anyone's knowledge, mentioned to the store's corporate ownership.
>
> At 15:14, the driver of the Louisiana pickup hands one of the NexCorp men a small manila envelope. The envelope is not sealed. The envelope contains cash. The NexCorp man puts the envelope in his back pocket. He nods. He says one more thing to the driver. The driver nods back. The window goes up. The two NexCorp men return to the van. The van leaves. The Louisiana pickup stays.
>
> At 15:16, Skip Donnelly looks up from his phone, registers that there is a pickup in his lot that has been in his lot longer than pickups generally stay in his lot, and considers, for maybe three seconds, whether to go out and ask if everything is okay. He decides not to. He decides this because he has, over the course of his life, learned that asking if everything is okay at a gas station is a question that creates paperwork roughly one time in four, and Skip Donnelly has filled out enough paperwork for one lifetime.
>
> He goes back to his phone. The pickup sits.
>
> The driver, at 15:17, reaches down to his glovebox and takes out a small stack of index cards, each with a handwritten address. He shuffles them. He selects one. He puts the card on the passenger seat. He starts the engine. He leaves.
>
> The address on the card, if anyone had seen it, is 892 Ashberry Drive.
>
> Diego Ramos does not live there anymore. Diego Ramos has not lived there since — technically — eleven-fifty-two last night, when he started the truck and pulled out without telling his grandmother.
>
> The driver of the Louisiana pickup does not know this. The driver of the Louisiana pickup has been handed a card by a man who should have updated him, and who has not.
>
> This is, in the system that operates beneath Harmony Creek Estates' visible surface, a kind of error.
>
> Errors compound.

#### 5. *The Cabin, Morning* — Vol 7 opening (Land of Milk and Honey, Ch. 1) — [Vol 7 wiki](lore/_VOL7_WIKI.md)

PNW grunge tempo. Two women up before light. A copper kettle, three SCUMM sticks in waxed paper, Tem's two fingers against Lena's wrist for a beat — the first time Tem has touched her since the April morning at the door. The dialogue is six short lines. The whole scene is in the white space between them.

> Lena woke at the cabin in the gray hour before the rain became visible.
>
> It was the rain she heard first, before she registered the bed, the cabin, the wool blanket across her legs. The rain on the cedar roof had been falling for, by some quick arithmetic she did before she opened her eyes, perhaps thirty hours; she had slept through eight of them; she had been listening to it for twenty-two. The rain in this country in late August was the rain that comes in off the Pacific and decides. The rain had decided.
>
> She opened her eyes.
>
> The window above the bed gave her the gray-green of cedars at maybe five-thirty in the morning. The window had no curtain. The window had not had a curtain in any of the three summers she had been coming up to this cabin and she did not, this morning, want one. The light was the right light. The light at this hour in this room had been one of the small reasons she had said yes when Tem had texted her on Saturday.
>
> She did not get up immediately. She lay on her side and watched the cedars and let the cold of the room arrive at her face by degrees.
>
> The wool blanket was Pendleton, a pattern she had come to associate with the cabin, woven by a mill in Washougal that had closed in 2046 and that the cabin had three blankets from. The blanket smelled like the cabin. The cabin smelled like cedar smoke and kerosene and the rosemary Tem had been burning in a clay dish on the windowsill since Saturday for reasons Tem had not explained.
>
> She got up. She put on the wool socks she had brought up, the jeans she had worn yesterday, the flannel that had been her aunt's before it had been hers. She left the small bedroom and crossed the short hallway to the main room. Tem was at the wood stove. Tem had built the fire back up. The kettle was on.
>
> Tem said, "I was about to wake you. The kettle was going to do it."
>
> Lena said, "I was already up."
>
> Tem said, "Coffee?"
>
> Lena said, "Yeah."
>
> She crossed the room and sat at the small wooden table beside the kerosene lamp. The lamp was not lit; the gray light through the south-facing window was enough. On the table, beside the John Frank novel and the small clay dish with the rosemary, were three SCUMM sticks in waxed paper sleeves. Lena had registered them last night when they had arrived; she had not, last night, asked.
>
> Tem brought the coffee. Tem poured Lena's cup first. When she set the cup down she put two fingers against the back of Lena's wrist for a beat. Just two fingers. Just a beat. Then her hand was gone and she was pouring her own cup and sitting down in the old chair across the table.
>
> Lena did not look up.
>
> The two fingers had been there long enough to register and short enough that, in the thirty seconds after, she could have decided she had imagined it. She had not, in the thirty seconds, decided. The April night was in her body where it had been since April. The April night was a Thursday. They had had coffee at Tem's apartment. Tem had asked her to stay for tea. She had stayed. Tea had become wine. The wine had become bed. The bed had been small and clean and had ended in the gray hour before the rain on the roof became visible, and Tem had made her coffee on the cone with the whistling kettle and had walked her to the door at six-something in the morning and had said *I'll see you Wednesday,* and on Wednesday Tem had been at the Daily Grind for their coffee at the regular hour and neither of them had mentioned the Thursday, and they had not, in the four months since, mentioned it, and they had not, in the four months since, slept together again. The two fingers were the first time Tem had touched her since the April morning at the door.
>
> Lena drank her coffee. Tem drank hers. The two of them sat with the coffee for a minute without speaking. The rain on the cedar roof was steady. The cabin held them.
>
> Lena said, "Tem."
>
> Tem said, "Yeah."
>
> Lena said, "I had a thing happen on the drive up."
>
> Tem said, "Tell me."
>
> Lena said, "I started writing the word *octopus* in my notebook. Around an hour out of town. I did not decide to write it. I just started writing it. I wrote it for forty-five minutes. Forty-one times. Kai counted at one point. He just said *how many.* I told him. He said okay. He kept driving."
>
> Tem said, "Yeah."
>
> Lena said, "Tem."
>
> Tem said, "Yeah."
>
> Lena said, "Has that happened to you."
>
> Tem said, after a moment, "Yes."
>
> Lena said, "When."
>
> Tem said, "Different word. Different time. Same shape."

#### 6. Visual-novel dialogue — *Alberto / Marta phone call* (Vol 5, post-Sinkhole) — [Vol 5 wiki](lore/_VOL5_WIKI.md)

Eight days after the wreck that killed Marta Romero's son in a D'Ambrosio-family work accident, Alberto D'Ambrosio (the elder son, indoors-in-sunglasses, in mourning) calls Marta from his father's hospital bedside. The diorama is the player's first long Alberto scene. It is two people doing the right thing without ever raising their voices. (Excerpted; full diorama is 4 minutes of timestamped dialogue at [godot/scenes/menu/AlbertoMartaCallDiorama.gd](godot/scenes/menu/AlbertoMartaCallDiorama.gd).)

> **MARTA:** Hello.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Hi. Mrs. Romero. This is — this is Alberto. Marroquín. D'Ambrosio.
>
> **MARTA:** *(small pause)* Yes. I know who you are.
>
> **ALBERTO:** I — yes. Okay. I'm sorry. I'm calling — I — okay.
>
> **MARTA:** Take your time, Alberto.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Thank you. I — okay. I'm calling because I want to tell you a few things. I don't expect you to — I'm not asking for anything. I just want to tell you them. Is that all right.
>
> **MARTA:** It's all right.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Okay. The first thing is I know about the deposition. The dates were correct when you gave them. The dates were moved after. I have the original timestamps. I will give them to your lawyer.
>
> **MARTA:** *(quiet)* That's a thing you can do.
>

## CRAFT VOCABULARY

- **Prose style** — clipped, specific, present-tense observational; aversion to expository dialogue.
- **Worldbuilding** — non-human phenomena treated as load-bearing infrastructure; cult and folklore detail researched and applied without ethnographic shorthand.
- **Branching narrative** — gated reveals, cross-referenced hotspots, save-state-aware dialogue, no false choice.
- **Tonal control** — three distinct registers across the same canon; tonal seams are scene transitions, not chapter breaks.
- **Toolchain** — Godot 4 (GDScript), Blender Python, Markdown-first lore corpus, Git-as-canon (every commit message a working note).
