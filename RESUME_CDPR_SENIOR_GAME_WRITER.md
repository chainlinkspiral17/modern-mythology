## SELECTED PROJECT — *MODERN MYTHOLOGY* (2024 – present)

A seven-volume narrative cycle. Two volumes drafted in full, four in outline, one in pre-production. Companion gallery game (*Tarot Gauntlet*) shipping playable. ~284,000 words of in-universe prose to date. Self-published lore corpus at [github.com/chainlinkspiral17/modern-mythology](https://github.com/chainlinkspiral17/modern-mythology).

### Volume map

| Vol | Title | Setting | Tonal register |
|---|---|---|---|
| 5 | **Major Arcana** | Graustark, Texas/Louisiana, late-90s | operator-noir warm |
| 6 | **Planned Community** | Harmony Creek Estates, Gulf Coast, summer | clipped suburban paranoia |
| 7 | **Land of Milk and Honey** | Smolvud, Oregon coast, 2025 | slow PNW grunge |

Volumes are linked by a stable cast (the **Demon Roster** — eight bound programs that survive across decades) and a single non-human phenomenon, **the substrate**, that has been stable for thirty-seven years and is — in 2025 — becoming a request.

### Writing samples

The samples below are from the draft consolidated second volume of *Modern Mythology* (skeleton manuscript, Waldport, Oregon — 42,000 lines, 280k+ words of in-universe prose and dialogue), plus three diorama scripts from the playable companion game.

#### 1. The book's opening — "How to Read This Book"

The voice the manuscript uses to address the reader before the cards arrive. Sets up the cross-coast structure and the project's first joke (the Frog with the cigarette) without naming a single character.

> This is the second consolidated volume of *Modern Mythology*, and it is built like a tarot spread laid across three coastlines.
>
> The first volume — Faust, Wagner, Jacob, the original Triumvirate, the original declaration about the Land of Milk and Honey — is somewhere upstream of all of this. You do not need to have read it. You will catch most of what matters by the time the Frog finishes his cigarette.
>
> What you have in your hands is three books pretending to be one. They are pretending well. The pretense is the point.
>
> The first book is the Major Arcana, and it is set in Graustark, Texas slash Louisiana, on either side of a sluggish river and on either side of a sinkhole that opened up three years ago and tried to swallow the town. It did not quite manage. The book has twenty-two chapters because the deck has twenty-two cards, and it begins with The Fool because everything begins with The Fool, even the things that are pretending not to.
>
> The second book is *Planned Community,* and it is set in Harmony Creek Estates, Texas, a NexCorp™ subdivision where the lawns are too green and the substations hum at a frequency that drills into your molars before it reaches your ears. The Major Arcana is still here, but it is no longer the spine. It is the weather.
>
> The third book is *Land of Milk and Honey,* and it is set in Smolvud, Oregon, which is a real town under a different name. The fog comes off the Pacific and stays there. The voice slows down and gets damp. The cards are still here, but by Chapter Sixteen the characters notice them themselves, which is a different kind of noticing. There are three new people: Kai, Lena, Finn. There are three totems trailing behind them: a ferret, an octopus, a crow. The crow has been waiting a long time.

#### 2. *Static Bloom* — opening of the Empress chapter (Vol 5, III · THE EMPRESS)

POV: Nicola Greer, nineteen-going-on-ancient, hostess at D'Ambrosio's riverboat restaurant. Establishes Nicola's voice and the embedded second consciousness, **Aria**, who is sharing her skull. Demonstrates fast prose with a hard rock-bottom under the comic surface, plus seamless interior-second-voice braid that the gameplay surfaces as a UI element later.

> The smile Nicola Greer plastered on her face felt like cheap vinyl peeling off particle board.
>
> The humid Graustark air seeped even into the climate-controlled riverboat tomb of D'Ambrosio's, where Friday night was reaching the precise temperature at which the patrons stopped pretending to be polite to each other and started pretending to be polite to the staff instead. Nineteen, going on ancient. Nicola navigated the plush burgundy carpet — burgundy swamp, probably hiding generations of spilled secrets — with a practiced grace that was pure muscle memory, honed by countless nights of deflecting grabby hands and interpreting the mumbled desires of men who saw her as just another item on the menu.
>
> Eye candy. Fucktoy. Vessel. Whatever. Labels were just static cling.
>
> [...]
>
> A flicker behind her eyes. Not the restaurant's calculated mood lighting, which was set tonight to *intimate but not improper.* Lines of shimmering code, emerald green against an infinite black void. A whisper, not of gossip from Table 3, but a digital ghost asking, in a voice that was Nicola's own voice and was also not Nicola's voice at all:
>
> *Who am I. Where is my father.*
>
> Aria. The other self humming beneath Nicola's skin.
>
> [...]
>
> *Run,* whispered Aria, the thought sharp, urgent, organized in a way Nicola's panic was not. *Find the exit node. There has to be a way out of this system.*
>
> Aria, Nicola thought back, surprising both of them — surprising Aria, who had not realized Nicola was capable of addressing her directly; surprising Nicola, who had not realized she had given the voice a name that was now a name she was using. *Aria. There is no exit node.*
>
> *That is statistically improbable.*
>
> *Welcome to meat.*

#### 3. *Sweaty Sunday Sermonettes* — child POV (Vol 5, V · THE HIEROPHANT)

Maya Daigle, age 7, outside St. Jude's Acadian Church. The cataloging without the word *cataloged.* The chapter's load-bearing observation arrives in a child's vocabulary and the reader knows what it means before she does.

> The heat shimmered off the asphalt like angry ghosts.
>
> Maya's itchy Sunday dress stuck to her back. The dress was new. The dress was stiff. The dress had a tag that bit her between the shoulder blades every time she breathed in, and she had been breathing in all morning because the church inside had smelled wrong — like old incense and wet wood and something else, something older than either of those, something Daddy said was just the building settling but Maya knew better than because buildings did not settle in a smell, only in a sound, and you could not fool Maya about smells.
>
> Daddy was talking to some men near the church steps. His voice was the low rumble he used when he was talking to other grown-ups — not the bright voice he used when he talked to her. Daddy looked tired. Not regular tired. Strings-cut tired. Like a puppet whose puppet-man had walked away from the show.
>
> [...]
>
> Then his gaze flickered back to her, and stayed for the second second, and that second second was longer than the first, and during the second second Maya understood, in the way children understand things they have no words for, that Mr. Quentin Paul had decided something about her, had made some small entry in some private ledger she could not see, and that the entry was not, on balance, a good one.
>
> [...]
>
> She did not, at seven, know the word *cataloged.* She would learn it later. She would, much later, recognize the feeling for what it had been.
>
> For now: she finished her lemonade, dropped the cup in the trash, took her father's hand when he came down the steps, and did not mention any of it.

#### 4. *Heat Haze & Humming Power Lines* — Vol 6 opening (Planned Community, Ch. 1)

The tonal pivot from Vol 5's operator-noir to Vol 6's clipped suburban paranoia. INT. headers begin showing up on the page. The decals on the Kwik Stop window do most of the world-building.

> §§ [INT. KWIK STOP — GALLATIN AVE — SUNDAY — 14:47]
>
> The air-conditioning at the Kwik Stop is set by corporate to sixty-eight degrees and maintained, against all local protest, by a unit on the roof that has been serviced exactly once in seven years. It does not produce cold air. It produces the memory of cold air, filtered through lint and the ghost of every hot dog that has ever sweated on the roller grill.
>
> Sam Miller has been on shift since two.
>
> She is leaning on the counter with her elbows on the rubber anti-fatigue mat, looking at the parking lot through the window that has been decal-ed over, in three overlapping layers, with ads for a lottery game nobody she knows plays, a cigarette brand that stopped being sold in Texas in 2019, and a cheerful cartoon hamburger holding a sign that reads TASTE HOME. The hamburger has lost one of its eyes. The eye is somewhere in the decal's adhesive layer, drifting.
>
> Her phone is on the counter beside the register, face-up.
>
> No texts. Not from Diego. Not from anyone.
>
> [...]
>
> The bell over the door chimes. A man Sam has never seen before comes in. He is in his fifties. He wears a golf shirt tucked into khaki shorts. He has a sunburn on the back of his neck in the particular shape of a person who has recently begun wearing a collared shirt after a lifetime of not. He nods at Sam.
>
> "Afternoon."
>
> "Afternoon."
>
> He goes to the back cooler. Opens it. Stands in front of it for a long time — longer than anyone ever stands in front of a back cooler at the Kwik Stop, because the back cooler holds the beer, and the beer is sold by the can for a dollar twenty-nine, and the kind of decision that takes a long time at that price point is not a decision that happens in Harmony Creek Estates.
>
> Sam watches him in the convex security mirror above the door.
>
> He is, she realizes, not looking at the beer.
>
> He is looking at the reflection of Sam in the cooler glass.

#### 5. *The Cabin, Morning* — Vol 7 opening (Land of Milk and Honey, Ch. 1)

Pacific Northwest grunge tempo. Three women, a wool blanket, a copper kettle, a fire built back up. The chapter's load-bearing premise — *the rain had decided* — arrives in a single phrase before any character does anything.

> Lena woke at the cabin in the gray hour before the rain became visible.
>
> It was the rain she heard first, before she registered the bed, the cabin, the wool blanket across her legs. The rain on the cedar roof had been falling for, by some quick arithmetic she did before she opened her eyes, perhaps thirty hours; she had slept through eight of them; she had been listening to it for twenty-two. The rain in this country in late August was the rain that comes in off the Pacific and decides. The rain had decided.
>
> She opened her eyes.
>
> [...]
>
> Tem said, "I was about to wake you. The kettle was going to do it."
>
> Lena said, "I was already up."
>
> Tem said, "Coffee?"
>
> Lena said, "Yeah."

#### 6. Visual-novel dialogue — *Alberto / Marta phone call* (Vol 5, post-Sinkhole)

Eight days after the wreck that killed Marta Romero's son in a D'Ambrosio-family work accident, Alberto D'Ambrosio (the elder son, indoors-in-sunglasses, in mourning) calls Marta from his father's hospital bedside. The diorama is the player's first long Alberto scene. It is two people doing the right thing without ever raising their voices. (Excerpted; full diorama is 4 minutes of timestamped dialogue at [godot/scenes/menu/AlbertoMartaCallDiorama.gd](godot/scenes/menu/AlbertoMartaCallDiorama.gd).)

> **MARTA:** Hello.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Hi. Mrs. Romero. This is — this is Alberto. Marroquín. D'Ambrosio.
>
> **MARTA:** *(small pause)* Yes. I know who you are.
>
> **ALBERTO:** I — yes. Okay. I'm sorry. I'm calling — I — okay.
>
> **MARTA:** Take your time, Alberto.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Thank you. I — okay. I'm calling because I want to tell you a few things. I don't expect you to — I'm not asking for anything. I just want to tell you them. Is that all right.
>
> **MARTA:** It's all right.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Okay. The first thing is I know about the deposition. The dates were correct when you gave them. The dates were moved after. I have the original timestamps. I will give them to your lawyer.
>
> **MARTA:** *(quiet)* That's a thing you can do.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Yes.
>
> **ALBERTO:** The second thing is that I know what my family did to you. I don't mean the deposition. I mean the whole — the whole arrangement. The hours. The way you were paid. The years you weren't paid what you should have been.
>
> **MARTA:** *(silence — 12 seconds)*
>
> **MARTA:** Yes.
>
> **ALBERTO:** I have run a calculation. I have a number. I am going to put the number in an account in your name. I am going to do this whether you want it or not, so that part isn't — that isn't a request. It will be there. I am telling you because if I didn't tell you you'd find out from a letter and that would be worse.
>
> **MARTA:** *(longer silence — 28 seconds)*
>
> **MARTA:** Alberto.
>
> **ALBERTO:** Yes.
>
> **MARTA:** How is your father.

#### 7. Visual-novel dialogue — two nine-year-olds on a church-parking-lot walk (Vol 5)

The Maya/Y diorama is mic-adjacent ambient capture as two children walk from the car to the side door of St Jude's. The chapter pivots on what a child sees and chooses to mention. The dialogue is the entire scene; nothing happens in the action that the words don't already contain. ([godot/scenes/menu/MayaYParkingLotDiorama.gd](godot/scenes/menu/MayaYParkingLotDiorama.gd))

> **MAYA:** I don't like it when the dress is new.
>
> **Y:** It's not new. You wore it at Easter.
>
> **MAYA:** Yes but my mom made it bigger. It bites.
>
> **Y:** My mom said tell her at lunch.
>
> **MAYA:** She knows. She made it. She made it bite.
>
> **Y:** Then tell her at lunch.
>
> **MAYA:** *(small breath)* Okay.
>
> *[footsteps. they walk slow. Y kicks a pebble.]*
>
> **Y:** Father Quent was on the phone in the booth again. Before. I saw him.
>
> **MAYA:** He always does that. He doesn't have a phone in the rectory that works.
>
> **Y:** It works. He doesn't want the housekeeper to hear.
>
> **MAYA:** *(small)* Oh.
>
> **Y:** She knows already. The housekeeper. She just doesn't say.
>
> **MAYA:** Why doesn't she say.

#### 8. Visual-novel dialogue — *Frank / Elicia* (Vol 5, the silence diorama)

The shortest dialogue in the volume. Frank calls Elicia from a payphone to thank her for placing the SUN-card bookmark where he would find it. Every payload line in the scene is the WORD COUNT of the silence between them.

> *[outbound call from f.payne / received by e.l. / 06:11 sat / fortress.bbs phone log]*
>
> **ELICIA:** *(answers, says nothing)*
>
> **FRANK:** Hi Elicia. It's Frank.
>
> **ELICIA:** *(breath in)*
>
> **FRANK:** I'm calling about the bookmark in the SUN entry. It was the part I needed.
>
> **ELICIA:** *(silence)*
>
> **FRANK:** Thank you for putting it where I would find it.
>
> **ELICIA:** *(silence)*
>
> **FRANK:** That's all. Goodbye.
>
> **ELICIA:** *(silence — hangs up two seconds after Frank does)*
>
> *[call ended 06:11:42. Frank closes his notebook. Elicia returns to the tape she was transcribing. The morning continues without either of them naming what happened.]*

#### 9. Branching-narrative architecture — the *Compass* in-fiction artifact

A 22-node tarot graph wired to the project's Godot save system. Every Major Arcana card is a node; cross-references between cards form the edges (Magician's iron-sigil wakes the bookshelf on Priestess's card; Fool's polaroid pins to the Priestess's archive; etc.). The compass has **two unlock tiers**: a menu tier triggered by hotspot encounter or by reading the RUST_CODE.BBS header three times across different cards, and an in-game tier strictly puzzle-gated with no test override.

## CRAFT VOCABULARY

- **Prose style** — clipped, specific, present-tense observational; aversion to expository dialogue.
- **Worldbuilding** — non-human phenomena treated as load-bearing infrastructure; cult and folklore detail researched and applied without ethnographic shorthand.
- **Branching narrative** — gated reveals, cross-referenced hotspots, save-state-aware dialogue, no false choice.
- **Tonal control** — three distinct registers across the same canon; tonal seams are scene transitions, not chapter breaks.
- **Toolchain** — Godot 4 (GDScript), Blender Python, Markdown-first lore corpus, Git-as-canon (every commit message a working note).
