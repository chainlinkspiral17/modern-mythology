# HANE NO NIWA (羽の庭 · THE FEATHER GARDEN) · DESIGN
### Yumemi Denshi, Kyoto · 1993 · SHRINE-KEEPING / EPISTOLARY
### four seasons · a neglected shrine · letters from something unseen

Japan's slowstick scene, canonized in one game. Designed by
**Sachiko Ono**, founder of Yumemi Denshi ("dream-seeing
electronics" — the Pacific's answer to Oneironautics' Greek).
Ono and Amélie Rocha corresponded from 1991; those letters are
why Fey Faire's roster reaches to kitsune, tengu, kappa,
yuki-onna, and kodama — Amélie's half of a conversation about
whether the numinous localizes. This game is Ono's half.

Olaf's copy: the 1995 US grey import, with a photocopied fan
translation folded into the case, annotated in two hands. One of
the hands is Amélie's.

---

## THE LOOK

Soft inked line on paper tones, composed vertically like a
hanging scroll — the play area is a tall center panel with wide
paper margins, and the margins are USED: season titles, the
letters, and the feather count live there in vertical text.

Four palettes, one per season — the game re-inks itself:

    SPRING  #f4ece0 paper · #d88a98 blossom · #8aa878 new leaf
            #5a6a8a rain line · #38302a ink
    SUMMER  #ecf0e4 · #4a8a58 cedar · #f0c848 hot light
            #7a98b8 cicada sky · #38302a ink
    AUTUMN  #f0e8d8 · #c85830 momiji · #b89038 gingko
            #6a5a48 smoke · #38302a ink
    WINTER  #f0f0ec · #b8c4d0 snow shadow · #d8dce0 snow
            #4a4a52 bare wood · #38302a ink

One hilltop shrine, eight fixed spots: the torii, the steps
(23), the cistern, the offering shelf, the honden door (never
opens), the camphor tree, the fox statues (two — one is
chipped), and the brazier. Weather redraws everything; the same
eight spots across four seasons is the whole art direction —
thirty-two states of one place.

**HeroImages:** the shrine in each season (4) · a letter,
unfolded, vertical · the feather on the offering shelf.

## THE SOUND

Sparse shrine acoustics: wind in the camphor, the cistern
dipper, one bell. Each season has a two-line melodic phrase on
the soft-sine voice; the phrases are quarter answers to each
other, and only a player who reaches winter hears that the four
of them are one melody. Cicadas in summer, mixed a little too
loud, on purpose, then achingly absent on the first autumn
visit.

## THE PLAYSTYLE

**Four seasons × nine visits.** Verbs: SWEEP · MEND · OFFER ·
SIT.

- SWEEP and MEND are maintenance with visible memory — the steps
  stay swept for a few visits, the rope frays back slowly. The
  shrine keeps a quiet UPKEEP state it never shows as a number:
  the fox statues' expressions read differently at high upkeep,
  and players argue about whether that's real. (It is.)
- **OFFER is the language.** Anything offered goes on the shelf
  and enters the yokai's LEXICON: offer a persimmon and it can
  speak of sweetness; offer a comb, of untangling; offer sake,
  of warmth and of your grandfather; offer nothing all season
  and its letter is shorter and gentler than any other. Twenty
  offerable things, found around the hill or brought (the town
  at the hill's foot is one menu, not a location).
- **THE LETTERS.** One per season, left on the shelf at the
  season's last visit — vertical text, composed from a grammar
  over the player's own offering-lexicon (the promise-reckoning
  lesson applied: since real understanding can't be checked, the
  game builds meaning out of exactly what you gave it). The
  fourth letter contains, regardless of lexicon, one sentence in
  English, uncommented: **"your carrnival is known here also."**
  — with the doubled r. The fan translation's margin note, in
  Amélie's hand: *"she spelled it like that on purpose. ask her
  yourself."* Nothing else in either hand explains it.
- The yokai is never shown. Feather evidence (one per season,
  black then grey then white then gold) suggests a tengu, and
  suggests it is getting closer, or older, or both.
- **Endings** by lexicon shape: a letter of weather · a letter
  of food and small comforts · a letter of grief (if the
  offerings skewed to belongings rather than goods) · and the
  true ending — the fourth letter left BLANK except for a gold
  feather, which the game files as the only letter that says
  everything. Then spring again, NG+: the honden door is open
  exactly one pixel wider. It never opens more.

## THE AMBITION

- Give the catalog its non-US pole and make the localization of
  the numinous a THEME with a game on each shore: the Faire
  travels; the shrine stays; both are visited.
- Canonize the Ono–Rocha correspondence as the reason Fey
  Faire's roster crosses the Pacific — and let the misspelled
  "carrnival" be the catalog's loudest unexplained object.
- The fan-translation-in-the-case is the frame story in
  miniature: an object annotated by two hands, one of which the
  player will recognize with a chill.
- Tokens out: `hane_no_niwa_finished`, `hnn_gold_feather` (Fey
  Faire: Moth's solo line gains one sentence about a garden
  across the sea). Tokens in: `fey_faire_titania_blessing_
  keepsake` — the summer letter adds a clause about roses that
  do not wilt.

## BUILD

Talikan-hub pattern (8 spots × verbs) + the letter composer (a
grammar over lexicon tags — templated clauses, the Codex
manuscript renderer for vertical-ish display) + 32 spot states
(4 seasons × 8, most are palette-swaps + small diffs). Four
phrases + shrine SFX. Moderate; the letter grammar is the real
work and the real point.
