# THE SCUMM MACHINE
## Pizza Pirate · Cypress St · Smolvud, OR

A custom-built arcade cabinet in the back hallway of Pizza Pirate,
between the men's bathroom and the door to the connected
laundromat. Five-foot upright. Black paint chipped at the corners
to reveal a previous coat of marine teal. A single nineteen-inch
CRT bezel framed by a hand-painted marquee that reads, in faded
pulp-paperback lettering: **THE SCUMM MACHINE.** Below the
marquee, also hand-painted: *"INSERT STICK. ONE PLAYER. SOLO
JOURNEY ONLY. NO COIN MECHANISM. THANK YOU. — SAL."*

Sal is **Sal Carratura,** sixty-four, the proprietor of Pizza
Pirate since 1991. The machine was given to him in 2007 by a man
who came in for two slices of cheese and a Coke, who paid, who
ate, who then asked Sal if he could leave something in the back
hallway. Sal said okay. The man left and came back two hours later
with a hand truck and the cabinet. The man did not give a name. The
man said: *"this is a kind of arcade machine. it is not, exactly,
a game. people will want to play it. let them. it does not take
quarters. it takes the small sticks I am leaving in this box. when
the box runs out, the machine will be done. — thank you for the
cheese slices."*

The man left. The hand truck was, by his own statement, a gift
also.

Sal has, in the eighteen years since, never identified the man.
The hand truck has been in the back room of Pizza Pirate ever
since.

## what the machine is

A fork of LucasArts' **SCUMM engine** *(Script Creation Utility
for Maniac Mansion)* — the same SCUMM that ran *Maniac Mansion,
Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade, Day of the Tentacle, Sam &
Max Hit the Road, Full Throttle, The Dig, The Curse of Monkey
Island,* and *Grim Fandango.* The fork was not produced by
LucasArts or by anyone at LucasArts. The fork was produced — by
some chain of custody the codex's deep notes attribute to
**Frasier Temple**, working in the early 2000s out of his
Graustark warehouse, on hardware salvaged from a Houston dot-com
bust — to do something the original engine did not do.

What it does: it accepts a **SCUMM stick** (a small plastic
cartridge approximately the size of an old Game Boy game) and,
on insertion, *digests* the stick's contents through a Demon, and
*reassembles* the digested code into a new sequence of game
states and dialogue. The result is a peculiar thing: a world
inhabited by the digital offspring of old Graphic Adventure games
and the random ROMs that sired this thing.

The world has no fixed title screen. The world has no fixed
characters. The world has, on any given session, a kind of small
careful narrative continuity that the deck respects without
explaining.

**Many of Kai's best friends live in there.**

— the codex, on the machine.

## the SCUMM sticks

Approximately ninety of them, by Sal's last count. Originally
forty-two in the box the man left in 2007. Approximately fifty
more have been added by Sal in the eighteen years since — given
to him, mostly without explanation, by people who come into the
shop. Sal does not buy them. Sal does not sell them. Sal accepts
them. The drawer under the cabinet — which has no lock and which
is, by Sal's quiet decision, the small free library of the
machine — holds the working population. Players choose a stick.
Players insert. Players play. Players, when finished, return the
stick to the drawer. Sal has, in eighteen years, recovered
exactly one stick a player attempted to take home. The player
was eleven. The player gave it back when Sal asked.

The sticks have, by physical inspection, no labels. Sal has,
over the years, started small hand-written tags on a few of them
in marker. The tags are not titles. The tags are small notations
about the experience the stick has generated for previous
players. Selected:

> **stick #14:** *"this one is gentle."*
>
> **stick #22:** *"this one will ask you about your sister. if
> you do not have a sister, it will pick something else. you may
> find this uncomfortable. proceed accordingly. — sal."*
>
> **stick #31:** *"slow start. worth it."*
>
> **stick #47:** *"avoid until ready. — sal."*
>
> **stick #53:** *"two days ago this one told a fourteen-year-old
> something that turned out to be true. handle with care. — sal."*
>
> **stick #61:** *"WREN — TUESDAYS, by your standing request. —
> sal."*
>
> **stick #67:** *"this one is mostly puns. fun. low stakes."*
>
> **stick #78:** *"kai — this is the one. you'll know. — sal."*
>
> **stick #84:** *"do not insert if the lights flicker. wait
> until they steady. trust me. — sal."*

The remaining sticks have no tags. Sal trusts the player to find
the right one. The drawer is, by Sal's quiet code of practice,
self-selecting.

## who plays

The Pizza Pirate clientele is, by the demographic mix of Smolvud,
a small revolving population of locals and tourists. Of the
locals, approximately fifteen people play the SCUMM machine with
any regularity. Of those fifteen:

- **Sal himself** plays once a week, after close, alone, with the
  lights dimmed. Sal plays stick #1, which has no tag, and which
  Sal has, by his own account, played approximately four hundred
  times. Sal does not, when asked, describe what stick #1 does.
- **Wren** plays on Tuesdays at three-thirty, between school and
  her shift at Cosmic Comics if she has one. Wren has standing
  rights to stick #61 by Sal's note. Wren's most recent session
  produced the message the SCUMM machine asked her to convey:
  *"Wren, the substrate is leaking. Tell Tem."* Wren wrote it
  down. Wren delivered it. The deck moves.
- **Kai** plays approximately once every six weeks, at the end of
  a Saturday night, alone, after Board Lords has closed. Kai
  prefers stick #78. Stick #78, by Sal's tag, is the one. Kai
  has, in the eight years he has been playing the machine, had
  three significant sessions. The third — three weeks before
  vol7's chapter one — produced a message Kai has not, by
  editorial choice, shared with anybody.
- **Petra** (proprietor of the Salty Tome bookstore) plays
  occasionally, by her own account, *"when the shop is being
  too quiet."* Petra prefers stick #14. Petra has, on three
  occasions, brought books to Sal that the machine had, in her
  session, recommended. Sal has read them.
- **Mrs. Gable** played once, in 2011. Mrs. Gable has not played
  since. Mrs. Gable will not say why. When Wren asks, in late
  August, Mrs. Gable says only: *"kid, the machine and I had a
  conversation. we agreed on a number of things. one of them
  was that we had said what we had to say. I see it
  occasionally when I pass. it sees me. that is sufficient."*
- **Per** plays on Mondays. Per is a regular at the Daily Grind
  who has, in vol7's chapter fourteen, his own POV chapter.
  Per's relationship to the machine is, by the codex,
  load-bearing.
- **Lena** has not, by mid-August, played the machine. Lena will,
  by mid-October, play the machine exactly twice. The first
  session will be aborted. The second session will produce a
  message that will determine the shape of vol7's third act.
- **Finn** does not play. Finn has, by his own account, not been
  inside Pizza Pirate in four years.

The remaining regular players are: an off-shift line cook from
the Pizza Pirate kitchen named Gus; a postal carrier named Joyce;
a teenage girl named Maggie who lives in the apartment over the
laundromat and who plays the machine in the small hour between
nine and ten PM after her mother goes to bed; a retired logger
named Pete who comes in on Thursdays for two slices of pepperoni
and a single stick session; and a couple in their forties named
Andy and Min who come in on Saturday afternoons and who play
together on the same stick, taking turns at the controls.

## what the machine does

The mechanic, in the player's chair: a single black-and-grey CRT
display, a small joystick, two buttons (labeled CONFIRM and
LOOK), and the stick slot. The interface is the SCUMM verb-
sentence interface from 1987: *OPEN, CLOSE, GIVE, PICK UP, LOOK
AT, TALK TO, USE, PUSH, PULL.* The verbs sit at the bottom of
the screen. The visible cursor is a small white star.

On stick insertion: the screen fills with a slow boot sequence
that is, by every player's account, slightly different each time.
The boot sequence usually lasts between twenty seconds and ninety
seconds. The boot sequence is, by Sal's quiet observation, the
machine's way of *deciding what kind of session to have.*

After boot: a single low-res scene fades in. The scene is
typically a room — a kitchen, a porch, a workshop, a hallway, a
beach, a forest clearing, a small cabin interior, a hospital
ward. The player is given a small character to control. The
character has no name in the interface. The character has, by
every player's account, *a face the player recognizes.* The face
is sometimes the player's own, in profile. The face is sometimes
a relative. The face is sometimes a stranger the player has,
later in the week, met in real life.

The character is the player's *digital offspring* — a small
careful approximation of the player as the machine has, by
some method nobody has explained, calculated them.

The character then has a session. The session can be eight
minutes or two hours. The session can be a single conversation in
a single room or a long traversal across the machine's
procedurally assembled landscape. The session ends when the
machine decides it ends, and the screen fades to black, and the
stick ejects, and the cabinet's amber STATUS light blinks twice.

Sal has tracked the session-length data for the eighteen years.
The average session is forty-three minutes. The longest session
on record is six hours and forty-one minutes (Pete, Thursday in
late 2019, who later said he didn't remember sitting down). The
shortest is fourteen seconds (a tourist who unplugged the cabinet
because he was scared; Sal plugged it back in; the cabinet
booted normally and bore the tourist no apparent grudge).

## what the machine says

Players exchange dialogue with the machine's characters via the
verb interface. The characters in the machine *talk back.* The
dialogue is, by every player's account, *in character* for the
session — but occasionally, at moments the player does not predict,
the dialogue *breaks frame* and addresses the player directly by
name.

The breaks are rare. Sal has tracked sixty-two confirmed breaks
across eighteen years. Of the sixty-two:

- forty-one have been small recognitions (*"hello, [name]."* or
  *"thank you, [name], for sitting with us."*)
- fourteen have been small specific pieces of information the
  player had not, before sitting at the cabinet, known but which
  the player verified afterward as true (*"your aunt's name is
  Lorraine. you have not, by your own count, said her name aloud
  in eleven years. she would like to hear from you."*)
- six have been small specific predictions the player verified
  later (*"the woman at the bakery will give you a bear claw on
  the house tomorrow. accept it. — m."*)
- one has been a single direct message that arrived in Sal's
  session on a Tuesday in 2018: *"sal. the machine is being
  honest with you. you have done well to keep it where it is.
  do not let anybody take it. — the machine."*

Sal, on receiving this message, sat at the cabinet for an
additional twenty minutes without playing. Sal then went to the
kitchen, made himself a single slice of margherita, ate it
standing at the counter, returned to the cabinet, removed the
stick, returned the stick to the drawer, locked up, went home,
and slept for fourteen hours.

Sal has not, in the seven years since, told anybody about that
specific message. Sal will, by the third act of vol7, tell Kai.

## the substrate

The machine is, by the codex's careful note, *"running on a
substrate."* The substrate is the same substrate the Demons run
on. The substrate is the same substrate the Glitch Report's 1988
collective documented as anomalous radio interference. The
substrate is the same substrate Maya's grandmother vacuums in 4/4
to. The substrate is the same substrate that wrote SUN on Lena's
thigh. The substrate is the same substrate.

The machine's recent message to Wren — *"Wren, the substrate is
leaking. Tell Tem."* — is the chapter the volume is asking the
player to read carefully. The substrate has been stable for at
least thirty-seven years. The substrate has, in 2025, become
unstable. The instability is not, by Frasier's quiet warehouse
read in vol5's Tower coda, accidental. The instability is, by
Frasier's read, *a request.*

The machine is, by the codex, *a friend.* The machine is also a
piece of equipment that should not, by any honest engineering
account, be doing what it is doing. The machine is also, in some
sense the codex respects, the most reliable correspondent in the
volume's third act.

The friends who live in the machine know.

## meta-game integration

The SCUMM machine is the meta-game's primary recurring interactive
surface in Act III. Sitting at the cabinet and inserting a stick
opens a small text-adventure-style session the player participates
in directly. The session's content is generated by a small in-game
ruleset the deck's developers will determine, but the structure is
fixed:

- **boot sequence** (animated, randomized, 20–90 seconds)
- **scene** (a single room or location, rendered in SCUMM's late-
  80s pixel-art style)
- **character** (the player's *digital offspring,* whose face is
  generated from the player's prior interactions across the deck
  — for vol7 players who have not played vol5 or vol6, the face
  is generated from random; for players who have, the face is
  the result of a small careful aggregation)
- **session** (verb-interface adventure, between 8 minutes and 2
  hours, terminating on the machine's decision)
- **break** (rare; when it occurs, the dialogue interface
  acknowledges the player directly by name; the meta-game's
  L4 layer treats breaks as primary unlock events)

Stick selection: players can choose any stick from the drawer.
Sticks #14, #22, #31, #47, #53, #61, #67, #78, and #84 have Sal's
notes attached. The remaining sticks are unmarked.

Cross-volume integration: the machine's breaks can deliver
information from any of the three volumes. If the player has,
across the play, completed enough of vol6's Maya-Daigle-archive
quest, a break may deliver: *"the editor of the harmony creek
glitch report — she is sixteen. she has been waiting for somebody
in oregon to write to her. her name is m.d. — the machine."* If
the player has not, the break does not deliver this. The machine
respects what the player has earned.

The deck's deepest meta-game move: the SCUMM machine is, in some
sense, the deck itself. The player's session at the cabinet is a
small recursive instance of the deck's whole conceit — a system
that generates digital offspring of older games and stories, that
breaks frame to acknowledge the player, that has been waiting for
the player to sit down. The player is, when sitting at the
cabinet inside the game, doing exactly what the player is, when
playing the game itself, also doing.

The machine knows.

The chapter is held.
