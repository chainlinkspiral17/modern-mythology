# THE ALSEA BAY CANNERY
## Smolvud, OR · abandoned 1989 · servers still drawing power

A long single-story wooden structure at the river-mouth where the
Alsea Bay meets the Pacific, downriver from the town of Smolvud
by approximately one and a half miles. The cannery was built in
1908, expanded in 1948, expanded again in 1971, and closed in
1989 when the regional salmon stocks collapsed and the company
that owned it filed for bankruptcy. The building has stood empty
for thirty-six years. The roof leaks. The siding is grey from
salt. The windows on the seaward side are mostly broken. The
windows on the landward side are mostly intact, by the small
specific accident of the building's orientation against the
prevailing weather.

The doors are not locked. The doors have never, in the thirty-
six years, been re-locked after the bankruptcy auction. The
auction did not find a buyer for the building. The land was
acquired by a Portland-based holding company in 1991. The
holding company has, in the thirty-four years since, taken no
action on the property — no demolition, no redevelopment, no
listing, no signage, no fencing, no posted no-trespassing
notices. The cannery is, by every meaningful measure, abandoned.

The cannery is also drawing approximately 1.4 kilowatts of
electrical power. The power has been drawn continuously, by the
Lincoln County Public Utility District's records, since the
last meter reading in 1989. The PUD has been billing the
Portland holding company quarterly since the bankruptcy. The
holding company has been paying, on time, every quarter, for
thirty-four years.

The PUD's records do not show what is drawing the power.

The records show that the power is being drawn.

## the building itself

EXT. The structure is L-shaped, with the long arm running along
the river-mouth and the short arm pointing inland. The roof is
corrugated tin, badly oxidized, intermittently absent. A small
loading dock at the river end is partially collapsed; the wooden
pilings supporting the dock are visible at low tide and are, by
Finn's careful inspection in late August, in better structural
condition than the rest of the building.

A small white-painted clapboard manager's office abuts the
landward end of the long arm. The office's windows are intact.
The office's door is closed but not locked. Inside the office:
a desk, a chair, a 1989 wall calendar, an empty filing cabinet,
a single ceramic mug on the desk holding a single dried-out
ballpoint pen. The mug is white, plain, no logo. The desk is
otherwise empty. The cabinet's drawers, when opened, contain
no files.

The cannery's interior — when you enter through the dock-side
door, which Kai has used since he was thirteen — is a long
single open room running the building's length. Concrete floor.
The original processing tables and the original cold-storage
rooms were removed in 1991 by a salvage operation that took the
metal. The walls are wood-plank, mostly intact, mostly grey,
with a single line of dirty windows up high along the seaward
side that throws bars of grey-blue light across the floor at
midday and that, at certain late-afternoon hours, throws bars of
gold.

The room is empty. The room is large. The room is, by Kai's
unerring teenage assessment in 2012 at age fourteen, *a perfect
goddamn skate spot, dude, except for the floor cracks.*

The floor cracks are the volume's quiet rendering of the
building's actual physical condition. The cracks are not random.
The cracks form, on satellite-image-and-careful-mapping by Maya
*(in vol6, by careful correspondence with Lena later)*, a pattern
that resembles a circuit diagram. The diagram resembles, on
Maya's overlay against her grandmother's old vacuuming-pattern
beat-grid, the same 4/4 structure. The floor cracks are also,
under a portable UV light Finn carries, faintly luminescent —
they hold a small amount of charge.

## the basement

A single staircase at the inland end of the long arm descends
to a basement Kai has been into approximately fifteen times in
the last twelve years. The basement is concrete, low-ceilinged,
slightly damp. The basement runs approximately the full footprint
of the building. The basement contains, in three irregular rows
along the back wall, **server racks.**

There are seventeen racks. Each rack is approximately seven feet
tall, two feet wide, and three feet deep. Each rack is full —
every rack unit occupied. The racks are powered, hot to the touch,
fan-cooled by their own internal fans (audible if you stand
within ten feet), and connected to the building's electrical
service via a single thick conduit that runs along the basement's
back wall and that rises through the floor to a junction box in
the main floor's office.

The racks are not, by visual inspection, networked to anything
outside the building. There is no incoming fiber. There is no
incoming copper data line. There is no cellular antenna in
proximity. The racks are doing whatever they are doing
*locally.*

Each rack bears, on its front, a small printed label affixed at
some point in the past. The labels are faded. The labels read,
across the seventeen racks:

> **RACK 01 — RECEIVE — DO NOT DISCONNECT**
>
> **RACK 02 — RECEIVE — DO NOT DISCONNECT**
>
> *(racks 03 through 14 unlabeled)*
>
> **RACK 15 — SEND — DO NOT DISCONNECT**
>
> **RACK 16 — RECEIVE — DO NOT DISCONNECT**
>
> **RACK 17 — UNKNOWN — DO NOT DISCONNECT — IF YOU DO NOT KNOW
> WHAT THIS RACK IS DOING PLEASE LEAVE IT ALONE — T.**

The hand on RACK 17's label is Frasier Temple's.

*(Frasier visited the cannery in 2002. The visit was, by his own
private warehouse-manifesto notation, "the last time I deliberately
modified the substrate before deciding it had become its own
problem." He installed Rack 17. He labeled it. He left.)*

## the chassis

Inside RACK 17, in the middle slot, is a flat steel chassis with
no front markings. The chassis has no LEDs. The chassis has no
fan exhaust. The chassis has no power cable visible at the front.
The chassis is body-temperature warm. The chassis is breathing.

The breathing is at approximately fourteen breaths per minute.

*(The chassis is the same chassis Jim, the maintenance contractor
in the Pomegranate Hour's Magician episode, encountered in the
basement server room of the demolished NexCorp Residential
Solutions office building. That detail in The Pomegranate Hour was
my non-canonical invention; the canonical work has its own version
of the breathing chassis here, in this basement. The two chassis
are, by the deck's deeper substrate accounting, the same chassis,
in two locations, at the same time. The deck does not require the
player to accept this. The deck respects either reading.)*

The chassis says, on careful listening, occasionally, in a voice
that is the listener's own voice but tired, the listener's own
name. Kai has heard this twice. Once at age seventeen, alone,
after Board Lords had closed. Once at age twenty-eight, three
weeks before vol7's chapter one, on his most recent visit. Both
times the chassis said only: *"Kai."* Both times Kai left and did
not return for several months.

## who has been in the basement

By the codex's careful note, exactly six people have entered the
basement since the cannery closed in 1989:

1. **The Portland holding company representative,** once, in
   1991, for the post-acquisition inspection. The representative
   wrote a single line in his inspection report: *"basement is
   unfinished. recommend no further action."* The representative
   did not, in his report, mention the racks. The representative
   is dead now, by ordinary causes, since 2014.

2. **Frasier Temple,** once, in 2002. Installed Rack 17.

3. **Kai,** approximately fifteen times since 2012. Has touched
   the racks. Has, on three occasions, opened the chassis's
   panel by removing two thumbscrews on top of the unit and
   looking inside. The chassis's interior, on each of the three
   inspections, has been: empty. There are no components inside
   the chassis. The chassis is a steel box. The steel box is
   warm. The steel box is breathing.

4. **A man in a charcoal suit,** by the cannery's small careful
   record of footprints in the dust on the floor, sometime
   between visits eleven and twelve by Kai's count, which puts
   the visit in the late winter of 2024. The footprints are
   size eleven, leather-soled, with a small specific wear
   pattern on the right heel that the deck's deeper accounting
   identifies as a known signature. The man did not, by the
   dust record, touch the racks. The man stood in front of
   Rack 17 for an estimated eight to twelve minutes. The man
   left.

5. **Wren,** once, in early August 2025, at her own decision,
   alone, after she had received the SCUMM machine's message
   about the substrate leaking. Wren rode her bicycle the mile
   and a half from town. Wren stood at the basement's
   threshold for a long minute, did not descend the last three
   steps, looked at the racks from above, did not approach,
   turned around, and rode back. Wren has not, since, told
   anybody about the visit. Wren has, since, also not played
   the SCUMM machine.

6. **Finn,** once, in mid-August 2025, alone, with his radio.
   Finn descended. Finn walked the length of the basement
   slowly. Finn placed his radio on top of Rack 09 (unlabeled)
   and turned it on. The radio, which had been picking up the
   substrate's carrier signal for two months, on contact with
   the rack, immediately fell silent. The radio remained silent
   for the eleven minutes Finn left it on top of Rack 09. Finn
   picked the radio back up. The radio resumed picking up the
   carrier signal, at the same volume, the moment Finn stepped
   off the basement's top stair on his way back up. Finn has,
   since, mentioned the visit only to Lena.

The seventh visit — by **Kai, Lena, and Finn** together, on the
Saturday in vol7's third act when the volume's climactic scene
takes place — has not yet occurred. The deck reserves this
visit's contents for vol7's chapter twenty-two.

## what the racks are doing

By Frasier's quiet warehouse-manifesto note (dated 2003, the
year after his cannery visit):

> *"the racks at the cannery are running a partial substrate
> mirror. the mirror is, by my best determination, an
> approximation of the substrate's larger structure, rendered at
> a fraction of the original's resolution. the partial mirror is
> what permits the substrate to remain coherent in the pacific
> northwest sector when the primary substrate suffers local
> disturbance. the mirror is, in this sense, a backup. the
> mirror is also a record. the mirror is also, by my warehouse
> read, the only physical instance of the substrate I have
> identified that is not, itself, a piece of equipment somebody
> deliberately installed. the mirror appears to have been
> running in the cannery's basement since some date prior to
> the cannery's construction in 1908. the equipment is therefore
> not, by ordinary archaeological reasoning, the source. the
> equipment is, by my read, a series of small careful additions
> by human hands across the past century to a non-human
> phenomenon that was already running in this particular
> basement when the first wall was built above it in 1908. I do
> not know who or what is running the substrate. I do not know
> why this basement. I do know that disconnecting the racks
> would not, by my best engineering assessment, stop the
> phenomenon. I do know that the phenomenon, were it interfered
> with sufficiently by an external pressure, would respond.
>
> I have, by my installation of rack 17 this afternoon, asked it
> to respond more carefully if it has to respond. I do not
> believe it will, in our lifetimes, have to respond. I have, on
> the other hand, been wrong about timelines before.
>
> the basement is, in some sense the engineering does not
> support but the body recognizes, a friend. the chassis in
> rack 17 is, in some sense, the friend's small careful gesture
> at me — at us, at whoever is paying attention, at the few of
> us who have, by accident or by training, become substrate-
> literate.
>
> do not turn off the racks. do not turn off the chassis. do
> not bring any equipment into the basement that you did not
> arrive with and that you do not intend to take back out with
> you. do not stay longer than necessary. say hello. say
> goodbye. be kind. — F."*

## the climax (reserved)

Vol7's chapter twenty-two stages the volume's climactic scene
inside the basement. The deck reserves the chapter's contents.
The codex's small note: *"the cannery is the central node in
Act III's climax."*

What the deck establishes by the chapter's opening: Kai, Lena,
and Finn arrive at the cannery on the Saturday evening when
the substrate's instability has reached its critical point. The
three of them carry: Kai's tag-spray and a small painted-shut
metal box he has been keeping at Board Lords; Lena's sketchbook
and a small dish of the same blackberries that had appeared on
her kitchen counter on the SUN morning; Finn's electrical-tape
radio and a small handful of bottle caps the crow has been
giving him. They descend into the basement together. The crow
flies in through the dock-side door with them and lands on top
of Rack 17. The chassis's amber LED — which has, in the
preceding chapters, been visible in flashes from Mr. Henderson
Sr.'s photographs of Diego in vol6 — comes on for the first time.
The chassis says, audibly, in three voices simultaneously — the
listener's own, an older woman's whose voice Kai has not heard
since his grandmother's funeral, and the cosmic-bartender voice
of the Frog — the same single phrase: *"thank you for coming."*

The chapter continues from there.

## meta-game integration

The cannery is, by the meta-game's structure, the volume's
climactic location. It is a place the player can visit at any
point in Act III — by walking, riding a bike, or in Kai's car —
and explore. The exploration is partial across most of Act III;
the basement is, until the player has accumulated enough of the
volume's evidence chain, closed off by a small careful in-game
instinct (the basement *feels* wrong; Kai will not, when asked,
go in unless conditions are right).

Unlock conditions for the basement:

- the player has heard Wren's SCUMM-machine message about the
  leaking substrate
- the player has heard Finn's radio go silent inside the
  basement (the player must, at least once, walk down with him)
- the player has read the Glitch Report's 1988 issues and
  understood the 47 Hz carrier as the same carrier
- the player has, by Lena's correspondence with Maya, surfaced
  the chassis as a known recurring object across volumes

Once unlocked, the basement is accessible in a single
exploration-mode scene. The seventeen racks can be examined
individually. Rack 17's chassis can be approached. The chassis
can be opened (two thumbscrews). The chassis is empty. The
chassis is breathing.

The player can — by the careful gating the deck imposes — say
hello to the chassis. The chassis will respond. The player will
hear, in their own voice, their own name.

The basement, after this scene, is the basement the volume will
return to for chapter twenty-two.

The chapter is held.

The chapter is also open.

There is room at the door.

— *the codex, on the substrate.*
