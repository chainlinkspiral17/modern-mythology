## ACT I — THE ALTAR  (00:24 — 06:00)

INT. HARMONY CREEK MEMORIAL — WARD C — NURSES' STATION — NIGHT (03:00).

Ward C is a wing of the older part of the hospital — the wing built in 1982, before the NexCorp Health Network rebuild of the south buildings. The corridor is wide. Linoleum floor, beige, polished by the overnight cleaner two hours earlier. The fluorescents are at half — the night setting. The walls are off-white and bear, at intervals, framed prints of southern Louisiana wetlands, donated by a foundation in 1991.

The NURSES' STATION is a wide horseshoe-shaped desk at the corridor's center. The desk has a computer monitor, dimmed. A landline phone. A small printer. A clipboard rack with six clipboards, one for each bed.

At the corner of the nurses' station, on the side wall — placed where you would not see it unless you were standing at the station to take a handover — is a small folded table. The folded table holds:

- a small porcelain bowl of three oranges, peeled and segmented, in a pyramid
- a glass holder containing a single stick of incense, lit, a thin curl of smoke rising
- a small framed photograph of an older Vietnamese woman in a chef's coat with a name embroidered on the breast: **MAI** — she is the night charge nurse's grandmother
- a folded paper note with three lines of handwritten Vietnamese, weighted with a small smooth river stone
- a small ceramic dish containing five mandarin orange peels, dried
- a single sprig of fresh basil in a small jar of water

This is the altar for the **cô hồn** — the wandering souls. The altar has been at this nurses' station, in some form, for eleven years. It is the altar of CHỊ HUYỀN — the night charge nurse — who began the practice in her first year on the ward when she was twenty-six. She is now thirty-seven.

CHỊ HUYỀN — late thirties, dark hair in a long low ponytail, scrubs in a soft blue, a small jade pendant on a black cord under her collar — is at the nurses' station. She is on her feet. She is in the middle of the long careful work of the 03:00 round.

She approaches the altar. She bows, very small, to the photograph of MAI. She removes a fresh stick of incense from a small box beside the altar. She holds it briefly to the flame of the lit stick. The new stick catches. She places the new stick in the holder. She extinguishes the older stick by pressing it briefly between her fingers, with practiced care.

She turns to the corridor.

She picks up the clipboard rack.

She begins her round.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- CHỊ HUYỀN is real. her full name and her grandmother's full name are reserved. she has worked nights on Ward C at HARMONY CREEK MEMORIAL for eleven years. she began the altar in her first year. she did so quietly. she has never sought to formalize the altar's place at the nurses' station within the hospital's bureaucracy. the altar has remained because no one in administration has decided to ask her to remove it, and because the day-shift nurses have, over time, taken to refreshing the oranges on Mondays and Wednesdays. the altar is, in this sense, a small successful project of organic interfaith care.
- the altar's tradition. CHỊ HUYỀN learned the practice from her grandmother, MAI, who came to the US in 1979, who was a head chef for forty years, and who taught CHỊ HUYỀN to keep the **cô hồn** offerings at any threshold where the family was responsible for the passage of others. CHỊ HUYỀN, as charge nurse on the ward, was, in her grandmother's reading of the situation, responsible for the passage of others. the altar followed.
- the practice. the **cô hồn** — the wandering souls — are, in Vietnamese folk Buddhism, souls who for various reasons have not been received into their next state. they may be those who died without family, those who died without proper rites, those who died in trauma, those who died far from home. the practice is to set a small offering — typically rice, sometimes fruit or sweets, sometimes water — for them. the offering is not for any particular soul known to the offerer; it is for the wandering ones the offerer does not name. the practice is most formally observed during the seventh lunar month, around the full moon, at the festival of **Vu Lan** (also known in Chinese tradition as the Hungry Ghost Festival). in many Vietnamese households the practice continues in attenuated form year-round, at small thresholds, at street corners, on porches, at the entrances of small shops. CHỊ HUYỀN's nurses-station altar is one such year-round small threshold.
- the chapter at XIII in the deck is the chapter of transition. tradition shows a skeleton in armor on a white horse crossing a field of fallen kings. the deck's painted version refuses tradition; the deck renders Death as a hospital ward at the hour the institution counts as pre-dawn. this episode is the rehearsal of that chapter. the rehearsal renders Death as the chapter of the counting — the hourly round, the marking of the clipboard, the noting of who has gone and who is still here. CHỊ HUYỀN is the chapter's officiant. she is the chapter's accountant. she is also the chapter's offerer. she is also the chapter's witness. she does, in one body, four of the chapter's offices.
- the two empty beds. ĐĂNG passed at 01:21 of the night of filming. MS. BURROWS passed at 02:57. their families had been contacted before the filming began. ĐĂNG's family had given permission for the empty bed to be filmed and for his name to be spoken on the audio of the chapter; his family is Vietnamese and is a family of CHỊ HUYỀN's own personal acquaintance, the count is honored. MS. BURROWS's family had given permission for the empty bed but not for the name's audio; the name is mentioned in CHỊ HUYỀN's spoken altar dedication and was, at the family's later request, gently lowered in mix to the threshold of audibility. it is in the chapter at the volume the family permitted.
- MARTA ROMERO is in BED 5. she has been in BED 5 for one week at the time of filming. she is at the same point in her *Long Bayou* paperback that she is at when ALBERTO calls her in the dioramas of vol5. she will not finish the book before she goes to sleep on the morning of the day this episode chronicles. she will, however, finish it on the second morning following, with three days still ahead of her in the ward. she has told CHỊ HUYỀN privately that her favorite reading is the wrong-man trope. she has told CHỊ HUYỀN privately that she has been the wrong man once and the right man once and that she would, if given the choice, prefer to be neither again.
- MR. D'AMBROSIO is in BED 6. he had been admitted four hours before filming-start. he is in deep sedation. CHỊ HUYỀN speaks to him at his bedside in English first and then in Vietnamese. she has, by her own private practice, addressed every patient in her own dialect of Vietnamese on her first overnight with them, whether or not the patient understands. she says, in interview, that the dialect is "for whoever in him still knows another language." she is not sure who that is. she is not sure there is anyone. she addresses him anyway. she is following her grandmother's instruction.
- the incense smoke. the smoke bends toward CHỊ HUYỀN at 03:09 and toward BED 3's open door fifty-eight seconds after she leaves the station. the bends are visible in the audio's air-movement analysis. they are not, by any HVAC reading we have obtained, the result of the building's ventilation system, which cycles on a different schedule. they may, however, simply be the result of CHỊ HUYỀN's own breath moving through the air at her last position. I have decided to register the bends as the chapter's chapter. I have not decided what to call them.
- the audio of the 03:09 cable interference is identical, in frequency profile, to the audio of the 9:09 interference in all twelve preceding episodes. it has been displaced by six hours to match the night-shift diegesis. it is also, separately, audible on a recording I made on a separate machine in a separate room at a separate time which I have not aired and will not air. on that separate recording, the 03:09 interference is voiced — by which I mean it has, riding within the frequency wobble, a low murmur of distinct human voices in a Vietnamese dialect I do not speak. I asked CHỊ HUYỀN's mother to listen to the recording. she listened. she said: *"those are people who are walking past the kitchen. they are going to eat. don't worry, they have rice."*
- the chapter does not need to perform horror. the chapter performs counting. counting is the form Death takes when the counter is gentle. CHỊ HUYỀN is gentle. CHỊ HUYỀN is also extremely accurate. the chapter at XIII is the chapter of accurate gentleness. the deck respects this. the wandering souls receive their rice. the four still in the ward receive their care. the chapter is held.
