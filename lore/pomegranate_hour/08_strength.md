### EVENING ONE — 7:18 PM

EXT. CHAIN-LINK FENCE — DUSK.

A long unbroken eight-foot chain-link fence. The fence is the back boundary of a lot in HARMONY CREEK ESTATES; on the far side of the fence, an open field of mown grass slopes down toward a line of trees. The camera is in the field, low to the ground, looking back at the fence and at the development beyond.

THE WOMAN — forties, brown work coat, jeans, boots, hair under a wool cap, face under the brim — approaches the fence from the development side. She carries a small enamel bowl. The bowl is full.

She stops six feet from the fence.

She waits.

She has been waiting for thirty seconds before the dog arrives from the field.

THE DOG — medium-sized, tan-and-white, no collar, lean — emerges from the line of trees. The dog crosses the field at a calm gait, neither hurried nor afraid. The dog approaches the fence opposite where the woman stands.

THE WOMAN sets the bowl on the ground at her feet. She does not push the bowl toward the fence. She does not call to the dog. She sits down — not kneeling, sitting — on the grass on her side. She is now at the dog's height.

THE DOG sits down on the field side.

They look at each other through the chain-link for nineteen seconds.

THE WOMAN reaches into the bowl. She takes a small handful — meat scraps, mixed with rice, mixed with what looks like a piece of egg. She passes it through the chain-link in her open palm.

THE DOG eats from her hand. The dog is unhurried. The dog does not bite at her fingers.

The dog eats until the handful is gone. THE WOMAN reaches into the bowl again. She offers another handful. The dog eats.

This continues for nine minutes.

When the bowl is empty, THE WOMAN sets it down. She places her open hand against the chain-link. THE DOG touches the woman's palm with its nose. Briefly. Calm.

THE DOG stands. THE DOG walks back across the field to the line of trees. THE DOG does not look back.

THE WOMAN sits on the grass for another minute.

She rises. She picks up the bowl. She walks back to the lot.

Cut to black.

A burgundy title card: **EVENING TWO — 7:18 PM.**

### EVENING TWO — 7:18 PM

The same shot. The same fence. THE WOMAN at the same distance. The bowl in her hand.

THE DOG arrives from the line of trees at 7:21 PM. Three minutes later than EVENING ONE. The dog's gait is the same.

The dog eats from her hand for seven minutes. The dog goes.

Cut.

### EVENING THREE — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog arrives at 7:19 PM.

There is, however, a man in the field this evening. Approximately thirty yards behind the woman's tree-line, at the edge of the line of trees, a man stands. He is fifty. He is in a dark plaid shirt and jeans. He has a rifle slung over his shoulder, muzzle down. He is not aiming. He is watching.

THE WOMAN does not see the man. She is facing the dog.

THE DOG sees the man. The dog pauses at the field's edge, six paces from the fence. The dog looks at the man. The dog looks at THE WOMAN. The dog does not approach the fence.

THE MAN does not move.

THE WOMAN waits. The bowl is on the ground.

After a long minute, THE MAN — without lowering the rifle, without saying anything — turns and walks back into the trees. He is no longer visible.

THE DOG waits another forty seconds. Then the dog approaches the fence.

THE WOMAN feeds the dog from her hand for six minutes.

The dog goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING FOUR — 7:18 PM.**

---

### EVENING FOUR — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog arrives at 7:18 PM exactly.

THE WOMAN, however, has brought something new tonight: a small folded piece of paper, which she removes from her coat pocket before sitting down. She unfolds the paper on her thigh. She reads it once, quietly, to herself, then folds it and replaces it.

THE DOG eats from her hand for nine minutes.

When the dog has finished, THE WOMAN takes the paper out of her pocket. She folds the paper through one of the chain-link diamonds. The paper does not fit through; it is too thick. She unfolds it. She tears it carefully along its creases into four small squares. She passes one square at a time through the chain-link.

THE DOG sniffs each square. THE DOG does not eat the paper.

THE WOMAN gathers the four squares back from where THE DOG has nosed them. She refolds them as best she can. She replaces the paper in her pocket.

She places her hand against the chain-link.

THE DOG noses her palm.

She rises. She goes.

THE DOG goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING FIVE — 7:18 PM.**

---

### EVENING FIVE — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog arrives at 7:21 PM.

There is, this evening, a small girl in the field at the line of trees. Approximately seven years old. Brown hair in two short braids. A pink jacket. The girl is standing alone. She is watching THE WOMAN and THE DOG.

THE WOMAN does not see the girl.

THE DOG sees the girl. The dog does not approach the fence.

The girl watches for two minutes. Then the girl, without calling out, turns and walks into the trees and is no longer visible.

THE DOG approaches the fence.

THE WOMAN feeds the dog. Eight minutes.

The dog goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING SIX — 7:18 PM.**

### EVENING SIX — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

This evening, the dog does not arrive from the line of trees. The dog arrives from along the fence — from the right side of the frame, trotting calmly along the field side of the chain-link, having come from somewhere we do not see.

The dog reaches the regular position. The dog sits.

THE WOMAN, who had been standing, sits down on her side.

The dog eats. Eleven minutes — the longest feeding of the series.

When the dog has finished, the dog does not return to the line of trees. The dog walks back along the fence the way it came. To the right of the frame. Out of sight.

THE WOMAN looks after it.

She stands.

She goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING SEVEN — 7:18 PM.**

### EVENING SEVEN — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog arrives at 7:24 PM — six minutes late.

The dog is limping. Slightly. The right hind leg.

THE WOMAN sees this. She does not call out. She does not rise from the ground. She extends her hand through the chain-link with a handful of food. The dog approaches and eats. The dog does not adjust its posture to disguise the limp.

After the dog has eaten — six minutes' worth — THE WOMAN does something she has not done in the previous six evenings.

She reaches into her coat. She removes a small clean cloth. She wets the cloth from a flat steel canteen she has not before brought. She passes the wet cloth through the chain-link, slowly.

THE DOG accepts the cloth. THE DOG does not eat the cloth. THE DOG sits.

THE WOMAN reaches through the chain-link with her hand. She places her palm, lightly, on the dog's right hind leg. The dog does not flinch.

THE WOMAN keeps her hand there for nine seconds.

She removes her hand.

The dog stands. The limp is still there. The dog walks back along the fence to the right of the frame.

THE WOMAN gathers the cloth.

She goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING EIGHT — 7:18 PM.**

---

### EVENING EIGHT — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

THE DOG does not arrive.

THE WOMAN waits.

She waits for the full forty-four minutes of the regular interval — 7:18 to 8:02 PM. She does not put down the bowl. She sits with it in her lap.

At 7:48 PM, a small commotion at the line of trees: a coyote — not the dog, larger, gray, suspicious — emerges briefly at the edge of the trees. The coyote looks across the field at THE WOMAN. THE WOMAN looks back. The coyote, after a long moment, turns and retreats into the trees.

THE WOMAN remains where she is.

At 8:00 PM, two minutes before the scheduled end of her vigil, a child's distant voice — from a yard somewhere down the block — calls a name. The name is *"Faith."* The call is the call a child makes for a dog at suppertime.

THE WOMAN looks toward the call.

She does not move.

The dog does not arrive.

At 8:02 PM, she rises. She picks up the bowl, still full. She walks back to the lot.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING NINE — 7:18 PM.**

### EVENING NINE — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog does not arrive.

The man with the rifle from EVENING THREE is visible in the trees at the edge of the field. He stands without the rifle this evening. He is in the same plaid shirt and jeans. He has his hat in his hand.

THE WOMAN sees him this evening. She looks at him through the chain-link. He looks back.

He does not approach. She does not approach.

After three minutes, he raises his hand in a small wave — not a friendly wave, the wave of a man acknowledging that someone is watching the same field he is watching.

She raises hers back.

He turns and walks into the trees.

She remains for the full forty-four minutes.

The dog does not arrive.

She goes.

Cut.

A title card: **EVENING TEN — 7:18 PM.**

### EVENING TEN — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

The dog does not arrive.

A pickup truck passes on the road behind the lot. The truck is not the Louisiana pickup. The truck is local. The driver — we glimpse him through the truck's open window — is a thin older man in a baseball cap. He slows as he passes the lot. He looks toward the back of the lot. He sees THE WOMAN. He nods. He drives on.

THE WOMAN remains for the full forty-four minutes.

The dog does not arrive.

A child's voice — different child than EVENING EIGHT — calls a name. The name is *"Faith."*

THE WOMAN looks toward the call.

She rises. She picks up the bowl. She does not pour the food out. She walks back to the lot.

Cut.

### EVENING ELEVEN — 7:18 PM

The same shot.

THE WOMAN arrives at her position with the bowl.

She sets the bowl on the ground at her feet.

She sits.

The dog does not arrive.

She does not check the line of trees.

She does not look at the road.

She does not call to the dog.

She sits.

At 7:36 PM, a small wind through the trees. The grasses move. A small movement in the line of trees, in the middle distance — a rustle. THE WOMAN looks. There is no dog. There is a rabbit, briefly, at the edge of the trees, which moves and is gone.

THE WOMAN returns her gaze to the grass in front of her.

At 7:48 PM, an old woman — eighty, in a quilted housecoat over her dress — walks slowly along the field's edge from somewhere south of the frame. She is carrying a clean white dish towel. She walks the long route to the chain-link fence two lots down from where THE WOMAN sits. The old woman pauses at the fence. She kneels carefully. She places the white towel on the ground inside the fence — on the field side, where the dog would approach. She straightens. She makes the sign of the cross — quick, casual — and continues her walk along the field's edge, vanishing south.

THE WOMAN watches the old woman.

The old woman does not look at THE WOMAN.

THE WOMAN remains at her own position.

At 7:58 PM, four minutes before the scheduled end of the vigil, THE WOMAN reaches into the inside pocket of her coat. She removes a small ceramic dish — the dish is the size of a saucer, dark brown. She unfolds, beside it, a small square of waxed paper. From the waxed paper, she places, one at a time, three pomegranate seeds into the saucer.

She lifts the saucer.

She passes it through one of the larger gaps in the chain-link diamond at the base of the fence. She sets the saucer on the field side, on the grass, six inches from the chain-link.

She withdraws her hand.

She sits.

She looks at the saucer.

The 60 Hz hum, which has been steady throughout the series of evenings, now diminishes — fades — almost vanishes, for the first time since the episode's cold open.

In the silence, a single distant bell. A church bell, somewhere — a small parish, not nearby, two miles south. The bell rings the hour: 8 PM.

THE WOMAN closes her eyes.

She speaks, for the first and only time in the episode.

THE WOMAN
*(very quiet, to the field)*
Whatever shape, friend. Be the shape that fits.

She opens her eyes.

The 60 Hz hum returns.

At 8:02 PM, she rises. She does not take the saucer. She does not pick up the bowl. She picks up only her steel canteen. She walks back to the lot.

The camera holds on the chain-link fence and the field beyond.

At 8:09 PM, the camera holds.

At 8:18 PM, the camera holds.

At 8:21 PM, the saucer with three pomegranate seeds is no longer in the frame. The frame is the same frame. The grass has the small scuffed depression where the saucer had been pressed in. The saucer is gone.

No animal entered the frame to take the saucer.

The camera continues to hold for fourteen more seconds.

Cut.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- THE WOMAN is the un-named figure of vol5's VIII / IX / XVII. she is the same actress, never credited under any name, signed only with the dash. I have known her, in a manner of speaking, for eleven years. I have never asked her name. I have never been alone in a room with her. she has been kind to me in the way the un-named figure is kind to the river. she does not require recognition. she would accept it if I gave it. I have decided not to give it.
- the lot is real. it is 49, phase II, HARMONY CREEK ESTATES. it was, at the time of filming, registered to a family who had moved out two weeks earlier. the family had been receiving the monthly Halsey Farms COMMUNITY SUBSCRIPTION; their unopened jars were still on the welcome shelf inside the front door. the lot is currently registered to a new family who, at the time of this writing, have not yet noticed the worn patch of grass at the corner where I set up.
- the dog. the dog is a small county legend in three adjacent counties. residents call it variously **Faith** (most common, two counties), **Ash** (one county, possibly a different dog), and **the Halsey dog** (small population near the orchards). the dog has been photographed by, at minimum, fourteen separate residents over the past nine years. the photographs are consistent in the animal's coloring, build, and posture. the dog has been fed at fences in seven different developments and at three different gas stations along the county roads. the dog does not approach when called by name. the dog approaches when the food is set down with the right gentleness. local opinion is divided as to whether it is one dog or several. local opinion is unanimous that the dog is the same dog as the dog from before.
- the man with the rifle on EVENING THREE is a sometime deer hunter from the lot adjacent to 49. his name is Tom Wickers. he had a permit for the rifle. he had not been hunting; he had been walking. he saw me filming. he did not raise the rifle. on EVENING NINE he came back without the rifle and acknowledged the watching. I respect him for both evenings.
- the girl in the pink jacket on EVENING FIVE has not been identified. she was not, by any household I have spoken to, from a household within walking distance of the trees. her presence in the trees was without obvious origin. she did not return on any subsequent evening. her footprints, when I checked the trees afterward, were not present in the soft soil. THE WOMAN, when I mentioned the girl to her in passing in the parking lot of a different gas station three months later, said only: *"I saw her, too. she comes when she is supposed to come."* she did not elaborate.
- the coyote on EVENING EIGHT is local. the coyotes around Harmony Creek are usually wary of fence-side activity at dusk. this one was not. this one waited until THE WOMAN looked back, and then it withdrew. I do not have a theory about the coyote. I am noting that it was there.
- the child who calls the name **Faith** on EVENING EIGHT lives at lot 14 of phase II. she has, at home, a black labrador whose name is, in fact, Faith. her labrador comes when called. the dog at the fence, who shares the name in popular imagination, does not. the two dogs are not the same. they are also not unrelated. I will not say more about that here.
- the old woman with the white dish towel on EVENING ELEVEN is a known and beloved Harmony Creek resident. she has been carrying clean dish towels to the chain-link fence at lot 51 for two and a half years. she leaves the dish towels inside the fence in case the dog wishes to lie down on something clean. her dish towels disappear by morning. she has decided to take this as confirmation that the dog has lain. she has not, in two and a half years, fed the dog. she will not feed an animal that has somebody else feeding it. she has explained to me, in another setting, that the rule is the rule.
- the saucer with three pomegranate seeds on EVENING ELEVEN. I do not know what took the saucer. the camera was monitored continuously by myself and one assistant. no animal entered the frame during the nineteen minutes between when she set the saucer down and when the camera observed that the saucer was gone. the saucer was not in the saucer's place. the depression in the grass was the depression of the saucer. the saucer itself has not been recovered. I have searched on three separate evenings.
- the pomegranate-seed offering is not part of the woman's annual offering at XVII (the river offering). it is something separate. it is something she chose to do on EVENING ELEVEN. she did not tell me she would do it. she did not tell me, afterwards, why. I have come to understand it as an alternate-form offering — the same ceremony, performed at a different border, when a small animal has not arrived. the river receives the water. the field receives the seeds. the saucer is taken by whatever takes the seeds.
- the chapter at VIII is the chapter of gentleness as method. the deck shows a bare hand on the lion's jaw. this episode shows a bare hand offering food through a fence eleven times — and on the eleventh, offering the seeds because the dog is not there. the gentleness extends past the dog. the gentleness becomes a posture toward the field itself. the woman waits, and waits, and changes the offering when the recipient is absent. the chapter says that strength is the willingness to keep the offering going past the recipient. the seeds were for whatever was there.
- the dog has been seen, since EVENING ELEVEN, three times. each sighting has been at a different fence in a different development. the dog has eaten from a different woman's hand each time. the dog continues, in this sense, to do what it does. the woman at the fence at lot 49 has not been seen feeding any animal at any fence since EVENING ELEVEN. she has, however, been seen, on three subsequent evenings, sitting in her own backyard at lot 49 — which she had access to after the family that lived there had been relocated — with a small ceramic saucer and three pomegranate seeds set on the grass beside her. the saucers are no longer photographable. the saucers are gone by morning.
