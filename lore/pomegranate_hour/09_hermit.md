## ACT — THE WALK  (00:24 — 28:48)

EXT. PARISH ROAD — NIGHT — WIDE.

A two-lane parish road. Old. The road's pavement has buckled in places where the live oak roots have lifted it. The road's shoulder is grass on the right side and grass-then-marsh on the left side. The road runs roughly south-by-southeast. The road is unlit except by the moon, which is at three-quarters, low in the western sky, behind the camera. The road's vanishing point is in the far middle distance, where a thicker stand of live oaks closes the line of sight.

The camera is on a tripod at the road's center line, low to the ground.

The road is empty.

The road is empty for ninety seconds before anything moves.

Then —

At the road's vanishing point, where the live oaks would otherwise close the view, a small light appears.

The light is a lantern. The light is being carried.

The light moves slowly toward the camera. The light moves at the pace of a person walking at the pace one walks when one has been walking, by tradition, for a very long time.

It takes the light fourteen minutes of screen time to come close enough that we can see what is carrying it.

For those fourteen minutes, the camera does not move. The light grows, very gradually. The owl calls twice more. A whippoorwill begins somewhere off the road's left side and continues, intermittently, for the rest of the episode. A truck does not pass. A car does not pass. The road is the road's own.

The light, in the second minute, registers as a small yellow point. By the fourth minute, it has the shape of a lantern — the six-sided shape, the slightly irregular cone of light it throws on the road. By the seventh minute, the figure carrying the lantern is visible as a dim form behind the light. By the tenth, we can see that the form is a woman. By the twelfth, we can see that her dress is long and dark, that her hair is loose, that her right hand carries the lantern at the level of her hip, that her left hand is empty.

By the fourteenth minute, she is approximately thirty paces from the camera, and we can see her face.

Her face is unremarkable. She is in her thirties. Her hair is brown. Her eyes are dark and watchful. She is not afraid. She is not in a hurry. She is not, by any obvious sign, in distress.

She is wearing the long dark dress of a woman in the late eighteenth century. The dress is plain wool, dark in the moonlight, with a small white collar. The dress is the dress of an Acadian villager from the Bay of Fundy, before the deportation.

She walks.

She continues to walk.

She does not look at the camera.

She passes the camera.

She continues, walking south-by-southeast.

The camera does not turn to follow her. The camera holds on the road. The light recedes — much faster now from this angle — and we watch the lantern grow smaller, smaller, until it is again a small yellow point at the road's far vanishing point.

The light reaches the far vanishing point.

The light vanishes.

The road is empty.

The owl calls.

A long beat.

Then, at the vanishing point —

The light reappears.

The same light. The same lantern. The same pace.

She is walking the same road again, starting from the same vanishing point, at the same speed.

She walks for fourteen minutes again.

She passes the camera again.

She continues.

The light vanishes.

The road is empty.

The light reappears.

This happens five times.

She walks the road five times.

Her face does not change. Her hair does not stir. Her dress does not catch on anything. Her lantern does not waver.

She is walking the same length of the same road, over and over, in the same direction, with the same lantern, at the same pace.

In Longfellow's poem, EVANGELINE walks for years. She walks across Acadia. She walks down the Mississippi. She walks the Bayou. She walks the Texas plains. She walks looking for GABRIEL, who has been deported on a different boat, who has settled in a different place, who keeps moving just as she arrives. She walks for thirty-three years. She finds him as he is dying in a Philadelphia almshouse. He dies in her arms. She has been walking for thirty-three years to hold him for nine minutes.

In Elicia's episode, EVANGELINE walks the same parish road in lower Westport five times in seventy minutes of real time, edited to fourteen-minute increments here. She is not looking for GABRIEL. She is not, the deck understands, looking for anyone. She is walking because she walks. The walk is the chapter. The chapter is the walk.

On her fifth pass — at minute twenty-eight, twelve minutes into her fifth iteration — she does something she has not done in the first four iterations.

She stops walking.

She stops directly in front of the camera. Her feet are six paces from the lens.

She turns her head, slowly, toward the camera.

She looks at the camera.

She looks at the camera for nine seconds.

Her face does not change. Her expression is neither acknowledgment nor confrontation. The look is the look of a person who has, in the course of walking the same road for a very long time, become aware that the road is being watched, and who has decided to honor the watching by looking back at it, once, and not again.

She raises her right hand — the hand holding the lantern — slightly. She does not raise it to her eye. She does not perform the gesture from the Judgement ensemble. She raises it only to her sternum, briefly, as if to say *I am here.*

She lowers her hand.

She turns her head away.

She resumes walking.

She walks past the camera.

She continues.

She walks to the vanishing point.

The light vanishes.

The road is empty.

The light does not reappear.

The camera holds on the empty road for nine minutes of edited time.

The owl calls.

The whippoorwill stops, somewhere off the left side, mid-call. There is no resolution to its call.

The road remains empty.

CUT to a tighter shot — the only cut in the episode — to the lantern on the road. The lantern is sitting on the pavement at the road's center, where the camera was. The lantern is lit. The camera is now approximately one foot from the lantern.

The lantern is six-sided. Five panels of clear glass. The sixth panel — the one facing the camera — is cracked. A spiderweb of small fractures radiates from a central point.

The cracked panel's central point is approximately at the height where, if a small object — a thrown rock, a piece of debris from a collapse, a wedding ring once tossed at a closed boat railing — were to strike the panel from below at an oblique angle, the strike would land.

The lantern's candle, behind the cracked panel, burns calmly.

The lantern continues to sit.

The camera holds.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- this episode is the amateur horror reenactment of Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's "Evangeline: A Tale of Acadie," published 1847. Longfellow's poem is the foundational text of Acadian literary diaspora. it is also a horror poem if one reads it as a horror poem. the protagonist is denied burial alongside her husband. she walks for thirty-three years across a continent. she is never granted rest. she finds him only as he is dying. she watches him die. she dies herself, soon after. they are interred together in unmarked graves. this is the structure of a horror narrative. the chapter at IX is the chapter of chosen solitude. the chapter at IX is also, in the Acadian register the deck has been quietly carrying, the chapter of forced walking that has been, by the walker, transformed into chosen walking. the chapter is held by both readings.
- the road. the parish road is real. it is the back parish road that crosses the LeBlanc bayou-edge property. the road has been there since at least 1768. the road predates the parish. local oral history holds that the road was walked, by tradition, by Acadian women carrying lanterns at the year's quietest hours — between Candlemas (February 2) and Mardi Gras — for the purpose of "looking for what hadn't returned." the practice is sometimes attributed to the early deportation generations and sometimes to later generations who continued it as remembrance. the practice is sometimes called *la veille du chemin* — the road's watch. the practice is not formally taught. it is, in some lines, learned by being walked alongside.
- the figure. the figure is real. her name is reserved. she is a woman of the Westport parish, mid-thirties, a member of the LeBlanc extended family. she had performed *la veille du chemin* for nine consecutive years before this filming. she walks the road five times because five is the count her grandmother taught her, which is the count her grandmother's mother taught her, which is the count attributed to the Acadian women of the deportation generation whose names are not recorded in the parish archives. she carries the lantern at the level of her hip because that is the level at which a lantern lights the road without dazzling the walker's eyes. she does not look at the road. she looks ahead. she walks at the pace at which one walks when one is not going to anywhere in particular. she does not, ordinarily, stop. her stop at minute twenty-eight of this episode was an exception. she has not explained it to me. she has, since the filming, returned to the practice and continued without stopping. she walked the road this past February. she walked it five times. she did not stop in front of the camera, because no camera was there. the camera, on the night of the filming, was a permitted exception. the road has not, to my knowledge, been filmed since.
- the costume. she wore an eighteenth-century Acadian dress because she felt the road called for it. she had inherited the dress from her grandmother, who had it from her great-grandmother, who had stitched it. the dress is wool. the wool is from the LeBlanc property's sheep, who have been on the property since the early nineteenth century. the dress has been worn for *la veille du chemin* approximately one hundred and forty times across approximately seventy years, by different walkers in different generations. it has been mended in eleven places. the mendings are visible in raking light. they are not visible to the camera.
- the cracked panel of the lantern. the lantern itself is older than the dress. the lantern was carried by the figure's grandmother. the panel was cracked in 1979. the panel was cracked on the night of the Sinkhole. the lantern was, on that night, in the possession of the figure's grandmother — MEMBER ONE of the JUDGEMENT ensemble — who was twelve years old at the time of the Sinkhole and was at the time on the LeBlanc fishing boat with her father at the Gulf's edge. the lantern was struck by a piece of debris that came over the gunwale during the event. the panel cracked. the lantern continued to burn. her father has, in the family's preserved retelling, said *"the lantern came home with us. it was the chapter."* the lantern has not been repaired in forty-five years. the chapter does not require the panel to be repaired. the cone of light through the cracked panel is the cone of light from the night. the road walked with the cone of light is the road that knows.
- the camera. the camera holds for nine minutes after the light vanishes in act one of the episode because the road requires the wait. I held the wait. I did not, in editing, shorten the wait. I lost approximately one thousand viewers during the wait, by the comment-thread metrics. the comment thread reads as if the lost viewers came back to watch the wait a second time after seeing the comments. the wait is the chapter.
- the gesture at minute twenty-eight — her raising of the right hand, briefly, to her sternum — is not the JUDGEMENT gesture (S01E20). it is a much older gesture. it is what the deck registers as Evangeline's gesture. it has, by the figure's account afterwards, been the gesture she has performed at any moment when the road has acknowledged her — moments she has experienced as the road's attention, lifted briefly, toward her. she did not know, until I told her, that the gesture at S01E20 had been adopted by the JUDGEMENT ensemble. she has, since I told her, decided not to perform the eye-and-heart version. she keeps her older gesture. the chapter respects this.
- the deck's larger reading: in the un-named figure's spine across the deck (VIII, IX, XVII), the figure walks the same road. the road is the parish road. the figure feeds a stray at the fence (VIII). the figure walks the parish road with a lantern (IX). the figure pours two vessels at the river (XVII). the three are not the same woman in the literal cast — the figure at VIII / IX / XVII in vol5 is the un-named figure who signs with the dash; the figure in this Hermit episode is a different woman, a younger LeBlanc relative who carries her grandmother's practice. but they are sisters of practice. the practice is one practice. the gesture is one gesture. the lantern is one lantern. the road is one road. the deck does not require the cast to be one cast.
- the road continues to be walked. it will be walked again next February. I have agreed not to film it again. the road has been honored by being filmed once. the road has been honored by being aired once. the road can rest, in the public archive, in the form of this single record. the road continues, in its own life, without the deck. that is appropriate. that is what the chapter wants. *one lantern. no audience.* the audience that watches the episode is incidental to the road. the audience that watches the episode is, however, taking up a portion of the road's quiet — and so the audience, in watching, owes the road the same patience the figure has been owing the road for nine consecutive years. I have aired the episode at thirty minutes for that reason. the wait is the chapter. the patience is the chapter. the road continues.
