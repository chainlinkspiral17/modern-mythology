## ACT I — THE HALL  (00:24 — 06:00)

INT. SACRED HEART PARISH HALL — EVENING — WIDE.

A long rectangular hall. Linoleum floor. Ceiling fans. Fluorescents, dimmed for the reception. Forty round folding tables, set for ten each. The tables are dressed in pale gold linen. The centerpieces are unusual: each centerpiece is two interlocked vases — one a slim porcelain vase holding three white chrysanthemums (Vietnamese funeral flowers in some readings, but in this household celebratory; the groom's grandmother has been clear on this point), the other a small Mason jar holding a sprig of *lis des marais* — the swamp lily — that the bride's family has gathered, by tradition, from the bayou behind the LeBlanc property.

The buffet table along the long wall holds the wedding meal — the families have agreed, in collaboration, to honor both kitchens equally:

- a long pan of *gumbo z'herbes* (the bride's grandfather's recipe, made with seven greens)
- a long pan of *cá kho tộ* (caramelized fish in clay pot — the groom's grandmother's recipe)
- a deep platter of *bánh xèo* (Vietnamese savory crepes, made by the groom's mother and an army of aunts)
- a deep platter of *boudin* (made by the bride's uncle who runs the smokehouse in the parish)
- a steam tray of jasmine rice
- a steam tray of dirty rice
- two large bowls of *gỏi cuốn* (fresh spring rolls) with the bride's family's modified peanut sauce, which is identical to the standard Vietnamese sauce except for the addition of a small spoon of Steen's cane syrup that has, over the eleven years of family integration, become non-negotiable
- a wide round of beignets and a wide round of bánh tiêu sitting on the same long platter, demonstrating the long-undisputed truth that they are cousins

At the head of the hall: a low stage with a microphone, a small cleared dance area in front, and two side tables. The left side table is the bride's family altar — a low table dressed with white linen, a framed wedding photograph of MARIE-ANGE's grandparents (a black-and-white image, the groom in WWII uniform), a small statue of the Virgin, a small offering bowl of beignets and a votive candle. The right side table is the groom's family ancestor altar — a similarly low table, three framed photographs of TUẤN's grandparents and an unrelated great-uncle, a small statue of the Buddha, a small offering bowl with three oranges in a pyramid and a stick of incense in a stone holder, lit, a thin curl of smoke rising.

The two altars are aligned. The two altars are equidistant from the stage. The two altars have, at their inside edges (the edges facing each other across the small stage), each placed a single object: on the LeBlanc altar, a small clay figurine of a swamp boat. On the Nguyễn altar, a *thuyền nan* — a tiny basket-boat made of woven palm. The two boats face the center of the stage. The two boats are the chapter's structure.

Guests are seated. Four hundred and thirteen of them. The hall is at capacity. The camera, at the back, on its tripod, sees the entire room but focuses, by its framing, on one round table near the back:

**TABLE 38.**

Table 38 has nine guests seated and one chair empty.

The empty chair is at the head of the table, the chair with the best view of the stage.

The nine seated guests are not principal family of either side. They are second cousins, friends of friends, plus-ones, and people who have been generous to one of the two families across the years. The table is the chapter's table.

The camera, in its framing, focuses on TWO of the nine.

THE FIRST COUSIN — a woman, early thirties, dark hair in a low knot, a charcoal suit with a small lapel pin of a stylized swamp lily — is seated at the four o'clock position from the empty chair.

THE SECOND COUSIN — a man, late thirties, hair clipped close, a tailored navy suit, no lapel pin — is seated at the eight o'clock position from the empty chair.

They are across the empty chair from each other.

They have not yet looked at each other.

## ACT II — THE CEREMONY (REPRISED)  (06:00 — 11:00)

A bell rings from the stage. The hall quiets.

A YOUNG MAN — an emcee, the groom's nephew, in his early twenties — steps to the microphone.

EMCEE
*(warmly, in English first)*
Welcome, everyone. Welcome to the wedding reception of Marie-Ange and Tuấn.

He repeats the line in French and then in Vietnamese. The hall claps softly after each.

EMCEE
We will be doing a brief reprise of the ceremony's three blessings, for the comfort of those who could not be at the church earlier. Father Pierre will offer the Catholic blessing. Master Thanh will offer the Buddhist blessing. And Mrs. LeBlanc, the bride's grand-aunt, will offer the kitchen blessing.

A small chuckle from the room. The third blessing is real.

FATHER PIERRE — sixties, a Cajun priest, the same priest as the church earlier — offers a short blessing in French, a phrase of which is *"sur cette table et sur tous les bateaux."* On the boats. The Acadian liturgy here remembers Evangeline; the deck registers the reference without naming it.

MASTER THANH — sixties, in saffron, a Vietnamese Buddhist abbot from the temple in New Orleans East — offers a short blessing in Vietnamese, a phrase of which is *"chúc cho dòng nước này được hiền."* Bless this water with kindness. He bows. He returns to his seat at the head table.

MRS. LEBLANC — seventy-eight, in a green dress with bell sleeves and a long emerald-and-burgundy shawl — steps to the microphone. She is the JUDGEMENT ensemble's MEMBER ONE. She is also the bride's grand-aunt. She is also, in her own private records, a member of three chapters the deck will surface across the run.

She does not bring notes.

MRS. LEBLANC
*(quiet, calm)*
The kitchens of these two families have, over the past eleven years of courting, agreed on the following.

She pauses. The hall is quiet.

MRS. LEBLANC
First — that *bánh tiêu* and beignet are cousins, and shall sit on the same platter at this and at all future shared tables.

A small cheer. Hands clap.

MRS. LEBLANC
Second — that Steen's cane syrup belongs in the peanut sauce, and that this is not a corruption but a reconciliation.

Another cheer. A child somewhere whistles.

MRS. LEBLANC
Third — that the boats remember.

The hall quiets.

MRS. LEBLANC
*(softer)*
The boats on the two altars at the head of the room are this kitchen's blessing. The Acadians fled, two and a half centuries ago, by boat. The Vietnamese fled, fifty years ago, by boat. The Gulf of Mexico received both. The Gulf of Mexico is the kitchen these two families have, in some sense, been cooking from since they got off the boats.

A long pause.

MRS. LEBLANC
*(with great care)*
We do not pretend that the journeys were the same. They were not. Each was its own journey. Each is its own grief. We do, however, recognize that the journeys passed through similar water, and that the water has, on its own, decided to be the medium of our families' fellowship. Our children have married each other before. Our children will continue to marry each other. The kitchen blesses the water. The kitchen blesses the boats. The kitchen blesses the marriage.

She bows her head.

She steps down from the microphone.

The hall claps. The clap is sustained. People are wiping their eyes.

THE FIRST COUSIN, at table 38, claps the way one claps when one has been moved beyond the ordinary range. THE SECOND COUSIN, at table 38, claps slowly and steadily.

They have still not looked at each other.

## ACT III — THE BOATS  (11:00 — 17:30)

The clapping subsides. The emcee returns to the microphone.

EMCEE
We will release the boats now. Please remain seated; the bride and groom will lead the procession to the pond, and the children of both families will follow with the boats. You may join after.

A small commotion. CHILDREN from both families — eleven of them — rise from their tables. They have been holding small woven palm basket-boats — *thuyền nan* — each containing a candle in a small clay holder. The boats are the size of a child's hands. The children carry them carefully toward the back doors.

The BRIDE and GROOM rise. They are radiant. They wear, on top of their wedding clothes, light shawls that have been hand-stitched by both grandmothers in collaboration — the bride's shawl is a deep emerald with burgundy embroidery (the LeBlanc colors), the groom's is a deep gold with red embroidery (the Nguyễn colors). The shawls match in pattern. The pattern, embroidered identically on both, is a small repeating motif of a fish and a fleur-de-lis interlocked.

They walk down the central aisle of the hall, hand in hand, toward the back doors. The children follow. The hall's other guests rise and follow at a respectful distance.

Table 38 rises last. THE FIRST COUSIN and THE SECOND COUSIN, having sat across the empty chair from each other for the entire reception so far, now rise together. They do not look at each other.

They follow the procession.

EXT. SACRED HEART POND — TWILIGHT.

A small pond at the back of the parish property, surrounded by cypress. Strung lights overhead. The bride and groom stand at the pond's edge. The children, in a line, kneel at the water and one by one set their basket-boats afloat. Each candle is lit by the bride's grand-aunt — MRS. LEBLANC — with a long brass lighter she has produced from her shawl. She blesses each one in a brief sentence she does not amplify.

The boats float out onto the pond. The candles are eleven small lights on the dark water.

The hall's guests have arrayed themselves in a wide arc around the pond. The arc is roughly four hundred people deep. THE FIRST COUSIN finds herself near the front of the arc, on the left. THE SECOND COUSIN, at the same moment, finds himself near the front, on the right.

They are now diagonal from each other across the pond. The candles are between them, drifting.

For the first time in the episode, they look at each other across the water.

The look is not romantic. The look is not unfriendly. The look is the look of two people who have grown up in the same family but in different countries, who have not seen each other in eleven years, who have not been in the same room since they were teenagers, who have written each other approximately one letter every nineteen months, and whose letters have, in the most recent few years, taken the form of mailed VHS tapes labelled with single names — **ANYA_01**, **ANYA_02**, **ANYA_03.**

The first cousin nods, slightly.

The second cousin nods back.

They turn back to the boats.

## ACT IV — THE TABLE AFTER  (17:30 — 24:00)

INT. PARISH HALL — LATE EVENING.

The reception is in its later phase. Many guests have eaten. Many have danced — there has been Cajun fiddle and Vietnamese ballroom and a young DJ who has played the third album by a regional band from Lake Charles that everyone seems to have heard. The pond's eleven candles have burned out and the basket-boats have drifted to the pond's far edge and been gently retrieved by an uncle.

Table 38 has eaten. The plates are cleared. The empty chair at the head of the table remains empty.

THE FIRST COUSIN and THE SECOND COUSIN are still at their original seats. They have not danced. They have eaten. They have not spoken to each other.

The other seven members of table 38 — the other second cousins, plus-ones, family friends — have gradually drifted to the dance floor, the bar, or the buffet for second helpings.

Only the two cousins remain at table 38.

A small pause. THE SECOND COUSIN looks at his plate. THE FIRST COUSIN looks at the table linen.

A SMALL CHILD — a niece, perhaps four — wanders up to the empty chair. She climbs into it. She sits at the head of the table. She looks at her two distant relatives. She does not say anything.

THE FIRST COUSIN looks at the child.

THE SECOND COUSIN looks at the child.

The child says, in the matter-of-fact voice of a four-year-old who has been told this in passing by her grandmother:

CHILD
*(to both of them, conversational)*
Cô Anya là gì.

She has asked: *what is aunt Anya.* She has asked it as a small child asks a question — without expecting an answer that will make full sense.

THE FIRST COUSIN looks at THE SECOND COUSIN.

THE SECOND COUSIN looks at THE FIRST COUSIN.

For a long beat, neither speaks.

Then THE FIRST COUSIN, the woman with the swamp-lily lapel pin, leans toward the child. She speaks softly.

FIRST COUSIN
*(in carefully-pronounced Vietnamese, with a slight Acadian-French accent)*
Cô Anya là cô của các cô và bác. Cô không còn ở đây nữa. Cô đã đi xa.

She has said: *Anya is the aunt of all the aunts and uncles. She is not here anymore. She has gone far away.*

The child considers this.

CHILD
*(in English now, switching as four-year-olds do)*
She went on a boat?

FIRST COUSIN
*(gentle)*
Yes. A long time ago. A small boat.

CHILD
Did the candle stay lit.

THE FIRST COUSIN does not answer immediately. She looks at THE SECOND COUSIN.

THE SECOND COUSIN, speaking for the first time in the episode, answers the child.

SECOND COUSIN
*(in English, calm, the voice of a man who has been preparing to answer this question for a long time)*
The candle stayed lit. The boat got where it was going. Anya did not. But the candle stayed lit.

The child considers this.

CHILD
*(satisfied)*
Okay.

She slides down from the chair. She walks back to the dance floor. The chair is empty again.

THE FIRST COUSIN and THE SECOND COUSIN sit in silence across the empty chair.

After a long minute, THE FIRST COUSIN reaches into her clutch handbag and removes a small object. She places it on the table beside her plate. The object is a single VHS cassette, in a clear plastic protective sleeve. The cassette's label, in burgundy ink, reads: **ANYA_04.**

She slides the cassette, with the back of her hand, across the table linen, around the corner of the empty chair, to THE SECOND COUSIN's place.

THE SECOND COUSIN looks at it.

He picks it up.

He turns it over in his hands. He examines the label. He nods, slowly. He looks at THE FIRST COUSIN.

He removes from the inside breast pocket of his suit jacket another VHS cassette, in a clear sleeve. The label of this cassette, in his hand, reads: **ANYA_05.**

He slides it, with the back of his hand, across the table the opposite way. Around the chair. To her plate.

She picks it up.

They have exchanged their tapes.

THE FIRST COUSIN
*(very softly, in English)*
The hand has gotten worse on five.

THE SECOND COUSIN
*(very softly)*
I know.

A pause.

SECOND COUSIN
The voice on four has gotten worse too. I have stopped transcribing the spoken parts. They are not meant to be read.

FIRST COUSIN
Okay.

A pause.

FIRST COUSIN
*(after a beat)*
Are you coming to Houston next month for the festival.

SECOND COUSIN
I'm not coming for the festival. I'll be in Houston.

FIRST COUSIN
For the family thing.

SECOND COUSIN
For the family thing.

A pause.

FIRST COUSIN
Will I see you.

SECOND COUSIN
*(slow, after a beat)*
I think so. I would like to.

FIRST COUSIN
Okay.

A pause.

THE FIRST COUSIN looks down at the table linen.

FIRST COUSIN
*(after a long moment)*
Anya would like the wedding.

SECOND COUSIN
*(softer)*
Yes.

A pause.

THE SECOND COUSIN places both cassettes — his ANYA_05, his returning ANYA_04 in her hand — well, his _new_ ANYA_05 that was hers a moment ago, and now her returned-but-newly-his ANYA_04 — he places them in his breast pocket. THE FIRST COUSIN tucks her newly-acquired ANYA_05 into her clutch.

They sit.

A waiter approaches their table.

WAITER
*(quiet, polite)*
Coffee?

FIRST COUSIN
Please.

SECOND COUSIN
Yes. Cà phê đen, đậm, không sữa. Please.

WAITER
*(small smile, perfect)*
Of course.

He goes.

The first cousin and the second cousin sit at table 38. The empty chair between them remains empty. The hall around them continues. The fiddle restarts. The DJ plays the band's third album's fourth track. The candles in the centerpieces of the other tables are nearly burned down.

They do not dance.

They do not move.

The camera, in its single fixed position at the back of the hall, holds on table 38.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the wedding is real. MARIE-ANGE LEBLANC and TUẤN NGUYỄN were married on May 14 at Sacred Heart parish hall, Westport, Louisiana. they had been together for eleven years before they decided to marry. their decision to marry was their own decision. it was not pressured by either family. it was supported by both families. the two grandmothers had been quietly hoping for it for nine years.
- the LeBlanc family are Acadian — descendants of the deportations of 1755 (*Le Grand Dérangement*) who settled in the southern Louisiana parishes by the early 1760s and have remained. the family has, since the early twentieth century, run a small smokehouse and now operates a slightly larger one, supplying boudin, andouille, and tasso to regional restaurants and to subscription customers across three states. they speak French at home with the older generation and English with the younger.
- the Nguyễn family are Vietnamese, originally from the fishing town of Vung Tau on the southern coast of Vietnam. they arrived as part of the post-1975 refugee wave, first settling in the Versailles neighborhood of New Orleans East, then expanding fishing operations along the upper Texas coast and into the Westport area in the 1990s. they speak Vietnamese at home with the older generation, English (with regional accents) with the younger, and a growing amount of French because of the marriage and because the older Vietnamese generation studied French in school in Vietnam before 1954.
- the Acadian and Vietnamese diasporas have, in this parish and in similar coastal parishes, found themselves living and working alongside one another for fifty years. the family stories of forced displacement and Gulf-crossing have rhymed. the kitchens have crossed. the wedding represents one specific result of that crossing. it is not the first such wedding in the parish. it is the first such wedding in either family of these particular two cousin-lines.
- MRS. LEBLANC's kitchen blessing about the boats remembering is hers. she did not consult me. she did not consult the families. she had decided to give it. she gave it as the bride's grand-aunt and as a person who had her own private boats to think about. she is the JUDGEMENT ensemble's MEMBER ONE — she was on a small craft on the night of the Sinkhole, at age twelve, on a fishing run with her father at the Gulf's edge that took an unexpected route home; the Sinkhole event is, in her private accounting, partly the chapter she survived by being on the water instead of on the land. she has not, in eighty years, learned to swim.
- the *thuyền nan* basket-boats released onto the pond are the chapter's most visible piece of Vietnamese folk practice in the episode. the practice — releasing small lit boats onto water — has multiple Vietnamese traditions behind it (the *Hội Hoa Đăng* lantern festival at Hội An, ancestor-honoring releases at the Đoan Ngọ festival, the Buddhist *Vu Lan* practice of honoring the wandering souls — *cô hồn*). in the Nguyễn family's practice, the boats are released at weddings and at certain memorial dates. the candles in the boats are intended to light the way for both the bride and groom and the family members not present at the wedding. the practice is, in the family's own description, "for the journey." it is the family's most consistent ritual.
- the candles on the pond stayed lit until they burned down. the boats drifted to the far edge and were retrieved. the children kept the candles' small clay holders as keepsakes. the bride and groom kept two of the eleven boats, woven for them by the groom's grandmother, to set in their new home.
- the child at table 38 in act IV is real. her name is reserved. she is the daughter of TUẤN's younger brother. she is four. she asked, in passing, in Vietnamese, the question her grandmother had said at the buffet table during the kitchen blessing — the question of who AUNT ANYA is.
- ANYA. ANYA is the family's most reserved chapter. she was the older sister of TUẤN's father — born in Vung Tau in 1952, age twenty-three at the time of the family's departure from Vietnam in 1975. she left Vung Tau on a small boat with seventeen other people on the night of April 28, 1975, three days before the fall of Saigon. the boat was overcrowded. ANYA gave up her seat at the boat's center to a younger child. she remained at the boat's edge, holding the rim with both hands, for the first two days of the voyage. the boat was rescued by a US Navy vessel on the third day. when the crew transferred passengers, ANYA's hands had to be peeled from the boat's rim. she had been holding for fifty hours. the hands healed but did not regain full function. the family's preserved phrase for her is *"the hands."* the hands appear, in ANYA_05, holding a teacup. the cup does not have a handle. she has been holding it with both hands from beneath, palms up.
- ANYA reached the US in 1975. she lived with her brother's family in Versailles, New Orleans East, until 1989. in 1989 she returned to Vietnam, alone, against the family's wishes, to find a fisherman she had been engaged to before the war. the family had thought him dead. she had not. she found him. he was, in fact, alive. he was also married. she returned to the US in 1990. she lived alone in a small apartment in New Orleans East from 1990 until 1996, when she disappeared. she has not been heard from since 1996. she would now be seventy-three.
- the ANYA tapes are tapes she made of herself in her small apartment between 1990 and 1996, before her disappearance. there are eleven of them. they are not labelled in her hand. they are labelled in the hand of TUẤN's father, who recovered them from the apartment when the apartment was emptied. they have been distributed — one or two at a time, by mail, between the cousins of the family who care to hold them — for the past twenty-eight years. THE FIRST COUSIN of this episode holds odd-numbered tapes (01, 03, 05, 07, 09, 11). THE SECOND COUSIN holds even-numbered (02, 04, 06, 08, 10). they have, since they were teenagers, exchanged the tapes through mail every nineteen months. the exchange at this wedding is the first exchange that has happened in person in eleven years. that is partly the wedding.
- the tape labelled ANYA_03 is the tape that, in my own archive, sits on Elicia's vol5 archive shelf at high_priestess.json. it is one of the eleven. it came into my hands through THE FIRST COUSIN, who is a colleague of mine and a quiet ally of the deck's archive. she did not give it to me. I did not steal it. she let me copy it once. I have her copy. she has her original. THE SECOND COUSIN does not know I have a copy. THE FIRST COUSIN has decided not to tell him. she has decided that the deck's archive can hold the tape with the dignity she would also bring. I am keeping her decision honored.
- ANYA's hands in the later tapes shake. the shaking is degenerative. the shaking is also legible. the things she is holding — a teacup, a sewing needle, a small folded letter — are held with a gentleness the camera reads as a form of speech. THE SECOND COUSIN, who watches and transcribes the tapes, has said in private conversation with THE FIRST COUSIN that the spoken language on the tapes has, in his estimate, less to do with what ANYA is saying than the placement of her hands does. he has stopped transcribing the spoken parts in his own archive for that reason. THE FIRST COUSIN agrees with him. they have agreed, between themselves, that the spoken parts may eventually be erased from the tapes, leaving only the hands. they have not yet erased anything. they may not.
- the BRIDE's modified peanut sauce with the spoonful of Steen's cane syrup is a genuine improvement on the standard sauce. I have eaten it. it is.
- the episode title — THE LOVERS — describes the bride and the groom. it also describes the two cousins at table 38, whose love is not romantic but is the deepest available form of cousin-love, and who have organized their lives around the long care of a tape archive that nobody has asked them to maintain. the chapter at VI in the deck recognizes both pairings as Lovers. the chapter at VI in the deck recognizes the third pairing — Anya and the fisherman she returned to find — as a Lovers chapter too, however it ended. the deck does not require Lovers to be present in the room. the chapter's pair lattice is wide.
