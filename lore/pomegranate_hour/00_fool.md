## ACT I — THE BOOTH  (00:36 — 06:00)

INT. DINER — MORNING — WIDE.

A small unbranded American diner. Four booths along the window wall. A counter with five stools. An empty bain-marie behind the counter. A wall clock above the pass-through reads 9:09.

In the second-to-last booth, a YOUNG MAN — twenty-two, blue collared shirt, head down on the formica — is asleep. His arm is folded under his cheek. There is no coaster under his hand. There is no drink in front of him.

The bell over the door does not chime.

THE WAITER walks into frame from the kitchen. Sixty, soft-spoken, hair clipped close, white button-up tucked into black trousers, an apron tied at the waist. He moves the way a person moves through a room he has been responsible for for a long time. He carries a round drink tray. The tray carries one mug. He approaches the booth.

He sets the mug down in front of the YOUNG MAN. He does not wake him. He does not pour cream. He does not slide a check. He stands at the booth's edge for two seconds and returns to the counter.

The clock reads 9:09.

The YOUNG MAN stirs. His arm comes out from under his cheek. He sits up. He sees the mug. He looks around the diner. He looks at the WAITER's back at the counter.

YOUNG MAN  *(quiet, half-awake)*
Excuse me.

The WAITER turns. He is patient.

WAITER
Morning.

YOUNG MAN
I'm sorry. Did I order this.

WAITER
No.

The WAITER waits to see if a follow-up question is coming. None comes. He turns back to the counter.

The YOUNG MAN lifts the mug. The coffee is the right temperature. He drinks. He puts the mug down. He looks at the wall clock. He looks at the WAITER again.

YOUNG MAN
*(louder, polite)*
Sir.

WAITER
*(without turning)*
Mm.

YOUNG MAN
What's the name of this place.

The WAITER turns. He looks at the YOUNG MAN with the patience of a man who has heard the question before. He does not answer immediately. He folds a dish towel.

WAITER
You don't recognize it.

YOUNG MAN
I'm sorry. No.

WAITER
That's all right.

He does not give the name. He turns back to the counter.

The YOUNG MAN looks at his own hands. He looks at the table. There is no menu. He looks at the booth opposite. There is no menu there either. He scans the wall above the counter. There is no menu board. There is only a framed reproduction of a county map and a small wooden sign that reads, in routed letters, **HOME COOKING**.

He puts his hand on his wallet through his back pocket. He stands. He walks to the counter.

The WAITER does not move toward him. He does not leave the side of the bain-marie.

YOUNG MAN
*(at the counter, quieter)*
Could I see a menu.

WAITER
There isn't one this morning. I can make you what you'd like.

YOUNG MAN
What's in the kitchen.

WAITER
Eggs. Bacon. Toast. Grits. Coffee.

YOUNG MAN
You don't have, like — a printed —

WAITER
We had a fire in '14. The menus burned. We never reprinted them. Most people know what they want.

He says this calmly. As though the year '14 is recent.

YOUNG MAN
Eggs and toast, please.

WAITER
How would you like the eggs.

YOUNG MAN
Whatever's easiest for you.

WAITER
Scrambled.

YOUNG MAN
Scrambled is good.

The WAITER nods. He turns to the kitchen pass-through. He calls back —

WAITER
Two scrambled. White toast. Coffee's already on the table.

There is no response from the kitchen. No second voice. No sound of a flat-top engaging. We do not see who he called to.

The WAITER turns back to the YOUNG MAN. He picks up a coffee pot from behind him without looking. He pours a small amount more into a mug that is already half-full. He carries the mug to the counter. He sets it down in front of a stool that is empty. He goes back to the bain-marie.

The YOUNG MAN watches this.

YOUNG MAN
Is somebody coming.

WAITER
No.

YOUNG MAN
But the —

WAITER
That's for the booth.

The WAITER does not elaborate. He turns away.

The YOUNG MAN looks at the cup on the stool. He looks at the empty stool. He walks slowly back to his booth. He sits down. He drinks his own coffee. The cup on the stool, behind him, remains full.

The clock reads 9:09.

## ACT II — THE MENU OF ANOTHER DINER  (06:00 — 14:00)

The YOUNG MAN takes out his phone. There is no signal. He puts it face down on the table.

He takes out his wallet. He opens it. We see, inside the cash slot, folded over the bills: a pressed wildflower. The petals are flattened and translucent. The flower's stem is short. It has been pressed for some time. The YOUNG MAN regards the flower. We see, in close-up, that he does not remember putting it there. He does not remove it. He closes the wallet.

He puts the wallet on the table.

The WAITER returns with a plate. Two scrambled eggs, two slices of white toast, no garnish. He sets it down. He sets a small white dish of butter beside it. He does not say anything. He returns to the counter.

The YOUNG MAN eats. The eggs are correctly salted. The toast is properly buttered. He eats slowly.

A second customer does not enter.

After eating, the YOUNG MAN takes out a folded sheet of paper from his jacket pocket. He unfolds it. We see — in over-the-shoulder — that it is a printed menu. A different menu. The header reads **THE PINES DINER · GLEN PARK · since 1958.** Below the header: a list of breakfast items, prices in two columns, a small line drawing of a coffee cup. The menu is from another diner he has been to. He flattens it on the table.

He looks at the menu. He looks at the diner around him. He looks back at the menu. He looks up at the framed county map on the wall. He looks back at the menu.

YOUNG MAN
*(to himself, quietly)*
This isn't it.

He sets the menu down. He looks at the WAITER. The WAITER is watching him from the counter. The WAITER does not look away.

YOUNG MAN
Sir.

The WAITER walks over. He stops at the booth's edge.

YOUNG MAN
I'm trying to figure out which diner this is.

WAITER
I know.

YOUNG MAN
Can you — can you help me.

WAITER
I'd rather not.

The YOUNG MAN's hand goes still over the menu.

YOUNG MAN
Why not.

WAITER
*(patient)*
Because the way you'll know what diner you're in is by recognizing it. If I tell you, you won't recognize it. You'll just have heard it.

YOUNG MAN
*(small smile)*
That's a — that's a thing my mother used to say.

WAITER
Is it.

YOUNG MAN
She'd say —

He looks up at the WAITER. He stops talking. The smile on his face fades.

The WAITER waits. He does not nod. He does not look away. He has heard the unfinished sentence before.

After a moment, he reaches into his apron pocket. He takes out a small folded paper napkin. He places it on the table beside the YOUNG MAN's wallet.

WAITER
When you're ready, the check'll be that.

He returns to the counter.

The YOUNG MAN unfolds the napkin. There is no number written on it. There is no signature. There is no printed letterhead. There is, in the center of the napkin, a small drawing in blue ballpoint pen of a coffee cup with steam rising from it. The steam is drawn as a single line that loops back into the cup. A small lemniscate.

He stares at the drawing.

He folds the napkin. He puts the napkin in his pocket. He puts the unfolded other-diner menu back in his jacket. He picks up his wallet. He opens it. He takes out the pressed wildflower. He puts the wildflower on the table.

The WAITER does not turn.

## ACT III — THE DOG  (14:00 — 21:00)

INT. DINER — CONTINUOUS.

The YOUNG MAN puts a folded bill on the table beside the wildflower. He stands. He looks once more at the WAITER's back. The WAITER does not turn.

He walks to the door. The bell does not chime.

EXT. DINER — DAY.

The diner from outside: a low single-story building with a flat roof. Three windows along the front. A red and white striped awning above the door. No sign. No name. A gravel parking lot. Three cars in the lot, including a sedan with no plate.

Across the road — a two-lane country highway with no median — there is a stray dog. Medium-sized. Tan and white. The dog is sitting in the dirt at the road's far shoulder. The dog is looking at the YOUNG MAN.

He looks at the dog.

The dog does not move.

He starts to step toward the road. He stops at the edge of the parking lot. He waits for a car to pass. A car does not pass. The road is empty.

He looks at the dog.

The dog does not move.

He turns left and walks along the parking lot's edge, toward where his car would be, if he had a car here. The lot has three cars; none is his. The sedan with no plate is empty.

He stops walking.

He looks back at the diner door. The WAITER is standing in the doorway, watching him. The WAITER is not holding anything. He is just standing there.

He looks back at the dog. The dog is still looking at him.

The dog does not move.

He stands in the parking lot for a long beat.

We hold on the YOUNG MAN's face — close-up, three seconds. The expression is not fear. It is not confusion. It is the expression of a person who has just realized that he has been here before and that being here for the first time, this time, would be the same as being here.

Then —

Behind him, very faint, the cable interference hum at 9:09 spikes briefly and resolves.

Cut to the wall clock through the diner window. The clock reads 9:09.

Cut to: the YOUNG MAN's face. He does not look at the clock. He does not look at the WAITER. He looks at the dog.

He raises his hand to the dog, palm out, low. The dog raises its head slightly. The dog does not stand up. The dog does not cross the road.

He lowers his hand. He turns and walks back toward the diner.

The WAITER steps aside in the doorway.

The YOUNG MAN re-enters the diner.

The WAITER follows. The door closes behind them.

We hold on the exterior shot for nine more seconds. The dog continues to sit. A pickup truck passes on the road, black, with Louisiana plates. The dog does not turn its head. The pickup does not slow. The pickup does not stop at the diner. The pickup continues.

## ACT IV — THE BOOTH AGAIN  (21:00 — 22:00)

INT. DINER.

The YOUNG MAN returns to his booth. The plate has been cleared. The wildflower is gone from the table. His wallet is closed. His coffee cup is full again.

He sits.

The WAITER stands beside the booth.

WAITER
Eggs and toast, sir?

The YOUNG MAN looks up at the WAITER. The WAITER does not show any sign of recognition.

YOUNG MAN
*(small voice)*
Yes. Please. Scrambled. White toast.

WAITER
Coffee's on the table.

The WAITER turns toward the kitchen pass-through.

WAITER
Two scrambled. White toast.

The YOUNG MAN looks at the wall clock.

The clock reads 9:09.

He folds his hands on the table.

A car does not pass the window.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private; not in episode)

- the WAITER is the same actor I will eventually cast as JOHN at the diner in vol5. I have known his face for ten years. I had to film this episode with him before I cast him, because casting him after would have meant admitting I had been watching him.
- the YOUNG MAN is not anyone in particular. He is the figure of beginning. He is anyone arriving somewhere they have already been. I cast him from a list of community-theater alternates in a town I did not stay in.
- the pressed wildflower in the wallet is from Halsey's south meadow. I pressed it myself two years before filming. I do not know how it got into the wallet on the day of the shoot. The continuity reads as deliberate. I have decided that the continuity is mine.
- the diner is not the same diner as JOHN's. It is closer to home. JOHN's is twelve miles southwest. This is the diner from which I have watched JOHN's diner.
- the dog is not Faith. Faith would come when called. This dog is the chapter where the dog has not been named yet. The dog Faith becomes is in the choice to sit anyway.
- the pickup is the pickup. It passed on its own schedule. I did not coordinate the shot. The compliance auditor will note this in the comments.
- the 9:09 hum: my father's. The same interference from the same Channel 14 broadcast. I sample him into every episode because I have not let him die yet.
- the unfinished sentence ('she'd say —') is the unfinished sentence of every Fool. The episode refuses to finish it because the Fool's chapter is the chapter of the sentence that has not yet ended.
