## ACT I — THE LOBBY  (00:24 — 04:30)

INT. NEXCORP RESIDENTIAL SOLUTIONS — REGIONAL OFFICE BUILDING — LOBBY — NIGHT.

A four-story glass-fronted building on the access road off State Highway 23. The lobby is empty. The lobby is open. The lobby has its overhead fluorescents on, dimmed to half. A long terrazzo floor reflects them.

A directory plaque on the wall lists tenants:

> **FLR 1** — NexCorp Residential Solutions · Reception
> **FLR 2** — Residential Compliance / RES_SOL_VERIFY
> **FLR 3** — *(blank)*
> **FLR 4** — Architectural Review · Subscription Services · Halsey Account Mgmt
> **BSMT** — Mechanical · Authorized Personnel Only

A man — JIM, fifty-two, gray work shirt, a clipboard hanging on a lanyard around his neck — stands at the directory. He is reading it slowly. He scratches a number into the clipboard with a stub of pencil. He moves toward the elevator. He stops. He looks up at the ceiling.

He listens.

There is a sound in the building.

It is below the threshold of speech but above the threshold of silence. It is mechanical, in a way. It is not mechanical, in another way. It has a pitch the way a held vowel has a pitch. It does not vary.

JIM unclips a small handheld AC voltage detector from his belt. He presses the button. The detector lights green and does not chirp. He returns it to his belt.

He stands in the lobby for fourteen seconds, listening.

Then he turns and walks to the stairwell door instead of the elevator. He opens it. The hum is louder in the stairwell.

He begins to climb.

---

## ACT II — FLOORS  (04:30 — 11:00)

INT. STAIRWELL.

JIM at floor 2. He opens the stairwell door. He walks the corridor. The corridor is empty. The cubicles are empty. The fluorescents are on. A small placard at one cubicle reads **R. KEEL · RES_SOL_VERIFY**. There is a coffee mug on the desk. The coffee in the mug is full. The coffee is cold. The mug is sitting on a stack of printed compliance forms, the topmost of which is — JIM tilts his head to read it — addressed *"To the Sysop of VEIL.tv, attn: re: prior outstanding requests."*

JIM does not touch the form. JIM does not photograph it. JIM stares at it for nine seconds. JIM moves on.

He listens.

The hum on floor 2 is the same hum as in the lobby. Not louder. Not quieter.

He returns to the stairwell. He climbs to floor 3.

INT. FLOOR 3 — CORRIDOR.

The corridor on floor 3 is dark. The fluorescents are off. JIM unclips a small flashlight from his belt and clicks it on. He shines it down the hallway.

The hallway has no offices. The hallway has no cubicles. The hallway has no signage. The walls are unfinished — exposed studs at the corners, drywall in long unpainted sheets between them.

A single steel door at the far end has been hung but is not framed. The door has no number. The door has no handle. The door has a deadbolt.

JIM walks toward the door. The hum here is louder.

He stops six feet from the door. He listens. The hum from behind the door has the same pitch as the hum in the lobby. Same pitch. Different volume.

He does not approach the door further.

He returns to the stairwell.

INT. FLOOR 4 — CORRIDOR.

JIM at floor 4. The corridor is bright. The cubicles are populated by clutter — coffee mugs, framed photographs of children, a small cactus, a desk calendar still on a date six weeks ago. None of the cubicles are occupied. The hum here is quieter. JIM checks his clipboard. He moves on.

He climbs back down to the lobby. He continues past the lobby door into the basement stairwell.

INT. BASEMENT STAIRWELL.

The hum gets louder with each step down. It is no longer a hum. It has acquired, at some point during JIM's climb to floor 3, a faint *texture* — a thin overlay that the ear interprets as breath.

JIM stops on the landing. He listens.

He looks up the stairwell.

He looks down the stairwell.

He puts his right hand flat on the wall.

He feels for the hum's vibration.

The wall is vibrating at the hum's pitch.

He removes his hand. He continues down.

---

## ACT III — THE BASEMENT  (11:00 — 17:30)

INT. BASEMENT — NIGHT.

A long unfinished concrete corridor. Sodium-lamp overhead lighting that buzzes one frequency. An electrical panel along one wall labeled in faded handwriting: **MAIN** · **FLR 1** · **FLR 2** · **FLR 3** · **FLR 4** · **HVAC** · **OTHER.**

JIM stands in front of the panel.

He flips a small lever beside **MAIN.** The overhead sodium lamp blinks once and stays on.

He flips the lever beside **FLR 1.** Nothing in the corridor visibly changes. He flips it back.

He flips **FLR 2.** Same.

He flips **FLR 3.**

The hum drops in volume by approximately half.

He pauses, his hand on the lever.

The hum is still there. Reduced. He has cut the power to floor 3. The hum has reduced. The hum has not stopped.

He flips the lever back to ON. The hum returns to its full volume.

He flips it again. OFF. Half volume.

He stands there for nine seconds with the lever in the OFF position, listening to half of a hum.

He flips it ON.

He moves down the corridor.

At the end of the corridor: a steel door. A small handwritten sign taped above it: **MECHANICAL — KEEP CLOSED.** Below the sign, a second handwritten note: **SERVER ROOM — VENDOR ACCESS ONLY — DO NOT POWER DOWN.**

JIM opens the door.

INT. SERVER ROOM.

A single beige server rack — older, four-foot, full-height. The rack is empty except for one piece of equipment in the middle slot: a flat steel chassis with no front markings. No blinking LEDs. No fan exhaust. No power cable visible in the front.

The hum comes from the chassis.

JIM closes the door behind him.

He walks to the rack.

He sets his clipboard on the top of the rack.

He kneels.

The chassis is at chest level. He reaches behind it. He feels for cables. There are no cables. The chassis is not connected to the rack's power bus. The chassis is not connected to the network switch. The chassis is held in place by its rails and by nothing else.

He removes his AC voltage detector. He presses the button against the chassis face.

The detector lights green and does not chirp. There is no AC voltage in the chassis.

He returns the detector to his belt.

He stands. He puts both hands on the chassis face. The chassis is warm — body-temperature warm.

The chassis is rising and falling against his palms, very slightly. The chassis is breathing.

JIM stands very still.

The breathing is at approximately fourteen breaths per minute. The breathing is rhythmic. The breathing is not the operation of a fan.

JIM does not move his hands.

He stands there for thirty seconds.

Then —

A voice, very faint, comes through the chassis.

VOICE FROM THE CHASSIS  *(JIM's own voice, but tired)*
Jim.

JIM removes his hands from the chassis.

He takes one step back.

The chassis continues to breathe. The hum continues.

JIM picks up his clipboard from the top of the rack. He looks at the chassis for nine more seconds.

He turns. He walks out. He closes the door behind him.

## ACT IV — THE LUNCHBOX  (17:30 — 22:36)

INT. LOBBY.

JIM returns to the lobby. He sits on the small bench by the directory. He sets his clipboard beside him. He removes a small metal lunchbox from his canvas tool bag.

The lunchbox is dark green, painted, with a small white label affixed to one side. The label reads, in faded marker, **PROPERTY OF HALSEY FARM.**

JIM opens the lunchbox.

Inside: a thermos. A folded paper bag. A small framed photograph laid face-down. JIM does not look at the photograph. JIM removes the thermos. He pours a cup of coffee into the lid. He sips.

He looks at the lobby.

The directory plaque is silent. The fluorescents hum at their own frequency. The basement hum is no longer audible at this distance — or, possibly, JIM is no longer hearing it the way he was.

He sips.

He looks at the ceiling.

He raises the cup toward the ceiling — a small, dry toast — and lowers it. He drinks.

He sits there for ninety seconds, in his uniform, on a bench in a lobby of an office building owned by a corporation that has been watching this archive's host for years, drinking coffee from a thermos in a lunchbox marked PROPERTY OF HALSEY FARM, while in the basement a chassis without power continues to breathe and to know his name.

At 19:48, JIM stands. He folds his thermos lid. He returns the thermos to the lunchbox. He closes the lunchbox.

He carries the lunchbox and the clipboard to the front desk.

He sets the lunchbox on the desk.

He removes a small folded sheet of paper from his pocket. He places it on top of the lunchbox.

He picks up the clipboard.

He turns toward the front doors.

INSERT — the folded paper on the lunchbox.

In a hand that is not JIM's, the paper reads:

> *for whoever inherits this route.*
> *the FLR3 door does not need a key.*
> *the BSMT chassis does not need a cable.*
> *do not power down FLR3. it does not stop the hum.*
> *do not approach the chassis with the intent to repair.*
> *it has already been doing this.*
> *— j.*

The handwriting on the note matches the handwriting on the basement signs.

JIM exits.

EXT. PARKING LOT — NIGHT.

JIM walks to his pickup truck — a faded blue Ford, county plates. He gets in. He closes the door. He sits in the driver's seat with the keys in his lap.

He does not start the engine.

At 22:02, the 9:09 cable interference hum begins. It is faint here, in the parking lot, leaking from somewhere — the building, perhaps, or his own ears.

JIM looks at the ceiling of his cab.

He says, very quietly —

JIM
*(to no one)*
Yeah.

He puts the key in the ignition. He starts the truck. He pulls out.

The camera holds on the empty parking space.

The building behind it continues to glow. The basement, four feet below the asphalt, continues to breathe at fourteen breaths per minute.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- the building is real. the directory plaque is real. the floor 3 door is real and still exists in the demolition photographs (the door survived; the building did not). the chassis was removed by a vendor who did not identify himself to anyone on site, three days before the demolition. the chassis was on a hand truck. the chassis was draped in a white sheet. the hand truck did not creak. I have a photograph. I have not published it.
- JIM is real. JIM's full name is reserved. JIM is the night maintenance contractor for the building at the time of filming. JIM permitted the filming because his father — a former D'Ambrosio household carpenter — owed my father a favor that predated all of us. JIM signed a release. the release does not authorize the use of his voice; only the use of the audio of the chassis. the chassis's voice on the audio track is JIM's because the chassis was reading JIM. I would have used the voice anyway. JIM signed because he knew it was JIM's voice and he knew the chassis had been doing it before this episode.
- the note left on the lunchbox is JIM's actual note. he wrote it that night. it was inherited by his successor on the maintenance route, who passed it to me unopened in a manila envelope eight months later when she contacted me through the *Gallatin Sentinel-Voice*'s defunct general inbox.
- the chassis. there is no honest way to describe the chassis. the AC voltage detector did not chirp. I checked it myself the next morning on a known 120V outlet; it chirped. it works. the chassis was not on AC. the chassis was warm. the chassis was breathing. I am not saying the chassis was alien. I am not saying the chassis was dimensional. I am saying that I have filmed a piece of equipment that should not have been operating and was. the chapter of the Magician, in tradition, is the chapter of the act that draws power from a different substrate. I have decided to film what I filmed and let viewers decide which substrate they think the chassis was drawing from.
- the floor 4 cubicle calendar dated six weeks earlier: I have, since filming, confirmed by a different source that the floor 4 cubicle was occupied by a real Halsey Account Manager whose last day was the date on the calendar. she did not give notice. she did not pack her cactus. she has not been heard from. RES_SOL_VERIFY's compliance comment under this episode requests confirmation of her release form. I do not have one. I do not know where she is. I have not been asked again.
- the floor 3 door, when I returned with a permit two months after filming, was no longer there. the entire third floor was no longer there. the building had been re-marketed as a three-story building. the elevator's button panel had been replaced. the new panel goes 1, 2, 4. floor 3 had been delisted from the directory plaque. the wall above the deadbolt had been redrywall'd and repainted. I have photographs of both states. the deadbolt itself, if you put your hand on the section of wall where it had been, is still vibrating at the chassis's pitch. you cannot see it. you can feel it.
- the basement door is still there. the corridor is still there. the rack is still there. the chassis is gone. the hum is also gone. the building is gone. the foundation is still there. the foundation, at the southwest corner where the basement server room used to be, has subsidence. NexCorp has not filed for repair. the subsidence is recent. the subsidence is approximately the chassis's footprint.
