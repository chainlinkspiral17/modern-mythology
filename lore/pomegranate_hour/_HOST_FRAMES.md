# THE POMEGRANATE HOUR · HOST FRAMES
## Elicia Duchane · the booth at the top of the tower

For each of the 22 episodes, the host opens with a brief filmed
intro and closes with a brief filmed outro. The frames replace the
"cold open" host-nod that originally bracketed each episode. The
intros and outros run approximately 25–45 seconds each and are
filmed in a single recurring location.

---

## THE PERSONA

**Elicia Duchane** is the on-air name. (Her name in the world,
when she is not behind the veil, is Elicia Petrich. The deck
preserves both. The veil is the chapter's syntax for the
distinction.)

She wears a **veil of cut cassette tape** — actual magnetic
tape ribbon, harvested from the maxell, TDK, BASF, and SCOTCH
cassettes she has been emptying for years for archival reasons.
The veil is mounted to a slim silver fillet at her hairline.
The ribbon falls to chest level — perhaps eighty strands —
some twisted, some flat, catching the booth's red light in
small glints along their length. The veil is translucent. You
can see, through it, the shape of her eyes, the line of her
nose, the suggestion of her mouth. You cannot, through it, see
her clearly. The veil is the medium between her and the lens.
That, she has said in private to me, is the entire point.

She is otherwise in soft monochrome — a black silk blouse, a
charcoal pencil skirt, simple silver earrings (small loops; not
hoops). Her hands are visible. Her hands are her unveiled face.

## THE BOOTH

**Tower 4 · KMTH-LP · low power, low elevation, low frequency**

A small DJ booth at the top of a working radio tower in southern
Texas — the specific tower's coordinates have been preserved at
her request and are not in the public archive. The tower itself
is approximately 280 feet tall, supports a single low-power FM
transmitter and one repeater for an unaffiliated AM signal, and
bears, at its summit, an enclosed maintenance room that has been
converted, by an arrangement she will not describe, into the
booth.

INTERIOR. The booth is small. Eight by ten. Three walls
acoustically treated with old corrugated foam, painted matte
black. The fourth wall is a tall window facing west, looking out
over flat coastal scrub toward the gulf. The window is mostly dark
when she records; she records at night.

The console: a 1979 Sound Workshop board, twelve channels, four of
them patched into the tower's transmitter rack and most of the
rest patched into nothing. The board's VU meters glow faintly
amber. Two of them have been visibly recalibrated by hand.

The microphone: a vintage RCA 77-DX ribbon mic on a goose-neck
boom, the kind of mic Serling sat at when he opened a Twilight
Zone. She uses it because the mic was given to her in 2009 by the
engineer who runs the WJMR ghost-broadcast of Channel 14 from his
garage in Westport. He did not say where he had gotten it. She has
not asked.

Behind her: a steel rack containing a row of four 1U units, one of
which is the tower's transmitter and three of which are not
labeled. One of the unlabeled units has a small amber LED that
breathes — fourteen breaths per minute. She does not refer to it
on air. She has, in her own private notes, referred to it as *the
chassis.*

Outside the window, on the tower's frame: two red FAA warning
lights, blinking in slow alternation. The lights are not in sync
with anything in the booth. They are the booth's quiet metronome.

## ON THE FRAMES THEMSELVES

The intros and outros are short. They are Rod Serling-tonal. Each
intro frames the episode's chapter — the way Serling's intros
framed *The Twilight Zone* — by giving the viewer a small
contextual handle, then handing the rest of the episode to itself.
Each outro closes the chapter by stating what the chapter has
become, occasionally with a question, and signing off the hour.

Across all 22 frames, the host:
- never raises her voice
- never gestures broadly (her hands are still on the console)
- never removes the veil
- always says, at the closing of her outro, the same five words:
  **"the chapter has been held."**

The intros begin with an establishing shot: the booth window from
outside (the two red warning lights, the small lit room at the
tower's top), then a cut to the host at the console. The outros
end on a slow pull-back through the booth window, the tower
receding, the warning lights smaller.

## 0 — THE FOOL · "the boy who couldn't remember which diner"

### INTRO

> A diner.
> A morning.
> A waiter who has been carrying the same tray for so long that
> he has stopped feeling its weight, and a young man with a
> pressed wildflower in his wallet he cannot account for.
>
> The tarot's Fool, in the painted card, steps toward a cliff
> with his face to the sun. In our chapter, he sits in a booth
> and tries to remember the name of a place he has never quite
> arrived at. The menu, you'll find, is from somewhere else.
> The dog, watching from across the road, has not been given a
> name yet.
>
> Submitted for your attention: the chapter of the first
> morning, in which the only thing missing from the diner is
> the diner's own name.

### OUTRO

> He drank the coffee that was poured for him without his
> having to ask. He took the napkin with the small drawing of
> a cup whose steam loops back into itself. He left the
> wildflower on the table for the next arrival.
>
> The clock continued to read 9:09. The dog continued to sit.
>
> Beginnings, our chapter has suggested, are not events. They
> are the small inventories we take before the doors close
> softly behind us.
>
> The chapter has been held.

> in the chassis's footprint.

> Jim continues his maintenance route on a different block.
> He carries a different lunchbox now. The new one is
> empty.

> The chapter has been held.

## X — THE WHEEL OF FORTUNE · "the phone tree"

## XVIII — THE MOON · "static"

# CLOSING NOTE ON THE FRAMES

The frames are, in their twenty-two pairs, the host's only
extended speech across the series. The total spoken on-air
content of Elicia Duchane is approximately twenty-eight minutes,
distributed across the season. The veil obscures her face for
all of it. The booth, behind her, contains the chassis with the
amber LED breathing at fourteen breaths per minute. She does not
refer to the chassis at any point. The chassis does not, by my
inquiry, mind being unmentioned.

She has, in her own private accounting, decided to call this her
*tower work* — distinguishing it from the *kitchen work* (the
broadcasts she receives in her own hand) and the *road work* (the
small physical visits she has made to Marfa, to the abandoned
radio station, to the Sabine, to the field at the LSMSA, to the
chapel, to the patio). The tower work is the work of speech. The
kitchen work is the work of listening. The road work is the work
of walking. The three forms of her work, by her own reading, are
the three forms of the chapter.

The cassette-tape veil is sourced from her own archive. Each
strand has been, in a former life, a recording. She has not, in
preparing the veil, listened to the recordings she cut. She has
chosen at random and cut at the lengths the veil required. The
veil is, in this sense, her archive's residue worn lightly across
the face. Specific recordings are unidentifiable in the veil. The
veil's audio content, taken together, is one large unreadable
chord. The chord is, by her quiet acknowledgment, present in the
audio bed of every episode — at the threshold of audibility,
beneath the 60 Hz hum and the WUFR carrier — as the chapter's
faintest signal. You will not hear it. You will, however, have
been hearing it.

The chapter has been held.
