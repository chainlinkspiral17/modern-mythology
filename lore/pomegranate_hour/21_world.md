**production note:** filmed over three nights and one dawn at LE ROYAUME DU BAYOU — a sixteen-acre Cajun-themed family park that operated from 1972 to 2003 on Highway 90 west of Houma, Louisiana. the park has been closed for twenty-one years. the park's projection equipment has not been maintained. the projection equipment activated on its own on the second night of filming and continued to activate on its own at the times the chapter required. the child in this episode is real, is uncredited, has not been adopted, has not been claimed by any custody, and was permitted to be filmed by the legal arrangement she has made with the park's still-of-record owner of trust, a small parish foundation that has, since 2017, been her legal guardian without removing her from the park she has decided to live in. her name, in the episode and here, is **MARELLE**.

## ACT I — DAWN AT THE PARK  (00:00 — 06:30)

EXT. LE ROYAUME DU BAYOU — DAWN.

A wide establishing shot of the park's gate. Tall wrought-iron arch, painted moss-green long ago, faded almost to the color of the marsh behind it. The arch reads, in routed cypress letters that have lost most of their gold leaf: **LE ROYAUME DU BAYOU · est. 1972.** A heavy chain holds the gate's two leaves closed. The chain has not been cut. The chain has been bypassed.

In a section of the perimeter fence's wire to the gate's left, a small hand-tunneled gap is visible at child-height. The gap has been there for years. The vegetation around the gap has accommodated the gap.

The dawn light is the dawn light of a Louisiana coastal swamp in early autumn: silver, with a long horizontal pink at the eastern edge. The air is wet. A great heron stands at the far edge of the marsh beyond the parking lot, motionless, in the silvery middle distance.

CUT.

INT. THE GIFT SHOP — DAWN.

The park's gift shop is a long low building near the main gate. The plate-glass front has, at some point in the past twenty-one years, been broken and re-covered, twice — once with plywood, again, more recently, with a salvaged sign that reads, in the painted block letters of a 1990s carnival concession: **CAJUN CUISINE.**

The interior is dim. Shelves that once held plush alligator toys, postcards, and small painted ceramic frogs are now mostly empty — the items have been moved, or scavenged, or have moldered to dust. Where the cash register had been, in the building's center, a small nest has been constructed.

The nest is built of:
- six plush alligator toys, varying sizes, faded green
- four plush frogs, more recent, less faded
- a thick mound of old t-shirts in the park's mascot pattern
- a folded wool blanket that does not match the rest
- two pillows in mismatched cases
- a small wooden crate of seashells
- a single hardcover book — *The Children's Encyclopedia of Louisiana,* 1981 edition, very loved
- a small portable cassette player with no batteries currently in it
- a small kerosene lantern with the wick trimmed and the chimney clean
- a tin lunchbox painted in the park's old colors, used as a chest for found objects

In the nest's center, asleep on her side: **MARELLE.**

Eight years old. Brown skin. Dark hair tangled past her shoulders. A small, alert face even in sleep. She is dressed in a layered combination of children's clothes that have been outgrown by other children and given to her, and salvaged pieces of the park's old vendor uniforms — a faded *LE ROYAUME* apron tied at her waist over a clean pink t-shirt; a pair of denim shorts; one yellow rubber boot on her right foot and one bare foot on her left, the bare foot tucked under the boot for warmth.

She opens her eyes.

She does not yawn. She does not stretch. She sits up. She is awake the way an alert child wakes — completely, without transition.

She looks at the windows. The light is still soft. It is, by her reckoning, about six-thirty.

She rises from the nest. She slips her left foot into a second yellow rubber boot — the pair has been beside the nest. She picks up the tin lunchbox, opens it briefly, takes out a half-eaten granola bar, eats it in three bites, closes the lunchbox. She returns the lunchbox to the nest.

She crosses to the back of the gift shop. A small wooden door — its hinges recently oiled — leads to the building's stockroom. The stockroom contains:

- a stack of unused park-mascot t-shirts in eleven sizes
- a small bicycle with patched tires
- a workbench with a single hammer, a single screwdriver, a roll of duct tape, and a small jar of nails
- a battery charger plugged into a wall outlet whose breaker has, somehow, not tripped in twenty-one years
- a wall calendar dated 2003, never turned past July

MARELLE picks up the small bicycle. She walks it through the gift shop, out the side door, into the dawn.

She speaks her first line of the episode — to the bicycle, as one would speak to a tool one likes.

MARELLE
*(quietly, in Louisiana French)*
Allons, vieille amie.

She mounts the bicycle. She rides.

The camera holds for a long beat on the empty gift shop. The cassette player on the nest, briefly, plays one second of static — a small mechanical resurrection of a tape that is not in the deck. The static stops.

## ACT II — THE PROJECTION  (06:30 — 14:30)

EXT. THE PARK'S MAIN PROMENADE — DAWN.

A wide cracked-concrete path that loops the park. To the left, the SWAMP BOAT RIDE — a long flooded canal with painted plywood cypresses and three small flat-bottomed boats half-sunk at its loading dock. To the right, the BAYOU MARKET PAVILION — an open-sided pavilion that once held food stalls, now empty. Beyond, the COJOLLA TYRELL THEATER, a small domed building painted to suggest a hill with a path winding up its surface and, at its top, a small painted figure of a Cajun storyteller in mid-gesture.

MARELLE rides her bicycle down the promenade. She passes the swamp-boat dock. She passes the market. She is heading for the theater.

She stops outside the theater. She dismounts. She leans the bicycle against a parking-meter-style sign that reads, in faded paint:

> **COJOLLA TYRELL THEATER**
> *holographic presentations on the Louisiana past*
> *every hour on the hour · adult $4 · child $2 · under 5 free*

She approaches the theater's entrance. The door is a heavy steel double door, painted to look like a cypress trunk. The doors are not locked. MARELLE pushes them open.

INT. THE THEATER — DIM.

A small circular auditorium. Approximately forty fixed seats in three concentric rings around a central round platform. The platform is approximately eight feet across, recessed into the floor. Above the platform, mounted in the ceiling, a complex projection rig — eleven separate projector heads arranged in a hexagonal frame, each angled to converge on the platform's center. The rig is, on visual inspection, antique. The projection technology was state-of-the-art for a regional theme park in 1981.

The rig is dark.

MARELLE crosses to a seat in the second ring. She sits. She has, by the wear pattern on the seat, sat here many times.

She places her hands in her lap. She is waiting.

A long beat.

Then —

The eleven projector heads, in sequence, click on. The clicks are soft, mechanical, the sound of relays that have been waiting a long time to engage.

The central platform's recessed light comes up.

The projection begins.

A holographic figure appears on the platform. She is approximately five-foot-four. She is in a green-and-burgundy dress with a long shawl. Her hair is loose. Her face is, for the first time in the deck's entire run, fully resolved.

**THE VOID MOTHER.**

She is, in the hologram, slightly translucent — the marsh-light from the theater's high small windows passes through her. But she is unmistakable, and she is here, and she is looking at the seats.

She finds MARELLE in the second ring.

Her face does not change.

She speaks. Her voice is the void mother's voice — calm, the voice of an older woman who has long since stopped raising her voice for anything.

VOID MOTHER
*(to MARELLE)*
Hello, friend. I see you are here again. I was hoping you would be.

MARELLE
*(quiet, polite, in English with a soft Louisiana inflection)*
Bonjour, madame. I'm here.

VOID MOTHER
*(softly)*
You have grown a quarter-inch since last time. I can see it. Your boots are still too big. They will not be too big much longer.

MARELLE
*(small smile)*
I know.

VOID MOTHER
*(after a beat)*
I have come, this time, to tell you my chapter. You have heard the others. You have heard Hélène, and the priest, and Anya, and the woman at the river, and the chairman, and the nurse, and the man with the motes, and the burlesque ladies, and the boy with the lantern at his side. You have heard, by my count, fourteen of us. Today I am the fifteenth.

MARELLE
*(nodding, sitting up a little straighter)*
Yes, madame.

VOID MOTHER
*(she folds her hands at her waist; she does not approach the platform's edge)*
My chapter is the chapter the deck refused to render in its painted form. I was the first wife of a man in the city of New Orleans, a man whose name, friend, you do not need to know. I had two sons. The first son's name was Antonio. The second son's name is Alberto. The second son is still alive. The first son is not.

*(she pauses)*

I had a sister. Her name is reserved. She and I were close. We grew up in Westport. She was four years younger than I am. She worked, at the time of her death, at a small office a man in the city kept records at. The office's records were not lawful. She had begun to discover this. She was, when she died, in the process of considering what to do about it.

*(she pauses)*

She was at the Sinkhole. The event you have heard about in nine of the previous fifteen presentations. The event the priest will not speak of by name. The event the woman at the river pours her vessels for. The event the lantern's panel was cracked at. The event my own household was, in its way, responsible for. She was there. She did not come back.

*(she pauses)*

My chapter, friend, is the chapter of a woman who left her household after the Sinkhole because she could no longer remain in a household that had been part of the event. I left my husband. I left my sons. I left them because remaining would have meant pretending, daily, that the chapter could be set down. I was unable to pretend. The leaving was not, by the standards of the people who watched it, kind. The leaving was, by my own private accounting, the only honest thing I could do.

*(she lifts her right hand briefly, a small open gesture; she lowers it)*

I have been pregnant, in some sense the deck has not named clearly, for the entire time since. I have been carrying the chapter the household refused to carry. I have, by my own decision, decided to carry it rather than to set it down where someone else could lift it.

*(she looks at MARELLE directly)*

You, friend, are the child of a woman whose name I do not know, in a household I do not know, in a chapter the deck has not yet rendered. You are not my child. You are not my chapter's child. You are, however, by your patient sitting in this seat, the only person who has been willing to listen to my chapter from start to finish without interrupting. I am grateful. I have, in the listening of you, been a little less pregnant than I had been before.

*(she breathes)*

Thank you, friend.

MARELLE
*(small, very polite)*
You're welcome, madame.

VOID MOTHER
*(softly)*
The next presentation will be at eight o'clock. Hélène will be at the kitchen pavilion. She has new things to tell you about the gumbo. You should be there. She has been waiting.

MARELLE
*(nodding)*
I will be there.

VOID MOTHER
*(after a beat)*
And friend.

MARELLE
*(small)*
Yes, madame.

VOID MOTHER
*(gentle)*
When you have finished hearing the rest of us — when the projector has finished its presentations for the season — you will, by my reading of the chapter, leave the park. You will go north. You will go to a woman in Mauriceville who has been expecting you. She is, in some sense, your aunt-by-the-chapter. She does not yet know you are coming, but she has, for nine years, been keeping a kettle on, on Tuesday evenings, against the chance that someone exactly like you might appear at her door. You are someone exactly like that.

MARELLE
*(after a beat, very small)*
Okay.

VOID MOTHER
*(soft smile, the first the deck has rendered)*
I will see you, in some form, again. The chapter is held.

She raises both hands at the level of her stomach, palms up, briefly. Lowers them. She is wearing the gesture.

She fades.

The platform's central light dims.

The eleven projector heads click off, one at a time.

MARELLE sits in her seat in the second ring for a long beat.

She rises.

She exits the theater.

## ACT III — THE PROCESSION OF STATIONS  (14:30 — 24:00)

EXT. PARK PROMENADE — MORNING.

MARELLE rides her bicycle around the loop.

The presentations the void mother promised begin, one at a time, at each of the park's old attractions. The projection equipment at each station — long disconnected from any visible power — activates as MARELLE approaches.

We see, in a series of brief sequences:

---

### THE KITCHEN PAVILION — 08:00

An open-sided pavilion that had once been the park's demonstration kitchen, where Cajun home-cooks did public demonstrations on Saturday afternoons. The pavilion's prep table is intact. The pavilion's gas burner is intact.

The hologram is **HÉLÈNE.** She is in her chef's coat. She is standing at the prep table with a wooden spoon and a small porcelain bowl. The bowl, in the hologram, holds something.

HÉLÈNE
*(to MARELLE)*
*Chéri.* You came. The trinity today is celery, onion, bell pepper. The trinity in your kitchen up north will be celery, onion, bell pepper too. But the priest's-house gumbo at his memorial — that was different. You should know about it. Bring me your hand. I will show you the holy trinity by feel.

MARELLE extends her hand. The hologram brings her hologram-hand to meet it. MARELLE's hand passes through the hologram's hand, but the hologram pretends, briefly, that the contact has occurred. The pretense is, on the camera, a small tenderness the deck has not previously rendered.

Hélène teaches the trinity. The lesson takes four minutes.

She finishes. She smiles. She fades.

---

### THE SWAMP BOAT DOCK — 09:00

The flooded canal. The half-sunk flat-bottomed boats. The painted plywood cypresses.

The hologram is **ANYA.** She is at the rim of one of the boats. She is twenty-three. Her hands are gripping the rim. She is the chapter at the boat's edge.

ANYA
*(softly, in Vietnamese, with English subtitles in the projection)*
*Em bé. Tôi đã giữ chiếc thuyền này trong năm mươi giờ. Tôi đã đến nơi nó đến. Tôi không đến nơi tôi muốn đến. Nhưng tôi đã đến đâu đó. Em sẽ đến đâu đó nữa. Đừng sợ. Nắm tay vào cái gì em chọn. Đừng buông nó nếu nó cần em.*

*(Little one. I held this boat for fifty hours. It got where it was going. I did not get where I wanted to go. But I got somewhere. You will get somewhere too. Don't be afraid. Hold on to what you choose. Don't let go of it if it needs you.)*

MARELLE
*(after a beat, in English)*
Will you be at the kitchen Madame Hélène talked about?

ANYA
*(small smile)*
I am at every kitchen, *em bé.* I am especially at the kitchen in Mauriceville. I will be there when you arrive. I will be the cup beside the kettle.

She fades.

### THE CHURCH FACADE — 10:00

A small chapel facade, a third-scale replica of an Acadian country church. The doors are open. Inside the facade, a small stage with a wooden desk, a crucifix on a stand, a rotary telephone.

The hologram is **FATHER COSGRAVE.** He is at his desk. He is in his black shirt and white collar.

FATHER COSGRAVE
*(softly, to MARELLE)*
Friend. Sit a moment. You may sit on the stoop. I have not much time today. I have decided not to take calls during this presentation; the gentleman from Beaumont will understand. The door, friend, was locked. The door is still locked. But the door, when you come back through here one day, will be a door you, yourself, get to choose to open or not. That is the difference. Until then, friend, do not be in a hurry to find out what is in any locked door. The doors keep what they keep. Some of what they keep is for them. Some of what they keep is for the people who built them. Not all of it is for us.

He hangs up an imaginary phone, three times. The chapter's signature.

He fades.

THE UN-NAMED FIGURE

## ACT IV — THE FROG  (24:00 — 32:30)

EXT. THE LAGOON — AFTERNOON.

MARELLE rides her bicycle to the back of the park. The very back. Past the maintenance shed. Past the old peacock pen (peacocks have been gone since 2003). Through a small grove of cypress.

She arrives at the park's deepest pond. Called, on the old park map, **THE LAGOON OF THE BAYOU SAINT.**

The lagoon is approximately sixty feet across. Dark water. Cypress at its perimeter. A small wooden dock extending from the bank.

MARELLE dismounts. She walks the bicycle to the dock. She leans it against a cypress. She walks out onto the dock.

She sits at the dock's end. Her boots dangle over the water but do not touch it.

She waits.

A long beat.

Then —

The water in the lagoon's center begins, very slowly, to rise.

A great dome of dark green surfaces. The dome is approximately the size of a small car. The dome is the back of a frog.

The frog rises further. Its eyes — large, golden, slit-pupiled — surface above the waterline. Each eye is approximately the size of a saucepan. The eyes are watching MARELLE.

The frog rises until its head and forequarters are above the water. Its body remains submerged. Its head is now approximately twenty feet from the dock's end.

The frog is approximately the size of a Volkswagen Beetle. Its skin is dark green, with smaller patches of brighter green and one deep amber patch at its throat. The amber patch pulses, gently, at a slightly faster rhythm than the chassis at the booth.

MARELLE looks at the frog.

The frog looks at MARELLE.

A long beat.

THE FROG speaks. The voice is deep, calm, slightly amused, very old. The voice carries across the water without amplification.

THE FROG
*Bonjour, ma petite.*

MARELLE
*(small)*
Bonjour, monsieur le grenouille.

THE FROG
*(small chuckle, the deep registers of which ripple the lagoon's surface)*
You may call me Saint, *ma petite,* if it pleases you. It does not, in fact, please me. I would prefer that you call me whatever you choose to call me. I have been called many things by many small people across many years.

MARELLE
*(after a beat)*
I will call you *Grenouille.*

THE FROG
*(satisfied)*
*Grenouille* will do.

A pause.

GRENOUILLE
*(softer)*
*Ma petite.* I have been waiting for you to come to the dock. I have been waiting for many days. The presentations are nearly finished. You have, by my count, four more to attend before the season's projector has finished its work. The remaining four are the chairman, the nurse, the man with the motes, and the woman from the cassette-tape veil. After those four, the projector will go dark for the season. After that, *ma petite,* the chapter you have been listening to will end.

MARELLE
*(small)*
Will the projector come back next year?

GRENOUILLE
*(a slow blink — the eyes close, the eyes open)*
I do not know. I do not control the projector. The projector controls itself. The projector has been deciding, by some method of its own, who comes to the seats and what to show them. The projector showed the void mother today. The projector chose her, because she was ready. The projector will show the others as they are ready. After they are all ready, the projector may decide to rest. The projector may decide to begin again. I have observed projectors for a long time. They have their own seasons. They do not require my permission.

MARELLE
*(after a beat)*
Grenouille.

GRENOUILLE
*Ma petite.*

MARELLE
*(small)*
The void mother said I should go to the woman in Mauriceville.

GRENOUILLE
*(softly)*
The void mother is correct.

MARELLE
*(after a long beat)*
But I have lived here.

GRENOUILLE
*(very gently)*
You have. You have lived here well. You have built the nest. You have ridden the bicycle. You have attended the presentations. You have been a good listener and a careful, brave child. The park has been honored to host you.

A pause.

GRENOUILLE
*(softly)*
*Ma petite.* The world that has come before — the world of the priest, of the kitchen, of the un-named figure, of the cousins, of the boat, of the offering, of the medium, of the count — that world is being held by the projector for you to receive. The projector is showing it to you because the projector has decided you are the world that comes after. You are the world that will be. You are not required to be that world. You are, however, allowed to be that world. The void mother thinks you are ready. I think you are ready. The projector thinks you are ready. The kettle in Mauriceville has been on for nine years. The chamomile has been steeping for nine years. The cup is waiting.

MARELLE
*(very small)*
If I go, who will live in the gift shop?

GRENOUILLE
*(after a long beat, very gently)*
The next child, *ma petite.* There will be a next child. There is always a next child. The park has been, since 2003, a park that hosts one child at a time. The previous child was a boy named Caleb. He left in 2012 when he was nine. He went to a woman in Texas who had been keeping a kettle for him. He is now thirty-one. He is well. He sends, on certain Fridays, a postcard to the gift shop's mail slot. The postcards say only: *the kettle is still on.* The boy before Caleb was a girl named Tamika. The girl before Tamika was a boy whose name was withheld from the park's records by his own request. There have been, since 1972, nine children. You are the ninth. The tenth is not yet born.

MARELLE
*(after a long beat)*
What happens to the projector when I leave?

GRENOUILLE
*(quietly)*
The projector waits.

MARELLE
*(softly)*
For the tenth?

GRENOUILLE
*(eyes blinking again, slowly)*
For the tenth. For whoever the tenth turns out to be. The projector is patient. The projector has been patient for fifty-three years.

A long beat.

MARELLE
*(very small)*
Grenouille.

GRENOUILLE
*Ma petite.*

MARELLE
*(small)*
I will go.

GRENOUILLE
*(softly, the deep voice unmistakably moved)*
You are very brave, *ma petite.*

MARELLE
*(small)*
I would like to come back, sometimes.

GRENOUILLE
*(softly)*
You may come back any time. The bicycle will be in the gift shop. The lantern will be there. The book about Louisiana will be there. The nest will be there. The tenth child, when she comes, will not mind sharing. She will, in fact, be glad to have an elder sister visit.

A long beat.

GRENOUILLE
*(very softly)*
*Ma petite.*

MARELLE
*Oui?*

GRENOUILLE
I have something for you. I have been holding it. I am going to bring it to the dock now. Please do not be alarmed. I have not done this in some years.

The frog moves, slowly, toward the dock. The water parts around its enormous bulk. The frog approaches without urgency. The dock creaks, gently, as the frog approaches the end of it.

The frog comes within four feet of MARELLE.

The frog's mouth opens, slowly. The mouth is, on close inspection, full of soft amber light — not the lurid amber of a campfire but the calm honey-color of late afternoon. The light is the same color as the amber patch at its throat.

From the frog's mouth, in the amber light, the tongue rolls forward — long, calm, careful. The tongue places, on the planks of the dock at MARELLE's feet, a small object.

The object is a single VHS cassette tape.

The cassette's label, in burgundy ink, in a hand that is unmistakably the void mother's, reads: **THE WORLD — for M.**

The frog withdraws its tongue. The frog closes its mouth.

MARELLE
*(very small, very polite)*
Merci, Grenouille.

GRENOUILLE
*(very softly)*
*De rien, ma petite.* Take the tape to the woman in Mauriceville. She has a tape deck. She will know what to do with it. You may listen, with her, when she plays it. The tape is yours. It is also the chapter's. You and the chapter are now together.

A long beat.

GRENOUILLE
*(softer still)*
Go gently, *ma petite.*

The frog sinks, slowly, back into the lagoon. The water closes over the dome of its back. The lagoon becomes, again, a quiet dark pond at the back of an abandoned theme park.

A pause.

The amber patch at the throat is briefly visible underwater. It pulses. It fades.

MARELLE picks up the cassette. She holds it in both hands. She looks at it.

She rises.

She walks back down the dock to her bicycle.

## ACT V — THE ROAD NORTH  (32:30 — 37:30)

EXT. PARK PROMENADE — LATE AFTERNOON.

MARELLE attends the remaining four presentations across the rest of the afternoon. We see them briefly, in montage:

EXT. THE PARK'S GATE — DUSK.

MARELLE is at the gate. The small jar of Halsey honey the Triumvirate left her is in her hand. The cassette is in the tin lunchbox, which she has slung over her shoulder on a length of twine. The bicycle she has left in the gift shop, in the nest, for the tenth child.

She is wearing her yellow rubber boots, her park apron, a clean pink t-shirt, and a small backpack of essentials she has decided to bring. The backpack contains:

She slips through the gap in the fence.

She steps onto Highway 90.

She turns north.

She walks.

The light, on the highway, is the last of the day's gold. A great heron stands at the far edge of the marsh on the other side of the road. The heron does not move.

After perhaps a quarter mile, a small grey sedan slows on the highway. The sedan pulls onto the shoulder beside her. The driver's window rolls down.

In the driver's seat: **ELICIA.** She is not in her veil. She is in her ordinary life. Her hair is down. Her face is bare. Her expression is the expression of a woman who has driven six hours to pick up a child she knows by every measure that mattered.

ELICIA
*(small smile)*
The kettle's on. Would you like a ride.

MARELLE
*(after a beat, very small)*
*Oui, s'il vous plaît.*

Elicia leans across and opens the passenger door from the inside. Marelle climbs in. She places her backpack between her feet. The tin lunchbox stays on her lap.

Anything you want to bring up to Mauriceville?

Okay.

EXT. THE PARK — DUSK — WIDE.

The camera holds, wide, on the park's gate. The chain. The fence. The small gap at child-height.

Behind the gate, in the distance, the lagoon. The water is still. The frog is, again, not visible.

The eleven projector heads of the COJOLLA TYRELL THEATER, in sequence — heard, from this distance, as a series of faint clicks — power down for the season.

The light through the cypresses changes.

The park, by mutual decision between the park and the projector and the frog and the eight children who came before, settles into its wait for the tenth.

## ELICIA'S PRODUCTION NOTES  (private)

- LE ROYAUME DU BAYOU is real. it was operated by the Boutin family from 1972 to 2003. it closed when the family's senior partner died and the younger generation declined to take it over. the park has not been redeveloped, demolished, or sold. it sits on a sixteen-acre parcel held in trust by the **Boutin Parish Foundation,** a small private foundation administered by a parish lawyer in Houma. the foundation has, since 2017, had a unique guardianship arrangement that I will describe below.
- MARELLE is real. her full legal name is preserved on this and other public records. she is the ninth child the foundation has hosted. the foundation's arrangement — formalized in 1979 and amended several times since — is that the park hosts one child at a time, between the ages of approximately seven and twelve, who is otherwise unhoused or in an unsuitable placement, until the child is ready to leave for a chosen receiving household elsewhere. the foundation does not advertise the arrangement. the foundation finds the children by recommendation through a small network of social workers, parish priests, and rural-clinic nurses who know the foundation and have, by some quiet logic the foundation does not publicize, agreed with the foundation's method. the foundation has, in its fifty-three-year history, hosted eight children before MARELLE. each child has, by the foundation's records, gone on to a stable adult life. one of them — Caleb, the eighth — does indeed send postcards. the postcards do indeed say *the kettle is still on.* I have one in my own archive in Mauriceville.
- the projection equipment in the COJOLLA TYRELL THEATER was, at the park's closure in 2003, antique even by 1980s standards. it was a custom-built holographic projection rig commissioned by the Boutin family from a small workshop in Lafayette in 1981. the rig used a combination of laser-based phase-encoding and Fresnel mirrors to produce a recognizable 3D figure in the central platform. the rig was, in its day, regionally famous. the rig has not been powered on, by any human hand, since 2003.
- the rig has been powering itself on, on its own schedule, since 2017 — the year MARELLE was born, and the year the foundation's arrangement was last formally amended. the rig has, by its own internal scheduling, been producing presentations for the foundation's hosted child since the foundation's hosted child has been MARELLE. the previous host child — Caleb — reports, in private correspondence with the foundation that he has graciously permitted me to read, that the rig produced presentations for him also, on a similar schedule, during his time at the park (2003–2012). the rig appears to have been doing this for at least eleven years before that — perhaps since the park's closure — and possibly longer. its operation has not been documented in the rig's original maintenance records, which were lost in a 2005 hurricane.
- the holograms. the holograms are the holograms. they are not actors. they are not pre-recorded footage. I have asked the void mother, who appeared on the platform on the day of this filming, what she was. she did not, in the moments after her presentation, address me directly. she did, however, return that evening to the platform — alone, no MARELLE in the seats, just me and the camera — and said: *"friend. I am the void mother. I am present in many places. I am here at the park because I am where the park projects me. I am also at my kitchen counter in the model home, on certain Tuesdays. I am also, occasionally, on Elicia's CRT in Mauriceville at 9:09 PM. these presences are not separable. They are not also continuous. They are what they are. I have no firmer theory."* I do not have a firmer theory either. I have decided to film what I film and let the chapter's metaphysics remain the chapter's.
- the giant frog. **GRENOUILLE.** the frog is real. I have measured it. it is approximately the size of a Volkswagen Beetle. it weighs, by the lagoon's displacement, approximately one thousand four hundred kilograms. it is, by closest taxonomic guess, a species of *Lithobates* that should not exceed three pounds in adulthood. it is, by every available zoological standard, not what it is. it is also calm, articulate, fluent in Louisiana French, and possessed of a strong personal preference about being called "Saint." the Boutin Parish Foundation has been quietly providing the lagoon with a small monthly stipend (water-quality monitoring, mosquito-control fees, a small grant to a USDA biologist who has, separately, agreed not to publish) for as long as the foundation has existed. the foundation has not, by its own records, ever attempted to communicate with the frog. the frog has, by the children's reports, communicated with the children. the frog speaks. the frog does not, by any of the children's accounts, lie.
- the cassette **THE WORLD — for M.** is real. I have it now, in my apartment in Mauriceville. MARELLE and I have, in the four months since I picked her up at Highway 90, played the cassette together on a Tuesday evening with the kettle on, the chamomile steeped, both of us in the wingback chairs by the window. the cassette is approximately ninety minutes of the void mother humming the same song she hummed in S01E03 of this series. the song is the song. it has no words. it has a melody. the melody is, by MARELLE's quiet observation as we listened, "the melody Grenouille's amber patch was pulsing to." I had not noticed. I noticed when she said it. she is correct.
- MARELLE lives with me now. she has her own room in the apartment above the closed Sun-Lo Seafood Café. she has been enrolled in the local public elementary school where she is two grade levels ahead in reading and one level behind in math (a temporary state — she is improving rapidly). her legal arrangement is unusual but is sanctioned by the foundation and reviewed quarterly by the parish lawyer in Houma. she has decided to keep her name. she has decided to call me, in private, *Tata Elicia.* she has decided, on her own, to begin keeping a notebook the way Frank keeps his notebooks. she writes one line at the end of every Tuesday. her line, last Tuesday, was: *"Tata Elicia made gumbo. The trinity by feel. Madame Hélène's way. The gumbo was good. — M."* I had not been able to teach her the trinity from the kitchen pavilion's presentation; she had been able to teach me. she remembered. she knew.
- the chapter at XXI in the deck is the chapter of the world. tradition shows a dancing figure within a wreath at the center of the four creatures. our deck has, with this episode, rendered the chapter as the procession of the world that has come before being received, by a single child, in the seats of a defunct holographic theater in a Louisiana swamp park, with the chapter's keystone — the giant frog at the lagoon's center — delivering the closing benediction and the cassette. the world that has come before has, by its own decision, decided to host its own succession. the world that will be has stepped onto Highway 90 and turned north. the chapter has been held. the season has closed. the chapter is also open. there is room at the door. there is, in particular, room at the door of an apartment above a closed seafood café in Mauriceville, Texas.
