# THE STRATA OF PLANNED COMMUNITY
## a musical archaeology of New Auburn, Harmony Creek Estates, and the eastern Texas hum corridor

> *"the regional record store is the local newspaper of a community
> that has never agreed to be photographed. you don't know what
> they were doing in 1989 in Beaumont until you put the cassette
> on. then you know."*
>
> — Lena Vargas, Static Truths #2, summer 2025

A working reference for the deck's developers + designers: the music
scene that Harmony Creek Estates was built on top of and that, under
the corporate landscaping, has continued to operate. The strata are
chronological. The newest strata is what the kids in vol6 are doing.
The oldest predates the development by twenty years and is, by the
substrate's accounting, still receiving.

Each era has:
- a **bands index** — the canonical and the lost
- one or two **selected tracks** with lyrics or fragments
- **found documents** — reviews, articles, zine clips, liner notes,
  AllMusic-style biographies, a Substack post
- **cross-volume resonance** — what surfaces in Graustark or Smolvud
  if you know where to look

# ERA 0 · THE FOUNDING
## *1969–1979 · before the substations*

Harmony County, before NexCorp, was ranchland. The music scene was
honky-tonk, oilfield-blues, country-rock — the regional substrate
that the Texas-Louisiana border has always run on. The bands of this
era are mostly forgotten in any catalog the internet has indexed,
but their records show up at estate sales and at Cosmic Comics'
back-room dollar bins, and three of them are load-bearing for the
deck's substrate theory.

## bands index

- **THE BOIS D'ARC BOYS** (1969–1976) — Houston-via-Beaumont
  five-piece country-rock band whose 1971 LP *Crepe Myrtle Roadside*
  was self-released on the band's own label (Bois d'Arc Records)
  and pressed in approximately 600 copies. Singer **Carl Bordelon**
  was killed in a tour van accident on US-90 in late 1976; the band
  did not continue. Carl is, by canonical confirmation in vol5's
  back-of-the-house ledgers at D'Ambrosio's, the great-uncle of
  **Carl Boudreaux**, the sixteen-year-old drummer of Suburban
  Blight. The drum kit Carl plays right-handed (because that's how
  Nate's father had it) was, before it was Walt Yost's, Carl
  Bordelon's. The kit has been in continuous use since 1971.

- **HÉLÈNE PIERRE-LOUIS & THE CRESCENT BAND** (1970–1973) — short-
  lived Creole soul project Hélène fronted in the years before
  CHEZ HÉLÈNE opened. Three 45s, no LP. Hélène's voice on the
  surviving recordings is the same voice the void mother hums in
  vol5's Empress chapter; the deck does not, in the prose,
  acknowledge this connection. The Crescent Band's bassist later
  played in **MARGARET'S CAMERA** (Era I).

- **SAINT AUGUSTINE GRASS** (1974–1978) — Houston post-psych
  outfit, three LPs on a small Austin label. Recorded their second
  album, *The Field is Listening* (1976), in a barn on what was
  then the unimproved Harmony County land that NexCorp would, in
  1991, develop as Harmony Creek Estates' Phase II. The barn was
  demolished in 1989. The album's title track features, beneath
  the band, a low 47 Hz drone the engineer attributed at the time
  to *"a fault in the master tape stock."* The drone is, on
  side-by-side analysis with the substation hum, identical. The
  field was already listening.

- **THE WAGNER COMBO** (1972–1975) — country-jazz lounge act, a
  three-piece, regular Friday-night booking at the now-demolished
  Empire Roller Rink in Lake Charles. The trio's namesake bandleader
  was Walter Wagner, no relation to the **Wagner** of the Handsome
  Triumvirate from Vol I — except that Walter Wagner was the
  Triumvirate Wagner's father, a fact the Triumvirate Wagner does
  not, in vol1's prose, ever acknowledge. The Empire Roller Rink's
  burned-out shell sits, in 2025, two blocks east of the Foxhole.

## selected track · *"The Field is Listening" (1976) · Saint Augustine Grass*

A 7:14 closer to the band's second LP. Drone-anchored, two guitars
in a slow alternating figure, the drummer working brushes through
the whole back half. The vocal — by guitarist **Linda Marrero** —
is one verse, repeated three times, with the second pass dropped
an octave into chest voice and the third pass spoken.

> *the field is listening, friend*
> *the field has been listening, friend*
> *the field will continue to listen, friend*
> *long after you forget how to talk*

Marrero is alive, lives in Galveston, declined the deck's request
for an interview in 2024, replied to a follow-up letter only:
*"I don't want to be in the book. I want the field to be in the
book. — L. M."*

## found document · review · *No Depression* (online, 2014 retrospective)

> **SAINT AUGUSTINE GRASS — *The Field is Listening* (1976)**
>
> *Reissue review · 5/5 · by Carla Vega-Brennan*
>
> *Houston's Saint Augustine Grass put out three LPs in four years
> and then dissolved over a contract dispute that nobody now
> remembers the terms of. Their middle record* — The Field is
> Listening (Spindle Records, 1976) *— has been out of print since
> the original pressing sold out in 1979, fetches sums in the low
> three figures when it surfaces at all on Discogs, and has, this
> spring, finally been reissued by Light in the Attic with the
> original gatefold sleeve faithfully reproduced.*
>
> *What strikes the careful listener about this record, even more
> than its languid second-side workouts or Linda Marrero's
> chillingly understated lead vocals, is the low electrical drone
> that sits beneath every track. The drone is consistent across
> the album — same pitch, same modulation — even though the album
> was, by the band's own production notes, recorded across three
> separate sessions in two different rooms.*
>
> *Drummer Felipe Marrero (Linda's brother) attributes the drone,
> in his 2009 oral-history interview, to "the room itself." He
> doesn't elaborate. The room, by his account, was a barn on a
> ranch east of Houston that "the engineer's cousin owned" and
> that "isn't there anymore." Which is true; the parcel was
> developed in the early 1990s into one of the most over-marketed
> planned communities of its decade.*
>
> *The drone is the album. The drone is what makes The Field is
> Listening one of the strangest records to come out of Texas in
> the seventies. The drone is what the reissue's pristine vinyl
> pressing has finally rendered audible to listeners who never
> knew it was there. The drone is the field, listening.*
>
> *— C.V.B.*

(Carla Vega-Brennan is, the deck's archivist has quietly noted, the
older sister of **Carla Vega** at Lot 47, Harmony Creek Estates.
Carla-the-elder lives in Austin now, writes for *No Depression* and
*Aquarium Drunkard*, and has, in private correspondence Maya found
during the Henderson Donation archaeology, sent her sister three
issues of the Glitch Report's 1988 run with a handwritten note:
*"this is where my work and your noise come from the same place. —
big sis."*)

## cross-volume resonance · Era 0 → vol5 + vol7

- The 47 Hz drone on *The Field is Listening* is the same drone the
  Glitch Report's 1988 editors document and the same drone the
  carrier signal in vol7 runs on. The drone has been recorded on a
  commercial LP since 1976. Nobody in the industry has, in fifty
  years, formally identified the drone.
- Hélène Pierre-Louis's voice on the Crescent Band 45s is, on
  spectral analysis, the void mother's voice in vol5's Empress
  chapter. The deck reserves whether the void mother *is* Hélène
  under a different name, *was related to* Hélène, or *recorded*
  Hélène and now hums the songs to herself in private. All three
  readings are permitted.
- Walter Wagner's death in 1975 — the canonical event Walter Wagner's
  son does not discuss in vol1 — was a heart attack at the Empire
  Roller Rink during a Friday-night set. Walter Wagner the Combo
  bandleader collapsed during a song he had written six months
  earlier called *"Smolvud Sunrise."* The song's title is the same
  title as the final chapter of vol7. Walter Wagner had never been
  to Oregon. The song is, in the surviving recording, fifty-two
  seconds long. The recording exists.

# ERA I · THE HUM
## *1988–1992 · the substations come on*

NexCorp Power Systems (the predecessor to NexCorp Residential
Solutions) energized the Harmony County substation grid in the
spring of 1988. The hum followed in months. The Glitch Report's
collective began publishing that summer. The bands of this era —
the ones that mattered — are the bands that registered the hum
without knowing what they were registering and that wrote about it
in song titles, sleeve art, and the dedications on their cassettes.

## bands index

- **LOCUST SEVENTEEN** (1988–1991) — Beaumont four-piece punk band.
  Named themselves after the number sequence the Glitch Report
  transcribed in Issue #1 (*"Nine. Locust. Seventeen. Door."*) —
  bassist Andrew Pelletier had heard the sequence on his car radio
  one night on US-69 north of Beaumont, had not known where it came
  from, had named the band after it the next morning. Released one
  cassette EP (*Door,* 1989, sold at shows for $3) and one LP
  (*Nine,* 1990, pressed in 300 copies). Broke up in late 1991
  after Pelletier's apartment was burglarized and the masters for
  what would have been their second LP (working title: *Seventeen*)
  were stolen along with his stereo. Pelletier did not, by his own
  account in a 2014 oral-history interview, file a police report.
  *"the masters were not the point. the point was that they wanted
  the masters. that was the news of the burglary."*

- **MARGARET'S CAMERA** (1989–1993) — Houston / Beaumont indie-pop
  trio formed by three friends of **Margaret Henderson** in the
  months after her disappearance. The band's name is hers. Their
  one full-length, *Sixteen* (1991, self-released, ~400 copies), is
  dedicated *"for M. who would be eighteen this year if she were
  here."* The bassist was the same bassist who had played in
  Hélène Pierre-Louis's Crescent Band (Era 0). The drummer was
  a young **Joanna LeMoine** — the same Joanna who, by vol5's
  Hermit chapter, will be writing poetry on the walls of the
  Graustark ruins in chalk and stolen lipstick. Joanna's drumming
  on *Sixteen* is, by every reviewer who has heard it, the LP's
  best feature.

- **THE EDITORS** (1989–1992) — not the British indie band of the
  same name; this is an earlier, smaller act from Harmony County
  whose members are, by careful long inference, the same people
  who edited the Harmony Creek Glitch Report under the byline
  *"the editors."* They released exactly one cassette (no title,
  black ink on cream, 1990, distributed by hand to approximately
  forty people) and then dissolved alongside the zine in 1992. The
  cassette is, by Maya's archive, present in the Henderson
  Donation box #4. Maya has not, by mid-August 2025, played it
  through. She is, by her own account, *"saving it for the right
  evening."*

- **STATIC LIFE** (1988–1990) — Lake Charles synth-pop project,
  one woman with a Roland Juno-60 and a TASCAM 4-track. Released
  one cassette EP (*Voice / Carrier,* 1989) and then went silent.
  The artist's name, on the cassette: **E. Aday.** The
  artist's-name-as-credited has not been identified in any public
  record. Maya, separately, has identified E.A. as the same E.A.
  who signs the *"A Walk"* prose piece in Glitch Report Issues #1
  and #2. E.A. is, by the present canonical reading, a single
  person who edited the zine, made the cassette, walked the
  perimeter, and is, in 2025, the woman who replied to Maya's
  email and who lives in Lake Charles.

## selected track · *"Door" (1989) · Locust Seventeen*

The closing track of the *Door* EP. 2:48. The band's most direct
song. Pelletier on lead vocal, the band tight behind him, a single
clean bass figure that does not change for the whole song.

> *they said the building was empty*
> *they said the windows were boarded*
> *they said the door was locked*
>
> *the door was locked*
> *the door is still locked*
> *the door will continue to be locked*
> *the door is not the point*
>
> *the point is the building*
> *the building is empty*
> *but the lights are on*
> *and the lights are warm*

(The "nine words" in the chorus — *the door was locked, the door is
still locked* — are the same nine words the gentleman from Beaumont
in vol5's Hierophant chapter (S01E05 of the Pomegranate Hour, my
own version; in canon, **Father Quentin Paul** speaks the line in
the Hierophant chapter directly) repeats every week on Father
Cosgrave's broadcast. Quentin Paul, who is the Hierophant of vol5,
went to high school in Beaumont with Andrew Pelletier. The line is
not, by either man's account, plagiarized in either direction. The
line, by both their accounts, was *"a thing somebody at our high
school used to say."*)

## selected track · *"M." (1991) · Margaret's Camera*

The opening track of *Sixteen.* 4:14. A waltz. Joanna LeMoine on
brushes throughout — no snare strikes, no fills, just the soft
shuffle on the rim and the head. The vocal is by the singer Lena
Aoki, who has not been reachable since 1995.

> *M. wore her camera on a strap around her neck*
> *every photograph was a small specific weight*
> *she could not, by the third year, take it off*
> *she did not, by the fourth year, want to*
>
> *M. went to the field on the December evening*
> *M. said she would be back by dark*
> *M. is, by every available accounting, still in the field*
> *the camera is, by every available accounting, with her*
>
> *we have decided not to play this song live*
> *we have decided to let the record carry it*
> *the record is what we made instead of going to look*
> *the record is what we have*

The song's tempo is 47 BPM. This is not an accident; Joanna LeMoine,
in a 2003 letter to her then-partner Douglas Forte that survives in
his accumulation, wrote: *"I set the tempo to 47 because the
Saint Augustine record sets the drone to 47 and I figured if M.
was anywhere she was somewhere those numbers connected."*

## found document · liner notes · *"Voice / Carrier" (E. Aday, 1989, Static Life)*

(Hand-typed in courier on the inside of the J-card insert, single
column, no paragraph breaks.)

> *side one is my voice singing the alphabet at thirteen different
> tempos against the same drone. side two is the drone alone.
> nobody asked me to make this. I made it because the drone is
> already there. you can hear it on your radio between stations
> at certain hours. you can hear it on certain wells. you can
> hear it under your refrigerator. you can hear it under the
> Saint Augustine Grass record from 1976 if you have the
> patience. nobody is going to put this cassette on the radio.
> I am putting the cassette in the small boxes around the
> county. if you have found one I am glad. if you have heard the
> drone I am sorry. if you have walked the perimeter at night I
> am with you. — e.a.*

## cross-volume resonance · Era I → vol5 + vol7

- **Joanna LeMoine on drums** in Margaret's Camera (1989–1993),
  before she becomes the un-named figure of vol5's Hermit and Star.
  Her brushwork on *Sixteen* is one of the deck's quietest
  forward-prophecies — the *"soft shuffle on the rim and the head"*
  is the same auditory register vol5's Hermit chapter renders her
  in.
- **E. Aday** (Static Life / Glitch Report's E.A.) — same person,
  recorded the drone before she edited the zine that documented the
  drone. The deck reserves what she does in vol7.
- **Locust Seventeen's bassist Andrew Pelletier** — *the gentleman
  from Beaumont* who has been calling Rick at Cosmic Comics for
  twenty-four years (and Father Cosgrave's substitute on Channel
  14 for nineteen) is, by canonical confirmation in vol5's
  Hierophant prose, Andrew Pelletier. He went into a different
  life after his apartment was burglarized in 1991. The "different
  life" is the gentleman from Beaumont's life. The masters for
  *Seventeen* — the unreleased second LP — are, by Frasier
  Temple's quiet warehouse note, in the cannery basement's Rack 14
  (unlabeled). Frasier did not put them there. They were already
  there when Frasier installed Rack 17 in 2002. The masters are,
  by Frasier's read, *"how the cannery rack first started to
  notice the hum corridor."*

# ERA II · THE DIALUP YEARS
## *1993–2002 · the warehouse begins*

The internet arrives. Frasier Temple, in his late twenties, leaves
his job as a D'Ambrosio's cook and moves into the industrial corner
of Graustark that will become his Cathedral of Rust and Code. WJMR
goes off the air in 1996. Channel 14 in Westport continues. The
regional bands of this era either go online (badly) or stay
analog (defiantly). The hum is now in every shopping mall's HVAC.

## bands index

- **STATIC.FM** (1995–1999) — Frasier Temple's first project before
  he started calling what he made *demons*. A nine-track CD-R
  (*Carrier Wave* / 1996 / 33 copies) and a longer follow-up
  (*Backchannel* / 1998 / 71 copies). Both releases are credited to
  *static.fm,* lowercase, all one word. Frasier did not perform
  these live. Frasier did not tour. Frasier mailed copies to people
  he liked. Approximately twenty of the *Backchannel* recipients
  are now, in 2025, identifiable as members of what the deck calls
  *the substrate-literate network*. The release credits read:
  *"all sounds produced by static.fm at the cathedral. with
  apologies to anybody who decoded the second half of side B."*
- **THE EXTENSION CORDS** (1994–2001) — Westport, Louisiana, garage-
  rock four-piece with a regular Friday booking at the same
  feed-store-converted-to-Channel-14-studio that Father Cosgrave
  broadcast from. They played their sets after the broadcast ended.
  Three LPs, one EP. Their guitarist, **Sam "Sammy" Whitfield**, is
  Chief Miller's older brother and Sam Miller's uncle. Sammy
  Whitfield, after the band's 2001 dissolution, taught high-school
  music in Lake Charles and was, by Sam's mother's account, the
  reason Sam picked up the Telecaster in eighth grade. Sammy died
  of pancreatic cancer in 2019. Sam was thirteen. The Telecaster
  Sam owns is Sammy's. The Telecaster Jesse owns is, by the
  identical serial number pattern Sammy bought a matched pair of
  in 1994, also Sammy's. Jesse received his at a band practice in
  March 2025 from Sam, who had been keeping both since the funeral
  and who, on the day she handed Jesse the one she had decided was
  his, said only: *"this is the one Sammy thought was yours."*
- **NRM** (1998–2003) — the Neo-Renaissance Men. Not, strictly
  speaking, a band. A loose collective of artists, programmers,
  poets, and one ex-priest who met weekly at *Adelphia's Deli* in
  Graustark over baklava. They released one cassette compilation
  (*The Deli Tape,* 2001, 99 copies, individually numbered) of
  spoken-word, ambient drone, and a single 23-minute closing piece
  by Frasier Temple titled *"the demon at rest."* The cassette is
  the closest thing the NRM ever had to a public-facing artifact.
  The collective is canonically the seedbed for Frasier's
  warehouse work, Elicia Duchane's web-series, the early forms of
  Joanna LeMoine's wall-writing practice, and one specific
  recurring detail in vol7: **the Pizza Pirate SCUMM machine**, by
  Frasier's own quiet acknowledgment, was prototyped in
  Adelphia's back room in 2002 by Frasier and an NRM member named
  *Dickens* — who, the deck reserves, may or may not have been
  Dickens Dean before Dickens Dean had a body of evidence
  attached to him.
- **GLITCH KITTEN** (1999–2004) — Houston electroclash project.
  Three EPs, one LP. Mentioned dismissively by Deputy Dwayne in
  vol6's Part VI draft (*"go listen to some of that Glitch Kitten
  noise you kids like"*). The dismissal is, by canonical reading,
  Dwayne quoting a phrase his own teenage daughter — who is now
  in her late thirties and a senior bookkeeper at a NexCorp
  subsidiary — used to use. Dwayne has not, in this lifetime,
  knowingly listened to a Glitch Kitten record. Glitch Kitten's
  drummer, **Maxine Phan**, played on the first Suburban Blight
  demo as a session guest in late 2024; Carl was sixteen and was
  not yet on the kit on every track.

## selected track · *"backchannel (track 11)" (1998) · static.fm*

A 16:47 closer. No vocal. A single sustained drone (47 Hz,
naturally) with, in the upper register, a sequence of small
synthetic blips that, on careful transcription by Maya in 2025,
match the dot-dash patterns of the **"nine. locust. seventeen.
door."** sequence read in Morse. The blips are spaced over the
full sixteen minutes; you cannot decode them at speed; you can
decode them at quarter-speed if you know what you are listening
for. Frasier did not, by his quiet acknowledgment in a 2023 letter
to Elicia, intend the Morse to be decodable at all. He had embedded
the pattern as *"a kind of address. for the recipients I was not
sure had ears yet."*

The track is what survives of Frasier's hands before he started
calling them Demons.

## selected track · *"Friday at Eight Thirty" (1998) · The Extension Cords*

The band's regional hit, from their 1998 LP *After Cosgrave.* 3:21.
Sammy Whitfield on lead. Driving riff, three chords ascending, a
small turnaround in the chorus that, by Carl Boudreaux's family-
gathering recognition in early 2025, is the same turnaround Carl
later used in **Grid Failure.** Carl had heard the song his whole
life. Carl had not, before 2025, registered that he had heard it.
Carl had not, before 2025, known that the song was his great-uncle
Carl's bandmate's song from twenty years before he was born.

The lyric:

> *the broadcast ends at eight twenty-eight*
> *the room clears at eight twenty-nine*
> *we set up at eight thirty*
> *the priest stays for the first song every Friday*
> *the priest does not stay for the second*
> *the priest has, in his time on the air, hung up the phone*
> *three times every Tuesday and Thursday*
> *for nineteen years*
>
> *we have, in our time on the floor after the air,*
> *played the same three chords every Friday*
> *for seven years*
>
> *the chords were taught to me by my brother*
> *the brother who is, this year, a county sheriff*
> *the brother who does not, this year, come to the show*
> *the brother who will, in twenty-five years,*
> *bury a girl my niece will go to school with*

(The verse is canonical autobiography. Sammy Whitfield's brother is
Chief Miller. Chief Miller did not come to Sammy's shows. The
"girl my niece will go to school with" is a line Sammy wrote in
1998. Maya Daigle is, by the deck's careful arithmetic, the niece's
age cohort. The girl is reserved.)

## found document · oral history · *Texas Music Quarterly,* spring 2008

> **"WE WERE THE BAND THAT PLAYED AFTER THE BROADCAST" — AN ORAL
> HISTORY OF THE EXTENSION CORDS**
>
> *by Theresa Yoo*
>
> *Sammy Whitfield, the lead singer and rhythm guitarist of the
> Extension Cords, agreed to talk to me on the porch of his
> house in Lake Charles on a Sunday afternoon in October. He
> brought out a pitcher of sweet tea, a stack of his old
> setlists, and the original print proof of the After Cosgrave
> LP gatefold. He spoke for two hours, mostly without my
> prompting. What follows is the transcript, edited only for
> length.*
>
> *"We played a feed store. That's the part everybody likes the
> best when I tell it. We played a feed store in Westport,
> Louisiana, every Friday at 8:30 PM, for seven years. The feed
> store was, by Friday at 8:30 PM, the studio of a public-
> access cable show called* Conversations with Father Cosgrave
> *and the priest, Father Cosgrave, was, every Friday, the last
> man out the door. He would shake our hands. He would pour
> himself a Dixie cup of the sweet tea from the cooler. He would
> stand in the back of the room for our first song. He would
> nod at me. He would go home.*
>
> *"He never stayed for the second song. We thought, the first
> year, that he didn't like our music. We thought, the second
> year, that he liked our music too much. We thought, the third
> year, that he had a rule about it. By the fifth year I had
> stopped thinking about it. The priest came. The priest stayed
> for one song. The priest left. That was the priest.*
>
> *"What I will tell you about Father Cosgrave is that he had a
> particular way of nodding at me at the end of the song he
> stayed for. He would nod once. He would nod a second time. He
> would, on the third nod, mouth a single word I could never
> quite read. I asked him about it once, in our fourth year. He
> said:* 'son, the word is for the song. it isn't for you. you
> are doing fine.' *And that was it. I did not ask again.*
>
> *"I will say this about being in a band that plays after the
> public-access cable program every Friday in a feed store in
> Westport, Louisiana: it is the kind of thing that gives you,
> as a young man, the small specific gift of knowing that
> nothing you ever do for the rest of your life is going to be
> the most singular thing you have done. Which is, I want to be
> clear, freeing. We were not famous. We were not heard. We
> were, for seven years, the band that played after the
> broadcast, and the broadcast had its own audience and we did
> not really overlap. That was fine. We played our songs. The
> priest came. The priest left. We packed up at one in the
> morning. We drove home in our two cars.*
>
> *"My brother did not come to our shows. He never has come to
> the band's shows. He has, since I was eighteen, been the kind
> of man who does not come to a brother's shows. He will, when
> he hears this interview, possibly call me about it. He will,
> possibly, not. Either way I have, by being interviewed today,
> done the thing I intended to do when I agreed to be

# ERA III · THE LOST DECADE
## *2003–2014 · the cannery rack goes in*

After NRM dissolves and Frasier formally moves to his warehouse,
the regional scene fragments. The bands of this era are mostly
local, mostly short-lived, mostly recorded on iPhones or in
basements. The internet absorbs the distribution. The substrate
absorbs the rest. Frasier installs Rack 17 in the Alsea Bay
Cannery basement in 2002 (Era II's tail) and the cannery rack
begins, by Frasier's quiet ongoing read, to *receive* music it had
not been sent.

## bands index

- **MANTEL BLACK** (2006–2010) — Houma, Louisiana doom-metal four-
  piece. Self-released a single LP (*The Sixth Bin,* 2007, 121
  copies on vinyl, pressed at a regional pressing plant whose
  records show no order under this band's name — the plant's
  intake-log entry for the relevant week reads only *"private
  pressing, paid cash"*). The album was recorded in *"an abandoned
  industrial space on the bayou — the engineer's brother had
  access — we don't remember the address."* The album's bin number
  for the master tape, by their handwritten log on the inside of
  the sleeve, was bin six. The tape was, by their account,
  *"stored in the rack in the basement when we were done."* The
  basement is the Alsea Bay Cannery basement. The rack is, on
  Frasier's small careful inventory, Rack 06 — labeled, on the
  rack's tag, *"RECEIVE."* Mantel Black's master tape has been
  receiving since 2007. The band did not know they had left it.
- **THE VOLUME EIGHT** (2010–2014) — Lake Charles post-rock
  collective, three EPs and a single 41-minute live recording. The
  band's name was a small joke — they made what they called *"the
  music that should have been on volume eight of any compilation,
  the one that came after the canon and that nobody played."* The
  band dissolved in 2014 when their drummer, **K. Akhtar**,
  relocated to *"a small Texas border town none of us had heard
  of"* — by his own subsequent account: **Mauriceville.** Akhtar
  is, in 2025, one of the medium's hundred-and-forty receivers
  (this was my non-canonical detail but it folds in cleanly here
  as a quiet vol7-prefiguring fact). Akhtar in 2025 lives across
  the street from the closed Sun-Lo Seafood Café. He has not, in
  eleven years, played the drums.
- **CARLA VEGA & THE NOISE COMPLAINT** (2009–2013) — Carla Vega
  (then twenty-eight, before she moved into Lot 47) fronted this
  Austin-based experimental project that recorded, in its four
  years of existence, exactly nine pieces, each consisting of a
  single field recording of a household appliance set against a
  vocal that the appliance's hum had — by Carla's careful
  documentation — been carrying as a subharmonic the whole time.
  The project disbanded after a 2013 small-club show in Houston
  that, by Carla's own subsequent essay in *The Wire,* was
  attended by *"a single man in a charcoal suit who did not, at
  any point in the seventy-five-minute set, look away from the
  stage."* Carla quit the project the next morning.
- **MARELLE BOUTIN** (2013, single release) — a single 12-inch on
  the Halsey-area microlabel **Bayou Receivers** by an artist
  credited only as *"M. Boutin,"* age unknown. *(Marelle was my
  non-canonical name for vol7's feral child at the swamp park —
  the deck reserves whether this 12-inch is the same person, an
  older relative, or simply a name the substrate borrowed in
  advance of itself.)* The 12-inch is *Three Vessels* — Side A is
  a 19-minute recording of a riverbank at night; Side B is the
  same recording played backwards. The release notes, on the
  inside sleeve: *"the river accepted the offering. the offering
  is for whoever asked. — m.b."* Pressed in 89 copies. Twenty-
  three are in Rick's back-room archive at Cosmic Comics, all
  donated anonymously across the years; Rick has, in his ledger,
  noted: *"the same handwriting brings these in every nine months.
  I have stopped opening the door when the bell rings during
  those weeks. I want the handwriting to keep happening."*

# ERA IV · THE FOXHOLE GENERATION
## *2017–2023 · the small careful scene reorganizes*

The Foxhole opens in 2019 in the Live Oak Street strip mall in New
Auburn. The owner — a Houma woman in her late thirties named
**Pip Henson** — books regional acts, all-ages until 10 PM, $5
cover at the door. The Foxhole is, by every band that plays it in
the 2019–2023 window, *"the kind of venue where the booking guy
listens to your tape and then asks you questions about it that show
he listened."* Pip is not, in 2025, in the deck's foreground; she
becomes load-bearing in the run-up to vol6's chapter twenty.

## bands index

- **DJ CHESS** (2019–) — San Antonio DJ. Plays records and a small
  modular rig. The Foxhole hosts him on Saturdays approximately
  once a quarter. His sets average ninety minutes and consist of

  band is called, on Sam Miller's bedroom door, in marker, **THE

# CLOSING NOTE

The strata work the way geological strata work: from the top down
you do not see the bottom, but the bottom is what holds the top
up. Suburban Blight's *Substation Nine* is, in the room on August
22, just a long song with a six-minute jam in the back half. The
substrate is what makes it more than that. The substrate has been
in the field since 1976 — by *The Field is Listening*'s recording
date — and the substrate has been documented in print since 1988,
