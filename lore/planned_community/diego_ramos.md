# DIEGO RAMOS · accumulation
## drummer · missing since 11:52 PM Saturday · last known at his grandmother's

Nineteen. Black curls. A small scar on his left forearm from a snare-rim catch. The Gallatin Band's drummer since he was sixteen — recruited at a Lake Charles open mic by the band's bassist, who heard him play on a kit he had borrowed from a youth-group church friend who had borrowed it from his uncle. Sam's boyfriend since the August they were both seventeen. Lives — *lived* — with his grandmother **GRACIELA RAMOS** at 1414 Ashberry Drive (not 892, which is where the wrong-address pickup driver was sent on the morning of vol6 ch1; Diego knew that 892 was the listed address because Diego put 892 on a form a year ago for a specific reason; the meta-game will work out which reason).

Diego is not in the room. Diego's accumulation is what he left behind. Most of it is in two places — his bedroom at his grandmother's, and the band's practice space in the back room of Cosmic Comics, which Rick lets the band use Tuesday and Thursday nights after close. The rest is scattered — pieces with Sam, pieces with the band, pieces no one has located.

## II. THE COMIX — *"PHASE III"*
### by **DIEGO MONTOYA** (pseudonym; nobody but Sam, Rick, and Maya knows DIEGO MONTOYA is Diego Ramos)

A black-and-white photocopied minicomic series Diego has been drawing since he was sixteen and a half. He uses the surname Montoya — his mother's maiden name; she died when he was four; he never knew her but the surname is his to use — because he does not want the comic associated with his real name in the public scene. Rick at Cosmic Comics stocks the comix on the back counter, four copies at a time, $2 each, with the change going into a small jar Diego picks up every other month. The jar has, at the time of vol6 ch1, $87.40 in it. Diego has not picked it up in three months.

The series is at issue #11. Each issue is 24 pages, 5.5x8.5 inches, saddle-stitched, black ink on cream paper, drawn in a slightly unrefined Crumb-meets-Lynda-Barry register with occasional moments of unsettling photorealism in single panels.

### the premise

> **PHASE III** is the story of CARLO, a fifteen-year-old boy who lives with his grandmother in a planned community called **HARLEY CREEK MEADOWS**. Carlo gradually discovers that Harley Creek Meadows is, by varying readings, (a) a corporate-owned residential simulation built on top of an older incident the corporation has covered up, (b) a normal suburb that is also a corporate-owned residential simulation, or (c) a simulation that is also a normal suburb. The narrative does not commit. The comic's recurring joke is that Carlo treats each new revelation about the simulation's reality as a normal weekday inconvenience. "Oh, my grandmother is a hologram today. I guess I'll eat cereal."

### selected issues

**ISSUE #1 — "THE COOLER GLASS"**

Carlo at his summer job at the Harley Creek Kwik-Stamp gas station. A man in a golf shirt enters. The man looks at the back cooler. The man looks at Carlo's reflection in the cooler glass instead of the beer. Carlo, observing this, draws a small diagram of the cooler-glass angles and concludes — in a panel where he is bent over a notebook with his tongue between his teeth — that the man is, in some sense, the cooler.

The issue's final panel: Carlo looking directly at the reader, deadpan: *"I will not be drinking domestic this week."*

**ISSUE #4 — "THE BLACK PICKUP"**

Carlo on his porch at night. A black pickup truck with Louisiana plates drives slowly down his street. The pickup stops at Carlo's neighbor's driveway. The pickup driver hands Carlo's neighbor an envelope. The pickup leaves. Carlo's neighbor opens the envelope. The envelope is empty.

Carlo's neighbor turns to Carlo. Carlo's neighbor says, panel-to-panel: *"It's the third time this month. I keep waiting for the envelope to have something in it. Then I remember the envelope is the thing."*

**ISSUE #7 — "THE NINE-DAY MAN"**

The recurring back-cooler customer from Issue #1 returns. Carlo, who has been keeping a small notebook of his arrivals, identifies a nine-day cycle. Carlo, on the ninth night, lies in wait. He follows the man on his bicycle. The man drives to a small bungalow on the eastern edge of Harley Creek Meadows. The man enters the bungalow. The bungalow's lights do not come on. The bungalow has, on close inspection in Carlo's drawing, no electrical service.

Carlo waits across the street for two hours. The man does not come out. The lights do not come on. Carlo goes home. The next morning, the bungalow is gone. The lot is empty. The grass has not been disturbed.

The issue's final panel: Carlo at his kitchen table, eating cereal, holding the notebook, drawing a small grid: *"nine days. nine days. nine days. I am going to need a longer notebook."*

**ISSUE #11 — "THE ADDRESS"** *(most recent; in Rick's inventory; Diego had begun #12 but had not finished)*

Carlo discovers that the form he filled out for the Harley Creek Meadows community-resident registration two years earlier had, at one of its fields, asked for his home address. He had written down a different address than his actual address. He had done this on purpose because he had been told to. The person who had told him to was his grandmother, who had said only: *"mijo, if they want to find you, let them find a house that is not yours."*

The issue narrates Carlo's slow realization that everybody in Harley Creek Meadows who has put down a wrong address on the registration form is, in some sense, the same person. The wrong addresses converge, on the map, into a single small lot on the eastern edge of the development that does not, on any map, exist. The lot is the lot the bungalow stood on in Issue #7.

Carlo goes to the lot.

The issue ends, four pages early, mid-panel, with Carlo standing at the empty lot, holding his bicycle, in the rain.

The next issue's box at the bottom of the back cover reads: **#12 — "THE BUNGALOW THAT WAS THERE" — coming summer.**

Issue #12 was never finished. Diego had begun the pencil layouts on his bedroom floor on the Friday before he was taken. The layouts are still on the floor.

## III. THE PROTEST ZINE — *"WHAT THE COMMUNITY VOICE DID NOT PRINT"*

A four-issue zine series Diego co-edits with **CARLA VEGA** of Lot 47 — the woman whose noise complaint was silenced in S01E15 (THE POMEGRANATE HOUR · THE DEVIL). Carla has been making the zine since the HOA voted to silence her complaint. Diego joined as co-editor at issue #2 because he could do the layout on Rick's photocopier after Cosmic Comics closed at 7 PM on Tuesday nights.

The zine is half-letter, twelve pages, stapled, photocopied at a higher contrast than Sam's *Gas Station Observations* (Carla likes the look). Distribution is approximately fifty copies per issue. They are placed, by Carla on her morning walks, in:

- the wire rack at the entrance of the Harmony Creek HOA clubhouse (where they last, on average, six hours before MR. SAGE the sergeant-at-arms removes them)
- the manila envelope on the back of the Kwik Stop's slushie machine (Sam permits this)
- the racks at Cosmic Comics, behind the back-issue boxes
- a small wooden box at the foot of the cypress tree on the median of Linden Boulevard (Carla calls this the "tree mailbox")
- the lobby of the public library in Houma, alongside the local-author display

### contents — issue #4 (most recent; Diego's last layout)

**Page 1 — masthead.**

> *what the community voice did not print · a zine, issue #4 · summer · edited by C. Vega (lot 47) and D. Montoya (lot withheld) · printed at our own expense · distributed by hand · no advertising · no sponsors · no editorial board · we are the editorial board.*

**Page 2 — "the meeting i was at."**

Carla's account of the HOA meeting at which her noise complaint was silenced. The account is precise, dispassionate, lists the present board members by name, quotes the motion's exact language, and includes a single sentence at the bottom in bolder type: *"the matter was silenced. the sound continues. i have, since the meeting, recorded the sound on a small handheld device. the recording is included as audio insert with this issue (cassette in the manila envelope behind the slushie machine)."*

**Pages 3-4 — "the corridor of yellow lots."**

A photographic spread of the corkboard map from the HOA meeting room. Diego — using a phone camera, on a night Carla had arranged with a sympathetic janitor to access the room — photographed the map at the resolution required to read the highlighted yellow lots. The photographs are presented as a two-page foldout. The yellow corridor is visible. The yellow corridor terminates at the eastern fence at Halsey's wildflower meadow.

The caption, in Diego's hand: *"this is the delivery route the HOA does not say it is. each yellow lot has, by separate inquiry, been visited at least once by a NexCorp Residential Solutions vehicle bearing a payment to the resident. the payments are off the books. we have copies of three of the residents' canceled checks. they are in the supplementary materials, which we are not printing in the zine, which are with Carla, who will distribute them by hand, by request, to journalists who can substantiate the story without naming her as the source."*

**Page 5 — "the noise itself."**

Carla's transcription of the noise. Carla has been listening to her own recording at varying speeds and writing down what she hears.

> *the noise is, at normal playback, a soft mechanical hum at approximately 47 Hz, modulated by a slower 0.3 Hz envelope. at half speed, the hum carries, beneath the primary tone, a structured sub-bass that resembles human voices speaking at very low pitch. at quarter speed, the voices are intelligible. they are speaking in english. they are reciting addresses. all of the addresses are in the yellow corridor.*
>
> *i have not been able to identify the source of the recording. it is not coming from any of the houses on the corridor. it is not coming from any of the NexCorp service buildings on the corridor. it is, on best triangulation, coming from beneath the lots. the addresses being recited are being recited by the lots themselves.*
>
> *i have decided not to be alarmed by this. i have decided to be careful instead. carefulness is what is permitted.*

**Pages 6-8 — "the four-page conversation."**

A four-page transcribed conversation between Carla and **F.T.**, an anonymous correspondent who has been writing Carla letters about the yellow corridor for nine months. F.T.'s letters arrive without postage, slid under her door. The signatures match: F.T. is the same F.T. who messages Maya Daigle in vol6 ch1.

The conversation is presented as Q&A. F.T.'s responses are in a slightly different typeface. The conversation covers:

- F.T.'s claim that the corridor is part of an older incident NexCorp has been quietly building over
- F.T.'s confirmation that the resident-payments are partial compensation for "ground-floor disturbance that the residents have not been told the cause of"
- F.T.'s suggestion that the resident at lot 47 — Carla — is the only resident on the corridor who has refused the payment
- F.T.'s small personal note at the end: *"carla. you are doing the chapter's work. i am not, at present, able to come to harmony creek. i have other rooms i am holding. when you need me — and you will, in roughly four months — you may write to me via the address i have already given you. — f.t."*

**Page 9 — "what we are asking the community to do."**

A short bulleted list, by Carla:

- *if your address is on the yellow corridor (we have published the corridor on pages 3-4), please consider whether the recent NexCorp resident-improvement check you may have received is, in fact, compensation for something you have not been informed of.*
- *if you have not received a check, but you live on the corridor, please write to us (the box at the cypress on linden). we would like to know.*
- *if you have heard the noise, please make a recording. any device. any time. send the recording to us at the box.*
- *if you have not heard the noise but you live on the corridor and your dog has been behaving oddly between 1 and 4 AM, please write to us.*
- *if you are NexCorp Residential Solutions, please address the noise. we are not going away.*

**Pages 10-11 — "a comic."**

Diego's contribution. A two-page comic strip — *PHASE III* style, but signed at the bottom-right as D.M. — depicting Carlo at his kitchen table reading the zine. The strip's punchline: Carlo's grandmother, looking over his shoulder, says: *"mijo, that's good. you should add a recipe page."*

**Page 12 — back cover.**

A blank box with a hand-drawn frame and the words: **"if you would like to write a letter to the editors, please leave it in the manila envelope behind the slushie machine at the kwik stop. we read everything. — c.v. and d.m."**

## IV. DETRITUS — *the practice space*

The back room of Cosmic Comics, after-hours, where the Gallatin Band practices Tuesday and Thursday nights. Diego has accumulated, in a single small Rubbermaid tote pushed under the band's storage shelf, a tote of small objects he has not taken home.

## V. THE TRUCK — *when it is found*

Diego's 2008 Ford F-150, dark grey, plate **DGR-1188**, will be found in the abandoned parking lot of the Skyway Drive-In Theater, twelve miles north of Houma off Highway 90, on the Sunday morning following his disappearance. The driver's window is broken from the outside. The cab is otherwise undisturbed. The fuel tank is half full. The keys are in the ignition.

When Sam is permitted to retrieve her personal items from the cab — after the police have processed the vehicle and finished, with characteristic efficiency, dismissing the case as a runaway — she will find:

- the mixtape in the glove compartment (item I in this accumulation)
- a small handwritten note clipped to the visor, in Diego's hand: *"sammy — if you are reading this in my truck — i love you — i will see you when this is over — please drive the truck home — graciela says you can keep it for the duration — d."*
- a single drumstick on the passenger-side floor mat — the right one — apparently set there by Diego before he was taken
- a small zippered pouch under the driver's seat containing four printed Polaroid photographs of the eastern edge of the Harmony Creek Phase III construction site, taken by Diego with permission from the security guard who works the gate at night and who is, by separate cross-reference with the meta-game's medium-network, also one of the hundred and forty receivers
- a printed page from the Boutin Parish Foundation's web page (which the foundation does not, in fact, advertise; Diego found the page through a method he has not documented), showing the foundation's address in Houma and a single phrase at the bottom of the page: *"for emergency contact, leave a note at the gift shop at LE ROYAUME DU BAYOU, Highway 90 west of Houma. the gift shop is open by appointment only. — the foundation"*

The Polaroids will, on examination by Sam and Rick together at Rick's back-room counter, prove to depict the building on the east side of the Harmony Creek Phase III site that does not appear on any official site plan. The building is, by F.T.'s message to Maya on the Sunday afternoon of vol6 ch1, where Diego is being held. The building is, on careful inspection of Diego's photographs, the bungalow from PHASE III issue #7. The bungalow has, by some logic the meta-game permits but does not explain, become a building. The building is where Diego is.
